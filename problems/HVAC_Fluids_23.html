HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 23
Problem Statement
An energy storage facility uses pumped hydro to capitalize on the low cost of off-peak electricity of $0.02 per kWh. 400 gpm of 60°F water is pumped from a low reservoir to a high reservoir at 1000 ft higher elevation through an equivalent length of 3000 ft of 5-in schedule 40 steel pipe using a pump driven by an AC motor. The overall efficiency of the pump set is 75%. Assuming the off-peak operation lasts for 8 hrs per night, what is the cost of electricity for one night?
Answer Choices
A $2
B $13
C $17 ✓
D $52
✓ Correct Answer: C — $17
Quick Overview
  1. Identify the two head components the pump must overcome: elevation head (1000 ft, given directly) and friction head loss through 3000 ft of 5-in schedule 40 steel pipe.
  2. Look up pipe properties for 5-inch schedule 40 steel pipe (ID ≈ 5.047 in) and convert 400 gpm to ft³/s to find the velocity in the pipe.
  3. Calculate friction head loss using Darcy-Weisbach or pipe friction tables — if using tables, scale the per-100-ft value by the full pipe length ratio (3000/100 = 30).
  4. Sum elevation and friction head to get total pump head Htotal.
  5. Calculate input power to the pump set using flow rate, specific weight of water, total head, and the 75% overall efficiency.
  6. Multiply power (kW) by 8 hours to get total energy (kWh), then multiply by $0.02/kWh to find the overnight electricity cost.
2 · Full Solution

Given: Q = 400 gpm, T = 60°F water, elevation Δz = 1000 ft, equivalent pipe length L = 3000 ft, pipe = 5-in schedule 40 steel, overall efficiency η = 0.75, electricity rate = $0.02/kWh, operation time = 8 hr.

Step 1 — Convert flow rate to ft³/s.
Q = 400 gpm ÷ 448.83 gpm/(ft³/s) = 0.891 ft³/s

Step 2 — Look up pipe dimensions for 5-in Schedule 40.
From pipe tables: ID = 5.047 in = 0.4206 ft
Flow area: A = π/4 × (0.4206)² = 0.1388 ft²

Step 3 — Calculate velocity in the pipe.
V = Q / A = 0.891 / 0.1388 = 6.42 ft/s
Velocity head: V²/2g = (6.42)² / (2 × 32.2) = 41.22 / 64.4 = 0.640 ft

Step 4 — Determine Reynolds number and friction factor.
For 60°F water: ν ≈ 1.217 × 10⁻⁵ ft²/s
Re = V × D / ν = 6.42 × 0.4206 / (1.217 × 10⁻⁵) ≈ 222,000 (turbulent)
For commercial steel pipe: ε = 0.00015 ft → ε/D = 0.000357
From Moody chart: f ≈ 0.018

Step 5 — Calculate friction head loss (Darcy-Weisbach).
hf = f × (L/D) × V²/2g
hf = 0.018 × (3000 / 0.4206) × 0.640
hf = 0.018 × 7133 × 0.640
hf ≈ 82 ft

Alternatively, using friction head loss tables for 5-in Sch 40 steel at 400 gpm: look up the value per 100 ft, then multiply by 3000/100 = 30 to scale to the full pipe length. Always apply this length ratio — the table value alone is not the answer.

Step 6 — Total pump head.
Htotal = elevation head + friction head
Htotal = 1000 ft + 82 ft = 1082 ft

Step 7 — Calculate input power to the pump set.
For water at 60°F: γ ≈ 62.4 lb/ft³

Power (ft·lb/s) = γ × Q × Htotal / η
= 62.4 × 0.891 × 1082 / 0.75
= 60,190 / 0.75
= 80,253 ft·lb/s

Convert to horsepower: 80,253 / 550 = 145.9 hp
Convert to kilowatts: 145.9 × 0.7457 = 108.8 kW

Step 8 — Calculate total energy consumed over 8 hours.
Energy = Power × Time = 108.8 kW × 8 hr = 870 kWh

Step 9 — Calculate electricity cost.
Cost = Energy × Rate = 870 kWh × $0.02/kWh = $17.40

Rounding to the nearest answer choice: Answer C — $17

Note: Even ignoring friction head (using only the 1000 ft elevation head), the cost comes out to approximately $16 — still solidly in the range of answer C. Elevation head dominates this problem, and the friction loss is a secondary contributor. This is a good sanity check: if you get close to $17 by a slightly different path, you're likely on the right track.

3 · Video Synthesis
  • The pumped hydro problem combines elevation head with pipe friction losses — don't reduce it to a simple pressure-drop-only (P₁ − P₂)/γ approach, which ignores the gravity work the pump must do.
  • When reading friction head loss from pipe friction tables, the value is almost always given per 100 feet of pipe. Always multiply by (actual pipe length ÷ 100) to scale to the full equivalent length — for this problem, that multiplier is 3000/100 = 30.
  • "Overall efficiency" of the pump set means the combined pump-and-motor efficiency is already captured in a single number (0.75). Do not apply separate pump efficiency and motor efficiency corrections — that would be double-counting.
  • Converting between gpm, ft³/s, pipe area, and velocity requires careful unit tracking — confirm that your inside diameter is in feet (not inches) before computing the flow area.
  • Off-peak electricity cost problems are fundamentally pump power calculations with a simple economics wrapper at the end. Solve for power first, then convert to energy, then multiply by rate.
  • The answer of $17 is modest by design — industrial-scale pumped hydro at off-peak rates is inexpensive. If your answer is in the hundreds of dollars, check whether you forgot to apply efficiency or used the wrong pipe diameter.
4 · Key Takeaways
  • Table values for friction head loss are given per unit length (typically per 100 ft) — always scale to your actual pipe run by multiplying by the length ratio. Skipping this step is one of the most common mistakes on pipe friction problems.
  • "Overall efficiency" means the combined pump-and-motor efficiency is already accounted for in one number. Apply it once to convert water power to input power — not twice.
  • Pumped hydro problems are really just pump power calculations with an energy cost step at the end. Once you have the input power in kilowatts, the rest is arithmetic: multiply by hours to get kWh, then multiply by the electricity rate.
  • In tall-lift scenarios like this one (1000 ft of elevation), elevation head dominates over friction losses. If your friction head is larger than your elevation head, re-examine your calculation — something is likely off.
5 · Office Hours Q&A
OH 46 · October 19, 2022

Q: I used the (P₁ − P₂)/γ approach to solve this. Why didn't I get the right answer?

A: That approach misses the full picture — you need to account for elevation head AND friction losses together through the energy equation, not just a simple pressure difference. The pump has to overcome both gravity (lifting 1000 ft of water) and pipe friction. The (P₁ − P₂)/γ shortcut only works when both reservoirs are at the same elevation and friction is already captured in the pressure term. Here, you need the full extended Bernoulli equation with a pump head term that includes elevation and friction. (OH 46)

OH 46 · October 19, 2022

Q: I looked up 5.2 ft from the steel pipe friction table but still got the wrong answer. What went wrong?

A: The table gives friction head loss per 100 feet of pipe. You need to multiply by the ratio of your equivalent pipe length to 100 feet: 3000 ÷ 100 = 30. So the correct friction head contribution is 5.2 × 30 = 156 ft, not just 5.2 ft. Dan emphasizes: always think about what you're pulling from tables and apply the proper length scaling. Forgetting this 30× multiplier and using the raw table value is one of the most common mistakes he sees on pipe friction problems. (OH 46)
