An energy storage facility uses pump hydro to capitalize on the low cost of off-peak electricity of 2 cents per KWH. 400GPM of 60 degree water is pumped from a low reservoir to a high reservoir at a thousand feet higher elevation through an equivalent length of 3000 feet of 5 inch schedule 40 steel pipe using a pump driven by an AC motor. The overall efficiency of the pump set is 75%. Assuming the off-peak operation lasts for 8 hours per night, what is the cost of electricity for one night? So they have a scheme in this facility going on where they're pumping the water to a higher elevation at night on the cheap right? They're doing that for 2 cents per KWH. And then they can presumably sell that power back during the on-peak hours, the other 16 hours, and make a profit on that. This problem is just asking us to calculate the cost of the nighttime part of that operation, the part where they pump the water up to the higher elevation for 8 hours. And they just want the cost for one night. Now a question that comes up a lot when we work on these pumping problems is how to deal with the efficiencies of the pump and the motor. And when you have to include one versus the other versus both. So let's draw our usual picture here. We have a motor which is consuming electricity. So that's electrical power. And that's usually in KW. That's what enters the windings of an AC motor. What comes out of the motor is mechanical power of a rotating shaft that's break horsepower or BHP. That process happens with some efficiency. There are losses we call that the efficiency of the motor. That spinning shaft turns the pump and the pump produces hydraulic horsepower or WHP water horsepower. And that process happens with some efficiency called that the efficiency of the pump. Now if we abstract away the middle part of that the BHP and say well it's a pump set and it's one pair of objects with our couple together and they're stationed next to each other. You basically have electrical power KW in and hydraulic horsepower out. And that total process regardless of the inner workings of it has some overall efficiency, which is the product of the efficiency of the motor and the pump. And we can call that the total efficiency or the overall efficiency. So I'll call it overall and that's the product of the efficiency of the motor and the efficiency of the pump. And in this case that overall efficiency is 0.75. So that's what we're going to use. We don't have to get involved in the intermediate steps. And if we're going to find the cost we basically need three things. We need to know the amount of electrical power that the pump set consumes in KW. We need to know the amount of time which we do eight hours. And we need to know the electricity rate which we know is two cents per KWH. So really the main work of this problem is to find the electrical power that's consumed by the pump set to KW. So the way we can do that is by sizing the pump for the hydraulic horsepower that we need and then applying that total efficiency. So this is what I'll propose.
