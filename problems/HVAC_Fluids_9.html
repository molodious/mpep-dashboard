Problem 9 — Pressure Drop Through a Chilled Water Coil at Reduced Flow

The pressure drop through a chilled water coil at the design flow rate of 25 gpm is 8 psi. During normal operation, the actual flow rate is 4 gpm. What is the pressure drop in normal operation?
Answer Choices
A. 0.2 psi
B. 1.3 psi
C. 4.1 psi
D. 3600 rpm
✓ Answer: A — 0.2 psi

Using the pressure drop square law, pressure drop scales with the square of flow rate ratio. At 4 gpm vs. the design 25 gpm, the ratio is (4/25)² = 0.0256, giving ΔP₂ = 8 × 0.0256 ≈ 0.2 psi. See the Full Solution panel for the complete worked solution.

Key Relationship — Pressure Drop Square Law
Quantity Design Actual
Flow rate (Q) 25 gpm 4 gpm
Pressure drop (ΔP) 8 psi ?
Flow ratio Q₂/Q₁ = 4/25 = 0.16
ΔP ratio (Q₂/Q₁)² = 0.0256
ΔP₂ 8 × 0.0256 ≈ 0.2 psi

Pressure drop through piping and coils is proportional to the square of flow rate (turbulent flow regime). Halving the flow cuts ΔP to one-quarter.

1 · Quick Overview

High-level steps to find the pressure drop at a reduced flow rate:

  1. Identify the governing relationship. Pressure drop through piping and coils in turbulent flow follows the square law: ΔP is proportional to Q². This comes directly from the Darcy-Weisbach equation combined with the Moody friction factor.
  2. Set up the ratio. Write ΔP₂/ΔP₁ = (Q₂/Q₁)². You know ΔP₁ = 8 psi, Q₁ = 25 gpm, and Q₂ = 4 gpm.
  3. Calculate the flow ratio squared. (Q₂/Q₁) = 4/25 = 0.16, so (Q₂/Q₁)² = 0.0256.
  4. Solve for ΔP₂. Multiply: ΔP₂ = 8 × 0.0256 = 0.2048 psi.
  5. Select the closest answer: A (0.2 psi).

💡 Key insight: The flow rate dropped from 25 gpm to 4 gpm — a reduction to only 16% of design. Because ΔP scales with Q², the pressure drop falls to only (0.16)² = 2.56% of design. This steep reduction is why chilled water systems operate at much lower pressure drops during part-load conditions.

2 · Full Solution

Pressure drop through a piping system or coil in turbulent flow follows the square law: ΔP ∝ Q². This relationship is derived from the Darcy-Weisbach equation (ΔP = f × L/D × ρV²/2), where velocity V is proportional to flow rate Q. Since ΔP ∝ V² and V ∝ Q, we get ΔP ∝ Q².

Step 1 — Write the Square Law Ratio

For a fixed piping or coil geometry, the ratio of pressure drops at two different flow rates is:

ΔP₂ / ΔP₁ = (Q₂ / Q₁)² Where: ΔP₁ = design pressure drop = 8 psi Q₁ = design flow rate = 25 gpm Q₂ = actual flow rate = 4 gpm ΔP₂ = actual pressure drop = ? (what we want)

Step 2 — Calculate the Flow Rate Ratio

Q₂ / Q₁ = 4 gpm / 25 gpm = 0.16 (Q₂ / Q₁)² = (0.16)² = 0.0256

Step 3 — Solve for ΔP₂

ΔP₂ = ΔP₁ × (Q₂ / Q₁)² ΔP₂ = 8 psi × 0.0256 ΔP₂ = 0.2048 psi ΔP₂ ≈ 0.2 psi → Answer A

Result

The pressure drop during normal operation (at 4 gpm) is approximately 0.2 psi → Answer A.

Why the Other Choices Are Wrong

  • B (1.3 psi): This would result from applying a linear (rather than squared) relationship — e.g., ΔP₂ = 8 × (4/25) = 1.28 psi. This is the most common mistake: forgetting that ΔP scales with Q², not Q.
  • C (4.1 psi): This might come from inverting the ratio (using Q₁/Q₂ instead of Q₂/Q₁), or applying an incorrect exponent. For example, ΔP₂ = 8 × (25/4)² would give 312.5 psi — but if someone used √(25/4) ≈ 2.5 and divided, they could land near a wrong neighborhood. In any case, it's a calculation error.
  • D (3600 rpm): This is the answer to a different problem (nominal speed of a 4-pole synchronous motor, f = 60 Hz, n = 120f/p = 120×60/4 = 3600 rpm). It has nothing to do with pressure drop in a chilled water coil.

💡 Exam Tip: Whenever flow rate changes in a fixed piping system or coil, always apply the square law for pressure drop. If the problem involves pumps specifically (head vs. flow), the pump affinity laws apply — but for resistance (ΔP in the system), it is always ΔP ∝ Q². These two concepts pair together on the PE exam.

3 · Video Synthesis
📹 Key Concepts — Pressure Drop Square Law & Coil Behavior
  • The pressure drop square law is fundamental to HVAC hydronic system analysis. For any fixed resistance (pipe, coil, valve, fitting), ΔP ∝ Q². This means a 50% reduction in flow cuts the pressure drop to 25% of its original value — not 50%.
  • This law derives from the Darcy-Weisbach equation. ΔP = f(L/D)(ρV²/2). Since velocity V = Q/A, we have ΔP ∝ V² ∝ Q². The friction factor f is approximately constant in fully turbulent flow (high Reynolds number), which is typical for chilled water systems at design conditions.
  • The square law connects directly to pump affinity laws. The affinity laws tell us pump head scales with N² (speed squared) or Q² (flow squared). The system curve ΔP ∝ Q² is the system-side counterpart. The operating point is where the pump curve intersects the system curve.
  • Chilled water coils are sized at design conditions (25 gpm here), but operate at part load most of the time. At 4 gpm (16% of design), the coil sees only 2.56% of the design ΔP. This dramatic reduction is why variable flow systems save significant pumping energy during part-load operation.
  • Don't confuse the system square law with a linear relationship. A common error is assuming ΔP scales linearly with Q (like ohm's law for resistors in series). That would give 1.28 psi (answer B) — close enough to look plausible but physically incorrect for turbulent flow.
  • The square law applies to the entire system resistance. For a chilled water coil, the coil's internal water-side pressure drop follows the same law as pipe friction losses. Both scale with Q² in turbulent flow.
4 · Key Takeaways
⭐ Key Takeaways
  • Pressure drop scales with the square of flow rate in turbulent flow. When flow drops significantly below design, the pressure drop drops even more dramatically. This is the square law, and it applies to pipes, coils, fittings, and most HVAC hydronic components.
  • The most common exam mistake is applying a linear relationship instead of the square law. If you multiply the pressure drop by the flow ratio (rather than the flow ratio squared), you get a plausible-looking wrong answer. Always square the ratio when dealing with system resistance.
  • Part-load operation results in dramatically lower pressure drops. A system running at 16% of design flow sees only about 2.6% of design pressure drop. This is critical for understanding why variable flow systems save energy.
  • Know how to tell system resistance problems apart from pump problems. Pump affinity laws govern how pump performance changes with speed or flow. The square law governs how system resistance changes with flow. On the exam, read carefully — the same formula structure appears in both, but they answer different questions.
