Problem 16 — Refrigerator COP (Unit Conversion)
Thermodynamics · Refrigeration Cycle · Coefficient of Performance
A refrigerator draws 800 W of power and absorbs 4,000 Btu/hr from the internal volume. What is the coefficient of performance?
A. 0.7
B. 1.5 ✓
C. 1.7
D. 5.0
Answer: B — 1.5
1. Identify the COP formula for a refrigerator: COP_ref = Q_L / W_net where Q_L is heat absorbed from the refrigerated space and W_net is net work input. 2. Note that Q_L and W_net must be in the same units. The problem gives W in watts and Q_L in Btu/hr, so convert W to Btu/hr: 800 W × 3.412 Btu/hr per W = 2,729.6 Btu/hr 3. Apply the COP formula: COP = 4,000 Btu/hr ÷ 2,729.6 Btu/hr ≈ 1.47 4. Round to the nearest answer choice: 1.47 ≈ 1.5 (Answer B).
— — — Step 1: Identify the governing formula — — — The coefficient of performance (COP) of a refrigerator (or any vapor-compression cooling device) is defined as the ratio of the desired output to the required input. For a refrigerator, the desired output is the heat removed from the low-temperature space (Q_L), and the required input is the net work supplied to the compressor (W_net): COP_ref = Q_L / W_net This definition comes directly from the NCEES PE Reference Handbook (Thermodynamics section) and reflects the first-law energy balance on the refrigeration cycle. COP is dimensionless, but only when Q_L and W_net are expressed in the same units. — — — Step 2: Recognize the unit mismatch — — — The problem provides: • W_net = 800 W (SI unit of power) • Q_L = 4,000 Btu/hr (English unit of power, commonly used in HVAC) These cannot be divided directly. We must convert to a common unit. The standard conversion factor is: 1 W = 3.41214 Btu/hr This conversion appears in the NCEES PE Reference Handbook unit conversion tables. Memorize it — it appears constantly in HVAC problems. — — — Step 3: Convert W_net to Btu/hr — — — W_net = 800 W × 3.412 Btu/hr per W = 2,729.6 Btu/hr Alternatively, you could convert Q_L to watts: Q_L = 4,000 Btu/hr ÷ 3.412 Btu/hr per W = 1,172.3 W Either approach gives the same dimensionless COP. — — — Step 4: Calculate the COP — — — Using Btu/hr throughout: COP = Q_L / W_net = 4,000 / 2,729.6 ≈ 1.466 Using watts throughout: COP = Q_L / W_net = 1,172.3 / 800 ≈ 1.465 Both routes confirm COP ≈ 1.47. The answer choices are 0.7, 1.5, 1.7, and 5.0. The value 1.47 is closest to 1.5 (Answer B). — — — Why the wrong answers are wrong — — — A (0.7): This results from inverting the formula — computing W_net / Q_L instead of Q_L / W_net, using mixed units. Specifically: 800 / (4,000 / 3.412) ≈ 0.68, rounded to 0.7. A classic sign that the ratio was flipped. C (1.7): A plausible distractor. Could result from using a slightly incorrect conversion factor (e.g., 3.6 instead of 3.412), yielding 4,000 / (800 × 3.6) ≈ 1.39, or from rounding intermediate steps differently. Always use the handbook value of 3.412. D (5.0): This is a completely incorrect result. It might arise from dividing 4,000 by 800 directly without any unit conversion (4,000 / 800 = 5.0). This is the most tempting trap — it requires no work, gives a clean number, and is wrong precisely because the units were never reconciled. Never divide quantities in different units. — — — First Law check — — — As a sanity check, consider the energy balance on the entire refrigeration system: Q_H = Q_L + W_net Q_H = 4,000 + 2,729.6 = 6,729.6 Btu/hr The heat rejected to the environment (condenser) is greater than the heat absorbed — as expected, since the compressor work adds energy to the system. This confirms our setup is physically correct.
Concept Synthesis
🎓 Key Concepts for This Problem

COP Defined: Desired Output Over Required Input
The coefficient of performance is the thermodynamic efficiency metric for refrigerators and heat pumps. For a refrigerator, the desired output is the heat removed from the cold space (Q_L), and the required input is the compressor work (W_net). COP = Q_L / W_net. A COP greater than 1.0 is entirely possible and normal — it just means you’re moving more heat than the work you put in, thanks to the refrigeration cycle. This is fundamentally different from a heat engine, where efficiency is always less than 1.

The Critical Unit Conversion: Watts to Btu/hr
The PE exam frequently mixes SI and English units within a single problem, especially in HVAC. The conversion 1 W = 3.412 Btu/hr is one of the most important in this discipline. It appears in COP problems, cooling load calculations, and equipment sizing. Failing to apply it gives a dimensionally inconsistent ratio that is off by a factor of 3.412. In this problem, ignoring units gives 5.0; correct conversion gives 1.47 ≈ 1.5.

Refrigeration COP vs. Heat Pump COP
Both devices run on the same vapor-compression cycle, but they are evaluated differently. A refrigerator’s job is to remove heat from a cold space: COP_ref = Q_L / W_net. A heat pump’s job is to deliver heat to a warm space: COP_HP = Q_H / W_net. Since Q_H = Q_L + W_net, it follows that COP_HP = COP_ref + 1 for the same operating conditions. Reading the question carefully to identify which device is described is essential before choosing a formula.

Energy Balance on the Refrigeration Cycle
A useful check: Q_H = Q_L + W_net. In this problem, Q_H = 4,000 + 2,730 = 6,730 Btu/hr. The condenser rejects more heat than the evaporator absorbs, and the difference is exactly the compressor work. This first-law balance always holds and is a quick way to verify your unit conversions and formula setup are consistent.
Key Takeaways
  • The refrigerator COP formula requires Q_L and W_net in the same units — never divide watts by Btu/hr directly. Use the handbook conversion (1 W = 3.412 Btu/hr) to reconcile units before computing.
  • A COP greater than 1 is physically valid for refrigerators and heat pumps — you are moving heat, not creating it, so the ratio of heat transferred to work input can exceed 1.
  • The trap answer (5.0) comes from dividing the numbers without unit conversion. When a problem mixes SI and English units, always convert before calculating.
  • Remember that COP_HP = COP_ref + 1 for the same cycle — knowing whether the problem asks about a refrigerator or heat pump determines which formula to apply.
