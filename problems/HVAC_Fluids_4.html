HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 4
Problem Statement
30 gpm of 120°F water flows through a nominal 1½ in schedule 40 steel pipe. What is the Reynolds number?
Answer Choices
A.52,000
B.97,000
C.104,000 ✓
D.1,250,000
Solution
Answer: C
Quick Overview: 1. Convert flow rate from GPM to ft³/s using the conversion factor 1 ft³/s = 449 gpm. 2. Look up the inner diameter of a nominal 1½" Schedule 40 steel pipe from reference tables (ID = 1.610 in). 3. Calculate the cross-sectional flow area using A = π/4 × D². 4. Calculate the average flow velocity using V = Q/A. 5. Look up the kinematic viscosity of water at 120°F from fluid property tables (ν ≈ 0.609 × 10⁻⁵ ft²/s). 6. Calculate the Reynolds number using Re = V·D/ν and compare with the answer choices. Full Solution: The Reynolds number is a dimensionless parameter that characterizes the nature of flow in a pipe — whether it is laminar, transitional, or turbulent. It is defined as: Re = V · D / ν where V is the mean flow velocity (ft/s), D is the pipe inner diameter (ft), and ν is the kinematic viscosity of the fluid (ft²/s). Step 1 — Convert flow rate to ft³/s: The problem gives us a volumetric flow rate of 30 gpm. The standard conversion is: 1 ft³/s = 449 gpm Q = 30 gpm ÷ 449 gpm/(ft³/s) = 0.0668 ft³/s Step 2 — Pipe inner diameter: For a nominal 1½" Schedule 40 steel pipe, the reference handbook (Pipe Dimensions table) gives: ID = 1.610 in Convert to feet: D = 1.610 in ÷ 12 in/ft = 0.1342 ft Note: Always use the INNER diameter for pipe flow calculations — the nominal size (1½") is NOT the actual inside diameter. Step 3 — Cross-sectional flow area: A = (π/4) × D² A = (π/4) × (0.1342 ft)² A = 0.7854 × 0.01801 ft² A = 0.01414 ft² Step 4 — Mean flow velocity: V = Q / A V = 0.0668 ft³/s ÷ 0.01414 ft² V = 4.72 ft/s Step 5 — Kinematic viscosity at 120°F: From the water properties table at 120°F: ν ≈ 0.609 × 10⁻⁵ ft²/s Water becomes less viscous as temperature increases, so ν at 120°F is lower than at 60°F (ν ≈ 1.21 × 10⁻⁵ ft²/s at 60°F). Always look up fluid properties at the stated temperature. Step 6 — Reynolds number: Re = V · D / ν Re = (4.72 ft/s × 0.1342 ft) / (0.609 × 10⁻⁵ ft²/s) Re = 0.6334 / (0.609 × 10⁻⁵) Re = 0.6334 / 0.00000609 Re ≈ 104,000 This matches answer choice C: 104,000. At Re ≈ 104,000, the flow is well into the turbulent regime (Re > 4,000 is considered turbulent in pipe flow). This is typical for water flowing at reasonable velocities in building piping systems.
📹 Video Solution Synthesis:

• The Reynolds number (Re = V·D/ν) is the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces in the flow — a high Re means inertia dominates and flow is turbulent; a low Re means viscosity dominates and flow is laminar.

• The GPM-to-ft³/s conversion is a critical step and a common source of error — always remember that 1 ft³/s = 449 gpm (not 7.48 gpm/ft³, which is a volume conversion, not a flow rate conversion).

• Schedule 40 pipe dimensions must be looked up from tables — the nominal size (1½") does not equal the actual inner diameter (1.610 in). Never use nominal size directly in calculations.

• Water properties (especially kinematic viscosity) change significantly with temperature. At 120°F, ν ≈ 0.609 × 10⁻⁵ ft²/s — significantly less than at room temperature. Misusing room-temperature properties here would push the answer toward choice B or D.

• The turbulent-vs-laminar threshold in pipe flow: Re < 2,300 is laminar, Re > 4,000 is turbulent. At Re ≈ 104,000, this flow is fully turbulent — as expected for most water flows in HVAC piping systems.

• Exam strategy: write out Re = V·D/ν, then work backwards from the given data. Converting units carefully at each step prevents the factor-of-2 or factor-of-10 errors that produce the wrong answer choices.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
• Reynolds number problems are fundamentally unit-conversion exercises — master the GPM to ft³/s conversion (divide by 449) and you've won half the battle.

• Always use the INNER diameter for pipe flow calculations — the nominal pipe size is just a label, not the actual inside dimension. Grab the Schedule 40 pipe table from your reference handbook.

• Water properties change significantly with temperature — kinematic viscosity at 120°F is roughly half what it is at 60°F. Always look up the property at the temperature stated in the problem.

• Most pipe flows encountered on the PE exam are turbulent (Re well above 4,000). Recognizing this quickly lets you avoid overthinking the problem and confirms your answer is in the right ballpark.
