HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 21  |  Global #44 of 660
Problem Statement
A viscous liquid with a specific gravity of 1.1 is pumped with a volume flow rate of 100 gpm. The suction pressure measured at the inlet of the pump is 5 psi of vacuum. The discharge pressure measured 3 ft above the pump outlet is 10 psig. The diameter of the piping reduces from 4 in on the suction side to 2 in on the discharge side. The pump has a mechanical efficiency of 80%. What is the input power to the pump?
Answer Choices
A 3/4 hp
B 1 hp
C 1-1/4 hp
D 1-1/2 hp
✓ Correct Answer: C — 1-1/4 hp
1 · Quick Overview
  1. Identify the given information: flow rate (100 gpm), suction pressure (−5 psig / vacuum), discharge pressure (10 psig at 3 ft elevation), pipe diameters (4 in suction, 2 in discharge), specific gravity 1.1, efficiency 80%.
  2. Convert pressures to head in feet of the actual fluid — multiply the psi-to-feet-of-water factor (2.31 ft/psi) by the pressure, then divide by SG (1.1) to get feet of fluid.
  3. Calculate velocities at the suction and discharge using the continuity equation (flow rate ÷ pipe area), then find the velocity head difference in feet of fluid.
  4. Add up all three head components (pressure head difference + elevation change + velocity head difference) to get total differential head ΔH in feet of fluid.
  5. Use the head-based water horsepower formula — WHP = Q · ΔH · SG / 3960 — applying SG exactly once here.
  6. Divide WHP by mechanical efficiency (0.80) to get input power. Round to the nearest answer choice: ~1.24 hp ≈ 1-1/4 hp → Answer C.
2 · Full Solution

Given: Q = 100 gpm, SG = 1.1, P₁ = −5 psig (vacuum), P₂ = 10 psig, z₂ − z₁ = 3 ft (discharge gauge is 3 ft above pump outlet), D₁ = 4 in, D₂ = 2 in, η = 0.80.

Step 1 — Pressure head difference (feet of fluid).
The suction side is a vacuum: 5 psi vacuum = −5 psig.
ΔP = P₂ − P₁ = 10 − (−5) = 15 psi
Convert to feet of water: 15 psi × 2.31 ft/psi = 34.65 ft of water
Convert to feet of fluid: 34.65 ÷ 1.1 = 31.5 ft of fluid

Step 2 — Elevation head (feet of fluid).
The discharge gauge is already 3 ft above the pump outlet, so the elevation difference is directly:
Δz = 3 ft of fluid (no conversion needed — elevation is already in feet of fluid)

Step 3 — Velocity head difference (feet of fluid).
Flow area: A = π/4 × D²
Convert Q = 100 gpm to ft³/s: 100 ÷ 449 = 0.2227 ft³/s
A₁ (4-in pipe): π/4 × (4/12)² = π/4 × 0.1111 = 0.08727 ft²
A₂ (2-in pipe): π/4 × (2/12)² = π/4 × 0.02778 = 0.02182 ft²
V₁ = 0.2227 / 0.08727 = 2.552 ft/s
V₂ = 0.2227 / 0.02182 = 10.21 ft/s
Velocity head difference: (V₂² − V₁²) / (2g) = (10.21² − 2.552²) / (2 × 32.2)
= (104.24 − 6.51) / 64.4 = 97.73 / 64.4 = 1.518 ft of fluid

Step 4 — Total differential head ΔH.
ΔH = 31.5 + 3.0 + 1.518 ≈ 36.02 ft of fluid

Step 5 — Water horsepower (WHP).
Using the head-based formula with SG applied once:
WHP = Q × ΔH × SG / 3960
WHP = 100 × 36.02 × 1.1 / 3960
WHP = 3962.2 / 3960 ≈ 1.0006 hp

Note: With slightly rounded ΔH values, WHP ≈ 1.00 hp (water horsepower).

Step 6 — Input power (accounting for efficiency).
Input Power = WHP / η = 1.00 / 0.80 = 1.25 hp ≈ 1-1/4 hp

Dan's OH 111 calculation yields ~1.24 hp before dividing by efficiency, confirming the answer rounds to C) 1-1/4 hp.

3 · Video Synthesis
  • The most important teaching point is the "SG once" rule — specific gravity must be applied exactly one time, whether in the Bernoulli head terms or in the WHP formula, never in both.
  • Two equivalent approaches exist: (a) convert all head terms to feet of water and use WHP = Q·ΔH/3960 without SG, or (b) keep all head terms in feet of actual fluid and use WHP = Q·ΔH·SG/3960 with SG once at the end.
  • Vacuum pressure is negative gauge pressure: "5 psi vacuum" means P = −5 psig. The total ΔP across the pump becomes 10 − (−5) = 15 psi, which is larger than it might initially appear.
  • The 2.31 ft/psi conversion factor is derived for water (SG = 1.0). Dividing by SG adjusts the feet-of-water result to feet of the actual fluid — a denser fluid (SG > 1) requires a shorter column to produce the same pressure.
  • Even though velocity head is a small contributor relative to pressure head in this problem, it should not be ignored — the pipe area ratio is 4:1 (since the diameter halves), causing the velocity to quadruple on the discharge side.
  • This problem evolved over multiple OH sessions — Dan initially had a sign error, then corrected to answer D, then after further review confirmed C as the final correct answer in OH 111.
4 · Key Takeaways
  • Apply SG exactly once. Double-counting specific gravity (once in head conversion and once in the WHP formula) is the single most common error on this problem type. Decide your approach upfront and stick to it throughout.
  • Vacuum pressure is negative gauge pressure. "5 psi vacuum" = −5 psig. Always flip the sign before doing arithmetic — the total pressure rise across the pump will be larger than it looks.
  • Pipe diameter changes mean velocity changes matter. When the discharge pipe is smaller than the suction pipe, velocity head adds to the total pump head. Don't skip the velocity term just because it seems minor.
  • Efficiency divides, not multiplies. Divide the water horsepower by efficiency to get the (larger) input power. On the exam, if your WHP is close to one answer choice and efficiency is less than 1, the input power must be higher — confirm the final step every time.

OH 83 · Jun 2024

Q: A student questioned why the solution assumed steel pipe dimensions. The problem never specifies the pipe material — doesn't that affect the calculation?

A: Dan agreed the problem statement should have specified the pipe material. For the 4-inch nominal pipe, the difference between nominal and actual inside diameter is small enough that it doesn't significantly change the result. However, for pipes under 2 inches, the discrepancy matters more — since velocity depends on area, which depends on diameter squared, even a small diameter difference can produce a noticeable velocity error. On exam problems, assume nominal diameter equals actual unless told otherwise.

Q: A student asked whether using SG in the Bernoulli equation and again in the WHP formula is really double-counting, or whether each use serves a distinct purpose that justifies both.

A: Dan explained that using SG inside Bernoulli converts head to feet of the viscous liquid (not water), and using SG in the WHP formula corrects for the fact that the constant 3960 was derived for water. Both uses are legitimate in isolation — but you can only do one. If your Bernoulli terms are already in feet of fluid, you need SG in the WHP formula. If your Bernoulli terms are in feet of water, you don't. Dan also clarified vacuum pressure: 5 psi vacuum = −5 psig = approximately 9.7 psia. Vacuum pressure is just negative gauge pressure.

OH 16 · Jun 2021

Q: A student asked Dan to explain why there are two different WHP formulas — one using pressure in psi (Q·ΔP/1714) and one using head in feet (Q·ΔH/3960) — and when SG appears in each.

A: Dan explained that the two formulas are mathematically equivalent — the factor 2.31 (ft of water per psi) is the only difference between the denominators (3960 = 1714 × 2.31). SG appears in the head version because a denser fluid produces the same pressure from a shorter column height; the formula needs SG to reconcile the fluid's actual head with the water-based constant. His recommendation: pick one approach at the start (all in psi, or all in feet) and stay consistent throughout the entire problem.
