Problem Statement
A steam boiler uses 50lb of 40psia saturated steam to heat 10gpm hr of water. What is the expected increase in temperature for the water?

B.8◦F
C.9◦F
D.10◦F

Full Solution: A steam boiler uses 50 lbs of 40 psi a saturated steam to heat 10 gpm of water. What is the expected increase in temperature for the water? All the interesting thing about this problem is that we don't know enough about the leaving conditions of the steam. So we know it comes in at 14 psi a saturated but we don't know what it leaves as. We have to make an assumption. And I think the reasonable assumption to make here for a steam boiler is that it's going to completely condense. It's going to give up all of its latent heat and then leave as a saturated liquid. Now we don't know that with 100% confidence but to assume anything else in between, and we don't have any reason to say that the quality is going to be 30% or 20% or 80% so we might as well assume that it condenses completely, which would make sense because you know, a waste product of the steam boiler would be the condensate that has been used up because it's now a saturated liquid. So for the steam side we can quantify the amount of heat contributed by that steam. Normally we say m.delt to h and that's just fine we can do that here but what is delta h? Well if the steam comes in a saturated steam and leaves a saturated liquid that's the latent heat of vaporization that's hg minus hf which is hfg so we can look that up directly in the steam tables. So we'll just call that m.hfg. And we know the mass flow rate and we can look up hfg at 40 psi a. So go into the steam table properties of saturated water and steam organized by pressure 40 psi a and find that value for hfg that turns out to be 933.68 and let's go straight to substitution on this one mass flow rate is 50 pounds per hour times hfg is 933.68 and that's b to you per pound. So that's the change in enthalpy as that steam condenses and gives up its entire latent heat of vaporization. That cancels out pounds we end up with b to per hour that's 46,648. And now all of that is added to the water it's 10 gpm of water we know the volume flow rate we want to know the expected increase in temperature so we can just use the rule of thumb for sensible heating of water which as long as we have b to use per hour going in and we're using gpm for the volume flow rate of the water then we should expect to get a delta t out in degrees Fahrenheit so we don't even have to write units as long as we honor those units going in it's going to be q dot equals 500 gpm delta t and we'll solve for delta t q dot equals 500 over the gpm where q dot is the 46,500 and the gpm is 10 so the delta t should be 9.3 degrees Fahrenheit which is closest to answer choice c.
