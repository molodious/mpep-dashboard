The flow recombines at a T after flowing through the two branches. What is the velocity in branch A? So we have some water flowing and then it hits a T and it goes through two branches. Maybe we'll say it's got a little bit of an extra run that it has to do and then that hits a T and recombines. So this is our flow. We've got branch A which is short it's only 80 feet in length and branch B which is this longer path that the water may take. And the question is what's the velocity in branch A? So one of the fundamental principles about parallel piping is that the friction loss is going to be the same between these two branches and even if there were more branches of 30 or 405 branches in parallel, the friction loss is equal through all of them. So that's the key idea here and what that means is when it's set them equal. So we'll say the pressure loss in A is equal to the losses in B. And now we can expand on that by writing the Darcy equation for both sides. So we're going to write FL v squared and I'll write an A subscript on the left over 2 dg equals FL b v squared over 2 dg. And the reason I didn't bother to write A and B subscripts on some of these other terms is that they're going to cancel out and I'll explain why. So two is a constant and G is a constant. So we know those are going to cancel out. The diameter is two inch standard weight steel on both sides. So that's not changing anything. The friction factor is going to vary a little bit because the velocity is going to vary a little bit. But it's 80 feet in 120 feet. So these are not dramatically different lengths. If it was 80 feet in 8,000 feet, then the friction factor would probably be different in the two runs. But they're pretty close and I think it's safe to assume that they're going to be in the same ballpark. So if we get rid of the friction factor, then we can say more simply LA times v a squared equals l b times v b squared. And now let's take that and arrange it so that we have an expression just for the ratio of the velocities. I think that's going to be interesting because we're going after the velocity. So it'll be useful to get that together. So I'll divide by the velocity of A squared in both sides and then I'll divide by the length of b. So we could say LA over l b equals v b over v a quantity squared. And then of course we could take the square root of that and we could say v b over v a equals the square root of LA over l b. And we know LA and l b, those are the lengths. Those are 80 and 120. So if we do 80 feet over 120 feet, that's one and a half and then take the square root of that. That's going to equal 0.816. That's the ratio of the velocity. So whatever velocity A is, velocity b is 81.6% of that. And since the pipes have the same diameter, we can even say that's true about the volume flow rates, right? Because q A equals velocity A times area A and have the same area. So I'll just write big A and q B equals velocity b times area. So if we're going to write an expression v b over v a, v b is q b over A. So v b over v a is q b over A divided by q A over A. And these are grouped as such. So the area will cancel and the ratio of the velocities of b to A is the same as the ratio of the volume flow rates of b to A, which makes sense because they're linearly related. q b over q A also equals 0.816. And now that's extremely useful because we know there's 40 gpm of water being supplied. So if you've got this 40 gpm, that's entering those two branches. Some of it's going to take path A. Some of it's going to take path B. And then it's being recombined. Then the sum of the volume flow rate through A and B has to be 40 gpm.
