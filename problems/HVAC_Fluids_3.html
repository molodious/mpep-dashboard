Problem
HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 3
900 gpm of water flows through an 8-inch pipe with a relative roughness of 0.0004 and a Reynolds number of 400,000. What is the pressure loss per hundred feet of pipe?
Answer Choices
A 0.1 ft
B 0.2 ft
C 1.2 ft
D 1.4 ft  ✓
Answer: D — 1.4 ft
Quick Overview
  1. Convert 900 GPM to velocity using the pipe cross-sectional area (8" nominal schedule 40 steel, ID ≈ 7.981").
  2. Use the Moody chart with Re = 400,000 and ε/D = 0.0004 to read friction factor f ≈ 0.0175 (precise value 0.0172).
  3. Apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation: hL = f × (L/D) × (V²/2g) for L = 100 ft.
  4. Result: hL ≈ 1.36 ft → rounds to 1.4 ft → Answer D.
📝 Key formula: Darcy-Weisbach: hL = f × (L/D) × (V²/2g). The Moody chart gives friction factor as a function of two inputs: Reynolds number (x-axis, log scale) and relative roughness ε/D (curves on right). Read the friction factor from the left vertical axis (also log scale). Watch out — the horizontal axis is logarithmic, so 4×105 sits between the 105 and 106 gridlines, much closer to 106.

Full Solution

This is a classic pipe friction problem using the Darcy-Weisbach equation. We are given the Reynolds number and relative roughness directly, so the main task is reading the Moody chart accurately and then computing the head loss.

Given:

Flow rate Q = 900 gpm Pipe nominal diameter = 8 in (schedule 40 steel, ID = 7.981 in) Relative roughness ε/D = 0.0004 Reynolds number Re = 400,000 = 4×10⁵ Pipe length L = 100 ft

Step 1 — Convert flow rate to velocity:

Convert GPM to ft³/s using the conversion 1 ft³ = 7.48 gal and 1 min = 60 s:

Q = 900 / (7.48 × 60) = 900 / 448.8 = 2.005 ft³/s

Calculate the pipe cross-sectional area using the inside diameter 7.981 in = 7.981/12 = 0.6651 ft:

A = π/4 × D² = π/4 × (0.6651)² = 0.3474 ft²

Solve for velocity:

V = Q / A = 2.005 / 0.3474 = 5.77 ft/s

Step 2 — Read the Moody chart for friction factor:

Locate Re = 4×10⁵ on the horizontal (logarithmic) axis — this lies between 10⁵ and 10⁶, closer to 10⁶. Find the ε/D = 0.0004 curve on the chart (labeled on the right axis). Follow that curve to where it crosses Re = 4×10⁵, then read horizontally LEFT to the left vertical axis to get the friction factor:

f ≈ 0.0175 (Moody chart reading; precise Colebrook value: 0.0172)

Step 3 — Apply Darcy-Weisbach equation:

Use L/D = 100 ft / 0.6651 ft = 150.36 (dimensionless) and g = 32.2 ft/s²:

h_L = f × (L/D) × (V² / 2g) h_L = 0.0175 × 150.36 × (5.77² / (2 × 32.2)) h_L = 0.0175 × 150.36 × (33.29 / 64.4) h_L = 0.0175 × 150.36 × 0.5170 h_L = 0.0175 × 77.74 h_L = 1.360 ft

Answer: D — 1.4 ft. The computed value of ~1.36 ft rounds to 1.4 ft. Note that using the exact friction factor of 0.0172 gives hL = 1.34 ft — still closer to 1.4 than to 1.2, confirming Answer D.

Also note: using the literal 8" diameter instead of the nominal 7.981" ID gives approximately 1.34 ft — both values round to 1.4 ft, so the pipe table lookup does not change the answer for this pipe size.

📺 Video Synthesis
  • The Moody chart horizontal axis is logarithmic. Re = 4×10⁵ sits between the 10⁵ and 10⁶ gridlines — but NOT halfway. On a log scale, 4×10⁵ is about 60% of the way from 10⁵ to 10⁶ (log₁₀(4) ≈ 0.60). Misreading this by a decade is the most common error on this problem.
  • Follow the curves, not horizontal lines. The friction factor curves on a Moody chart are NOT horizontal in the turbulent zone — they slope downward as Re increases. You must follow the ε/D = 0.0004 curve to the correct Re, then read left. Going straight horizontally from the right axis gives the wrong value.
  • The relative roughness ε/D = 0.0004 is given directly in the problem. This means we don't need the pipe material to find friction factor — the roughness is already normalized by the diameter. Steel pipe is still needed to look up the actual inside diameter.
  • Nominal vs. actual diameter: For 8" schedule 40 steel, ID ≈ 7.981" vs. 8.000" nominal. For larger pipes (6"+), this difference is small enough that it won't change which answer choice you pick — verified by Dan in multiple office hours sessions.
  • The Darcy-Weisbach equation requires consistent units: diameter in feet (not inches), velocity in ft/s, g = 32.2 ft/s². Convert all values before substituting.
  • The two most common wrong answers are C (1.2 ft) — from using f = 0.016 due to a Moody chart misread — and forgetting to divide the diameter by 12 when computing L/D.
⚡ Key Takeaways
  • When reading the Moody chart, always follow the relative roughness curve — do not draw a horizontal line from the right axis. The curves are not horizontal in the turbulent regime.
  • The horizontal axis is logarithmic: 4×10⁵ is between 10⁵ and 10⁶, closer to 10⁶. Placing it at 4×10⁴ is a full-decade error that completely changes the answer.
  • For 8" and larger nominal pipe sizes, the difference between nominal and schedule 40 ID is negligible — both give the same answer choice.
  • When ε/D is given directly, you don't need the pipe material to read the Moody chart. Steel pipe lookup is only needed to find the actual inside diameter for velocity calculations.
  • Friction factor from Moody chart (~0.0175) vs. precise Colebrook formula (0.0172) — slight differences are expected and acceptable. Target the closest answer choice.

Student got friction factor 0.016 and chose answer C (1.2 ft). What went wrong?
Dan explained the key error: when using the Moody chart right vertical axis (relative roughness), you must follow the CURVES — not go horizontally across. The relative roughness curves slope downward as Re increases in the turbulent zone. If you follow the ε/D = 0.0004 curve to Re = 4×10⁵ and then read left, you get approximately 0.0175. Going straight horizontally from the right axis to the left gives about 0.016, which is wrong. Dan also reinforced that the horizontal axis is logarithmic — 1.5×10⁵ is NOT halfway between 1×10⁵ and 2×10⁵. On a log scale it's actually closer to the 2, since equal distances represent equal multiplicative steps, not additive ones.
OH 103B May 26th, 2025
How do you actually use the relative roughness line to find friction factor on the Moody chart?
Dan clarified that friction factor is a function of two inputs: Reynolds number and relative roughness (ε/D). The process is: (1) find Re = 4×10⁵ on the horizontal logarithmic axis, (2) identify the ε/D = 0.0004 curve (one of the lines plotted across the chart, labeled on the right axis), (3) follow that curve to where it intersects your Reynolds number, and (4) read horizontally left to the left vertical axis to get the friction factor — which is also a logarithmic axis. The critical point is that you find the CURVE for your relative roughness and follow it — not a straight horizontal line.
OH 103A May 26th, 2025
Should we assume steel pipe when the material isn't given in the problem?
Dan said the pipe material should be given — if it's missing, that's his oversight, not the student's. Steel is a reasonable default assumption (and the most common material for water systems), but strictly speaking you shouldn't need to assume. He emphasized that different materials give dramatically different results: PVC pipe is much smoother than steel, giving a lower friction factor and significantly less head loss. For this particular problem, the relative roughness is given directly (ε/D = 0.0004), so the material doesn't affect the friction factor lookup — it only matters for the inside diameter, where the difference for 8" nominal pipe is negligible.

OH 66 August 28th, 2023
The friction factor should be 0.0225 for Re = 400,000 and ε/D = 0.004 — why does Dan say 0.0175?
Dan explained the student had made a decade-scale error on the logarithmic axis. They placed Re = 4×10⁵ at the location of 4×10⁴, reading one full decade to the left of the correct position. The key fact: Re = 400,000 must sit BETWEEN 10⁵ (100,000) and 10⁶ (1,000,000) on the chart. The value 4×10⁴ = 40,000 sits between 10⁴ and 10⁵. At 40,000, the friction factor is indeed higher (~0.022–0.025 range), which is what the student found — but 40,000 ≠ 400,000. Dan also noted the relative roughness in this problem is ε/D = 0.0004, not 0.004 — a second potential misread. These two errors compounded to give a completely different answer.
OH 65 August 14th, 2023
Why do we assume steel pipe for this problem?
Dan explained three reasons: (1) Steel pipe is by far the most common material for water systems in buildings, so it's the natural default assumption for HVAC fluids problems. (2) The steel pipe friction/flow tables (like Cameron Hydraulic Data) are used here primarily to look up the inside diameter — since the problem gives ε/D directly, we don't rely on the pipe material for the friction factor itself. (3) For larger pipe sizes like 6" and 8", the difference between nominal diameter and schedule 40 actual inside diameter is very small, so the assumption has minimal impact on the answer. He also clarified that schedule 40 = standard weight for pipes in this size range.
