Full Solution: A 10-horse power pump with 90% efficiency transports water to an elevation of 200 feet above the pump. What volume of water is transported in a one-hour period? So the way we want to think about this problem is we have a pump which is using some power to do some work to increase the elevation of some water, which in so doing increases its potential energy by virtue of it being at a higher elevation. Just like if you do work to a spring, you compress it by applying a force over a distance and then that potential energy is stored in the spring. It's the same here except that spring energy is stored as 1 1,5kx squared and potential energy is going to be stored using a different formula, which can be found in the reference handbook by searching potential energy, but you'll probably remember it just from basic physics. And that's this idea that potential energy equals mgH. And if we're working in imperial units, which we are here, we also need to divide by gc, which is important to remember. And the reason for that is that this mass is going to be pound mass and gc is going to help us change that to pound force and then h is a distance. So by having pound force times a distance, foot pounds is truly a unit of energy or work and that's what we need. If we had pound mass times foot, that wouldn't quite do it. So that's why we need the gc. So we'll hold that thought for the moment and then the other substitution we want to make is for the volume. So what we can do is we can say mass equals density times volume and now we can substitute for mass rho v and that's helpful because ultimately at the end of the problem, we want to find the volume of water that's been transported in a one hour period and right now we don't have volume anywhere in this formula. So what we're saying is the potential energy equals the density times the volume times g times the new height over gc. So what do we know? Well we know the density of water is a constant number that we can use. We don't know the volume, that's what we're looking for. We know the acceleration due to gravity and the gravitational constant and we know the new height that the water is being raised to h. So if we knew the potential energy, we could find the volume. So now let's think about the other side of this equation or what we can equate this with, which is the work that's being done by the pump. Well how do we represent work? We've been given the power and we can say that power is work done over a period of time or another way to say the same thing is that work equals power times time. So for instance if we had kilowatts as the power and we multiply it at times seconds, then you would just get kilojoules as the work. Well those are SI units that's probably not particularly useful here but you get my picture. So now we're talking about the work that's being done by a pump that has this much power, this is the instantaneous power, but it also has an efficiency. So the amount of work done by the pump that 10 horsepower pump is actually not quite what you would expect. It's going to be only 90% of that. So we have to caveat this and say for our particular situation, the work is going to equal the efficiency of the pump times the nominal power 10 horsepower times time. So that's a little extra piece of information that has to get added in. So once we've included that we can set the work equal to the potential energy because however much work the pump actually does that contributes to the final potential energy of the water. So let's equate the two. We'll say efficiency times power times time equals rho vg h over g subsy and we know the power and we know the time period's one hour and we know the efficiency is 90% so really v is the only unknown here. Let's rearrange this and solve for v. v equals efficiency times power times time times g subsy all divided by rho g h.
