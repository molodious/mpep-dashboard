HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 24
Problem Statement
A pump transfers 100 gpm of water from an open reservoir at atmospheric pressure. The static suction lift is 5 ft, the static discharge head is 30 ft, and the working pressure at the outlet is 10 psig. The pump efficiency is 85% and the motor efficiency is 80%. Assuming losses are insignificant, what size motor is required?
Answer Choices
A.1½ hp
B.2 hp
C.3 hp ✓
D.5 hp
Solution
Answer: C
Quick Overview: 1. Identify all head components: static suction lift, static discharge head, and pressure head from the working outlet pressure. 2. Convert working pressure (10 psig) to feet of head using the conversion factor: 1 psi = 2.31 ft of water. 3. Sum all components to find Total Dynamic Head (TDH): static lift + discharge head + pressure head. 4. Calculate Water Horsepower (WHP) using the standard pump formula: WHP = (Q × TDH) / 3960, where Q is in gpm and TDH is in feet. 5. Account for pump efficiency (85%) and motor efficiency (80%) to find the required motor horsepower — divide by each efficiency in sequence. 6. Compare the calculated motor HP to the list of standard motor sizes and select the next size up. Full Solution: This problem uses the extended Bernoulli equation for a pump system. When a pump is involved, the total dynamic head (TDH) represents all the energy the pump must supply, expressed in feet of fluid. Step 1 — Identify head components: • Static suction lift: 5.0 ft • Static discharge head: 30.0 ft • Pressure head from outlet working pressure: 10 psi × 2.31 ft/psi = 23.1 ft Step 2 — Calculate Total Dynamic Head (TDH): TDH = suction lift + discharge head + pressure head TDH = 5.0 + 30.0 + 23.1 TDH = 58.1 ft Step 3 — Calculate Water Horsepower (WHP): WHP = (Q × TDH) / 3960 WHP = (100 gpm × 58.1 ft) / 3960 WHP = 5,810 / 3960 WHP ≈ 1.47 hp (The constant 3960 comes from unit conversions: 1 hp = 33,000 ft·lbf/min, and water weighs 8.34 lbm/gal.) Step 4 — Account for pump efficiency: Brake Horsepower (BHP) = WHP / η_pump BHP = 1.47 / 0.85 BHP ≈ 1.73 hp BHP is the power that must be delivered to the pump shaft. Because the pump is only 85% efficient, the shaft needs more power than the water actually receives. Step 5 — Account for motor efficiency: Motor HP = BHP / η_motor Motor HP = 1.73 / 0.80 Motor HP ≈ 2.16 hp The motor is only 80% efficient at converting electrical energy to shaft power, so the motor must be rated even higher than the BHP. Step 6 — Select standard motor size: Standard motor sizes: ½, ¾, 1, 1½, 2, 3, 5, 7½, 10 hp... Required: 2.16 hp → next standard size up = 3 hp Answer: C — 3 hp motor
📹 Video Solution Synthesis:

• The Bernoulli equation applied to a pump system adds a pump head term (h_A) — that extra term is the entire focus of these pump problems.

• Total dynamic head must account for static suction lift, static discharge head, AND the pressure head equivalent of any gauge pressure at the outlet. Don't forget the pressure conversion (1 psi = 2.31 ft).

• The WHP formula — Q(gpm) × H(ft) / 3960 — gives water horsepower directly in familiar units. This is the starting point before applying efficiency losses.

• Pump efficiency and motor efficiency compound: you divide by pump efficiency first to get BHP, then divide by motor efficiency to get the motor rating. Both inefficiencies stack, so the motor must be larger than you might initially expect.

• Standard motor sizes follow a fixed sequence (½, ¾, 1, 1½, 2, 3, 5, 7½, 10 hp). Always round UP to the next standard size — you can never undersize a motor.

• Avoid the shortcut of just using ΔP = P2 − P1 for pump problems. That approach ignores elevation and velocity terms and leads to an incorrect answer.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
• Pump problems require the extended Bernoulli equation — simple pressure differences miss the pump energy term and will give you the wrong answer.

• Always convert pressure to feet of head (or vice versa) before combining terms — mixing psi and feet without conversion is a common error.

• Motor size must account for both pump AND motor inefficiency before selecting the next standard size up — the two efficiencies multiply together and the required motor HP is always larger than the water HP.

• Getting comfortable with the WHP → BHP → Motor HP chain and the standard motor size list is essential for exam speed on pump problems.

Stick with the full head-based approach: it gives you the right answer and generalizes to all pump problems.

Q: Can I just use pressure differences (P2 − P1)/γ to solve pump problems?
Dan's response: No — that shortcut misses key energy terms. When there's a pump, you need the full Bernoulli equation with the additional h_A term. The entire point of these pump problems is to find that pump head. Going straight for ΔP/γ underestimates the total head and will lead you to the wrong motor size.
