HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 8
Problem Statement
A chiller plant producing a low chilled water supply temperature of 40°F uses a glycol/water mixture of 30% glycol by volume. At 40°F, glycol has a specific heat capacity of 0.56 Btu/(lbm·°F) and a specific gravity of 1.15. What is the specific heat capacity of the mixture?
Answer Choices
A.0.86 Btu/(lbm·°F) ✓
B.0.87 Btu/(lbm·°F)
C.0.88 Btu/(lbm·°F)
D.0.89 Btu/(lbm·°F)
Solution
Answer: A — 0.86 Btu/(lbm·°F)
💡 Quick Overview
  1. Recognize that the problem gives volume percentages, but specific heat of a mixture is a mass-weighted average — not a volume-weighted one. Using volumes directly will yield the wrong answer.
  2. Assume a convenient reference volume of 100 ft³ for the mixture: 70 ft³ water + 30 ft³ glycol. The total volume assumed will cancel out and does not affect the result.
  3. Use specific gravity to find glycol density: ρglycol = SG × ρwater = 1.15 × 62.4 lb/ft³ = 71.76 lb/ft³.
  4. Calculate the mass of each component: mwater = 62.4 × 70 = 4,368 lbm  |  mglycol = 71.76 × 30 = 2,152.8 lbm.
  5. Apply the mass-weighted mixing formula: cp,mix = (cp,glycol × mglycol + cp,water × mwater) ÷ (mglycol + mwater).
  6. Compute the result: cp,mix = (0.56 × 2,152.8 + 1.0 × 4,368) ÷ 6,520.8 = 0.855 Btu/(lbm·°F) → closest to Answer A (0.86).
📄 Full Solution

A chiller plant at 40°F uses a glycol/water mixture with 30% glycol by volume. Given: cp,glycol = 0.56 Btu/(lbm·°F), SGglycol = 1.15, cp,water = 1.0 Btu/(lbm·°F) (known), ρwater = 62.4 lb/ft³ (known). Find the specific heat capacity of the mixture.

The key physical principle: specific heat capacity of a fluid mixture is a mass-weighted average, not a volume-weighted average. Glycol is denser than water (SG > 1), so the 30% glycol by volume actually represents a higher fraction of the total mass than its volume implies. Using volume fractions directly would overestimate the mixture's thermal capacity.

Step 1 — Set Up Reference Volumes

Assume a total mixture volume of 100 ft³ (arbitrary; cancels out):

V_water = 0.70 × 100 ft³ = 70 ft³ V_glycol = 0.30 × 100 ft³ = 30 ft³
Step 2 — Find Glycol Density via Specific Gravity

From the NCEES Reference Handbook (Properties of Substances):

SG = ρ_glycol / ρ_water ρ_glycol = SG × ρ_water = 1.15 × 62.4 lb/ft³ = 71.76 lb/ft³
Step 3 — Find the Mass of Each Component
m_water = ρ_water × V_water = 62.4 lb/ft³ × 70 ft³ = 4,368 lbm m_glycol = ρ_glycol × V_glycol = 71.76 lb/ft³ × 30 ft³ = 2,152.8 lbm
Step 4 — Mass-Weighted Mixing Formula
c_p,mix = (c_p,glycol × m_glycol + c_p,water × m_water) / (m_glycol + m_water) c_p,mix = (0.56 Btu/(lbm·°F) × 2,152.8 lbm + 1.0 Btu/(lbm·°F) × 4,368 lbm) / (2,152.8 lbm + 4,368 lbm) c_p,mix = (1,205.6 + 4,368) / 6,520.8 c_p,mix = 5,573.6 / 6,520.8 = 0.855 Btu/(lbm·°F)

Rounded to the nearest answer choice: 0.86 Btu/(lbm·°F) → Answer A.

Handbook note: The current NCEES reference handbook includes tables for the specific heat capacity of aqueous ethylene glycol and propylene glycol solutions at 30%, 40%, and 50% concentrations. If the problem specifies the glycol type and the concentration matches a tabulated value, reading from the table is a valid shortcut. The analytical mass-weighted approach is always the fallback and remains the foundational method to master.

📹 Video Solution Synthesis
  • The instructor opens by flagging the trap: 30% glycol is given by volume, but specific heat capacity of a mixture must be mixed by mass — doing 0.3 × 0.56 + 0.7 × 1.0 with the raw volume fractions gives a result that is close but wrong.
  • To make the arithmetic tractable, assume a 100 ft³ total volume: 70 ft³ water + 30 ft³ glycol — the assumed volume cancels out, so the choice of 100 is purely for convenience.
  • Glycol density is derived from the given specific gravity: ρglycol = 1.15 × 62.4 = 71.76 lb/ft³, which then gives mglycol = 2,152.8 lbm and mwater = 4,368 lbm.
  • The mass-weighted mixing formula — cp,mix = Σ(cp,i × mi) / Σmi — yields 0.855 Btu/(lbm·°F), closest to answer A (0.86); the choices are tight by design to catch the volume-weighting error.
  • The instructor notes that glycol is denser than water, so it carries proportionally more mass per unit volume — this is precisely why volume-weighting underestimates the mass fraction of glycol and therefore underestimates how much glycol drags down the mixture's thermal capacity.
⭐ Key Takeaways
  • Specific heat of a fluid mixture is always mass-weighted, not volume-weighted — this is a fundamental physical principle, not a handbook formula to look up.
  • When a problem gives volume percentages for a mixture, use density (via specific gravity) to convert to mass fractions before applying any mixing calculation.
  • The answer choices in this problem are deliberately close (0.86–0.89) to penalize the volume-weighting shortcut — precise method matters just as much as precise arithmetic.
  • The modern NCEES handbook includes glycol solution tables at standard concentrations — if the glycol type is specified and the concentration is tabulated, the table is a valid and faster route on exam day.

OH 15 · May 28, 2021
Q: How do you know the specific heat is a mass-weighted average? I couldn't find that in the practice manual — is it pure memorization?
Dan's response: You won't find it in the reference handbook — it's a judgment call rooted in fundamentals. Think about density: density = mass/volume. If density changes slightly with temperature, the volume changes but the mass stays constant. If specific heat were volume-dependent, the thermal performance of the mixture would shift as conditions change, which would be problematic in engineering practice. So it's the mass that's the constant, meaningful quantity. I also chose the answer options close together intentionally — to force you to distinguish between a volume-weighted and a mass-weighted mixture. Expect more of these “traps” as you move through the program; uncovering them in practice is the whole point.
OH 18 · July 16, 2021
Q: Do we have to use mass because the density of the two fluids is different? I originally solved it just using specific heat and volume fractions.
Dan's response: Exactly right — the mixing must be done on a mass basis. Because glycol is denser than water (SG = 1.15), the same 30% volume fraction of glycol corresponds to a higher mass fraction than you'd expect. So not only does glycol drag down the blended specific heat capacity, it does so in greater proportion than its volume implies. That's why you get the wrong answer when you mix by volume: you underestimate how much glycol (by mass) is actually in the system.
OH 35 · May 2, 2022
Q: There's a chart in the reference manual for glycol percentages — can we just use that instead of the long analytical approach? Does this problem have underlying theory we need to learn?
Dan's response: Yes — if you're able to use the table, use it. But the underlying theory is non-negotiable: specific heat capacity of a mixture is weighted by relative mass, not relative volume. That main idea is what I want you to take away. The analytical path — density from SG, mass from density × volume, mixing formula — is only a few minutes of work and isn't particularly complex. Being comfortable with both the table shortcut and the first-principles approach is what prepares you for variations you might see on exam day.

OH 92 · November 25, 2024
Q: Where does the cp mixing formula come from? I couldn't find it in the reference handbook.
Dan's response: It's not a formula you'll find written out in the handbook — it's a conceptual idea. Different material properties take their “weighted average” by either mass or by volume, and specific heat capacity is one that's weighted by mass. So: cp,mix = Σ(mi × cp,i) / Σmi. This generalizes to any number of constituents. The bigger lesson: try to let go of pure plug-and-chug and instead extract the general idea behind each problem. That mindset will serve you on variations you haven't seen before.
