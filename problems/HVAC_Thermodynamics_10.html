HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 10
Problem Statement
What quantity of heat is released per unit mass when copper is cooled from 250°F to 75°F?
Answer Choices
A.16 Btu/lb ✓
B.18 Btu/lb
C.57 Btu/lb
D.157 Btu/lb
Solution
Quick Overview:
  1. Recognize this as a sensible heat transfer problem — copper is cooled with no phase change, so specific heat applies directly.
  2. Write the heat transfer formula per unit mass: q = cp × ΔT  [Btu/lb]
  3. Look up the specific heat of copper in the NCEES Reference Handbook under Properties of Metals: cp,copper = 0.09 Btu/(lb·°F)
  4. Calculate the temperature difference: ΔT = 250°F − 75°F = 175°F
  5. Substitute and solve: q = 0.09 Btu/(lb·°F) × 175°F = 15.75 Btu/lb
  6. Round to the nearest answer: 16 Btu/lb → Answer A

Full Solution:

This problem asks for the heat released per unit mass as copper is cooled — a classic sensible heat calculation. Because we're dealing with a pure substance (copper) with no phase change, we use the specific heat equation. Starting from the general form Q = mcpΔT, dividing both sides by mass m gives the specific (per unit mass) heat transfer:

q = cp ΔT    [Btu/lb]

The key material property needed is the specific heat capacity of copper, cp. This must be looked up in the NCEES PE Reference Handbook — search the Properties of Metals or Properties of Solids section. The handbook lists the specific heat of copper as:

cp,copper = 0.09 Btu/(lb·°F)

Next, calculate the temperature change. The copper is cooled from an initial temperature of 250°F to a final temperature of 75°F:

ΔT = Tinitial − Tfinal = 250°F − 75°F = 175°F

Note the sign convention: because the copper is being cooled, heat is released from the copper to its surroundings. The magnitude of the heat release per unit mass is what the problem asks for. Substituting into the formula:

q = 0.09 Btu/(lb·°F) × 175°F = 15.75 Btu/lb

This value rounds to 16 Btu/lb, which corresponds to Answer A. The other choices represent common errors: Choice B (18 Btu/lb) results from using a slightly incorrect specific heat; Choice C (57 Btu/lb) suggests using water's specific heat (1.0 Btu/lb·°F) for an incorrect temperature range; Choice D (157 Btu/lb) is far too large and may result from not dividing by mass. The critical habit here is looking up the correct material property rather than relying on memory.

Handbook Reference: NCEES PE Reference Handbook — Section 9.1.7 (Heat Transfer for Water), Properties of Metals table for cp of copper = 0.09 Btu/(lb·°F). The general form Q = mcpΔT also appears in various thermodynamics sections throughout the handbook.

OH 69 · October 17th, 2023
Q: I need clarification on the compression ratio in the reference manual. I used RV = V1/V2 when applying constant entropy processes and the formula T2/T1 = (V2/V1)k−1. In the worked solution, the formula with P2/P1 was used and the compression ratio was plugged in. Why is my method incorrect?
Dan's response: The key is to distinguish the volumetric compression ratio from the plain compression ratio. When we say "compression ratio" without any qualifier, we mean the ratio of pressures: R = P2/P1. The volumetric compression ratio — which requires that adjective — is V2/V1. These are different parameters. The reason you can't swap them is grounded in the ideal gas law: PV ∝ T. If temperature were constant, then P and V would be inversely proportional and you could relate one ratio to the other. But that assumption of constant temperature is not valid here — so the pressure ratio and the volume ratio are separate and distinct. You must know which one you're actually dealing with in order to move forward. If you had a constant-temperature (isothermal) process, knowing one would imply the other — but that's unusual in real compressor problems.
