HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 27
Problem:

100,000 gpm of water falls 900 ft through a hydroelectric turbine system with 90% efficiency. The friction loss through the system is 100 ft. How much power is generated by the system?
  • A) 13 MW
  • B) 15 MW
  • C) 17 MW
  • D) 19 MW ✓
Quick Overview
  1. Identify the gross available head (900 ft) and the system efficiency (90%). Note the 100 ft friction loss — in this problem the friction loss is embedded within the overall system efficiency term.
  2. Convert the flow rate from gpm to ft³/s using the standard factor (divide by 448.83).
  3. Look up or recall the specific weight of water: 62.4 lb/ft³.
  4. Apply the hydraulic power formula: P = γ × Q × H ÷ η, where H is the gross head and η accounts for all losses including friction.
  5. Convert from ft·lbf/s to horsepower (divide by 550), then to kilowatts (multiply by 0.7457).
  6. Confirm the result is approximately 19 MW → Answer D.
Key Takeaways
  • In hydroelectric turbine problems, efficiency can appear in the numerator (turbine output) or denominator (required input power) depending on how the problem is framed — read the question carefully to determine which direction efficiency flows.
  • Friction losses in a piping system reduce the available head that does useful work; when they are bundled into an overall system efficiency, you use the gross head in the formula rather than subtracting them first.
  • Unit conversions are the #1 source of errors in fluid power problems — always convert gpm to ft³/s before computing power in English units.
  • Hydroelectric power problems test the same core energy-balance concept as pump power problems, just with energy flowing in the opposite direction.
Full Solution

Understanding the Problem Setup

Water with a flow rate of 100,000 gpm descends 900 ft through a hydroelectric turbine system. The system has an overall efficiency of 90% — this efficiency term captures all real-world losses in the system, including mechanical friction in bearings, electrical conversion losses, and the 100 ft of pipe friction loss stated in the problem. Because the 90% efficiency already accounts for friction, we use the gross head (900 ft) in the denominator-form of the power equation.

Step 1 — Convert Flow Rate to ft³/s

The standard English-unit conversion is:

Q (ft³/s) = Q (gpm) ÷ 448.83
Q = 100,000 gpm ÷ 448.83 = 222.8 ft³/s

Step 2 — Recall the Specific Weight of Water

For water at standard conditions (60°F):

γ = 62.4 lb/ft³

Step 3 — Identify System Parameters

Gross Head (H)900 ft
Friction Loss (h_f)100 ft (captured in η)
System Efficiency (η)0.90
Specific Weight (γ)62.4 lb/ft³
Flow Rate (Q)222.8 ft³/s

Step 4 — Apply the Hydraulic Power Formula

For a hydroelectric system where efficiency accounts for all losses (including friction), the required hydraulic power relationship is:

P = (γ × Q × H) ÷ η

Substituting values:

P = (62.4 lb/ft³ × 222.8 ft³/s × 900 ft) ÷ 0.90
P = (12,512,448 ft·lbf/s) ÷ 0.90
P = 13,902,720 ft·lbf/s

Step 5 — Convert to Horsepower

Using 1 HP = 550 ft·lbf/s:

P = 13,902,720 ft·lbf/s ÷ 550 ft·lbf/s·HP
P = 25,278 HP

Step 6 — Convert to Kilowatts and Megawatts

Using 1 HP = 0.7457 kW:

P = 25,278 HP × 0.7457 kW/HP
P = 18,850 kW
P ≈ 19 MW
✓ Answer: D) 19 MW
Alternative Using the 3960 Shortcut:
Some PE exam references use the shorthand formula P(HP) = Q(gpm) × H(ft) ÷ (3960 × η) for turbine/pump power problems. Applying it here:

P = 100,000 × 900 ÷ (3960 × 0.90) = 90,000,000 ÷ 3,564 = 25,252 HP ≈ 18,831 kW ≈ 19 MW

This shortcut (3960 = 33,000 ft·lbf/min ÷ γ_water × unit adjustments) produces the same result and is faster on exam day.

Why Friction Loss Doesn't Get Subtracted Separately

The key insight in this problem is that the 90% system efficiency is an overall efficiency that bundles together mechanical losses, electrical conversion losses, and hydraulic friction losses. This is the way many real-world plant engineers report turbine performance — as a single combined efficiency from water-in to watts-out. Therefore, you use the full gross head (900 ft) and let the efficiency term do all the work of accounting for losses. If the problem had given a hydraulic efficiency and a mechanical efficiency separately, you would compute net head by subtracting friction first, then apply the mechanical efficiency.

Video Synthesis

No dedicated video for this problem. Key teaching points synthesized from hydroelectric turbine fundamentals:

  • Efficiency direction matters: For pumps, efficiency is in the denominator (you need more power input than the fluid receives). For turbines generating electricity, the same denominator form applies — the water delivers more power than the generator outputs. Always track which direction energy flows.
  • The 448.83 conversion factor (gpm → ft³/s) is one of the most commonly used and mis-remembered conversions on the PE exam. Practice it until it's automatic — Q(ft³/s) = Q(gpm) / 448.83.
  • Bundled vs. separated efficiency: When a problem gives you a single overall efficiency, use it in one step. When it gives you hydraulic efficiency + mechanical efficiency separately, work through them sequentially — subtract friction head to get net head, then apply mechanical efficiency.
  • Gross head vs. net head framing: Problems vary in whether the stated head is gross (before losses) or net (after losses). The phrase "falls X feet" typically means gross available head. The friction loss is a separate deduction unless the efficiency already captures it.
  • Unit conversion chain: ft·lbf/s → HP (÷550) → kW (×0.7457) → MW (÷1000). This chain shows up on virtually every fluid power problem on the PE exam. Write it on your reference sheet.
  • Order-of-magnitude check: 100,000 gpm is an enormous industrial-scale flow rate (think major dam, not building HVAC). For problems like this, MW-range answers are physically reasonable — a result in kW would be a red flag that something went wrong with unit conversions.
