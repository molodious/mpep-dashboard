HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 5
Problem Statement
Water at 400 psi and 700°F is in what thermodynamic state?
Answer Choices
A.Liquid
B.Saturated Mixture
C.Saturated Vapor
D.Superheated Vapor ✓
Solution
Answer: D
1. Identify given information: Water at P = 400 psi and T = 700°F. Determine the thermodynamic state. 2. Open the saturated steam table organized by pressure and find the entry for 400 psi. 3. Read the saturation temperature at 400 psi: T_sat ≈ 444.6°F. 4. Compare the actual temperature to the saturation temperature: 700°F ≫ 444.6°F — the actual temperature is far above saturation. 5. Conclude: Since T_actual > T_sat at this pressure, the water must be superheated vapor (it's past the saturation curve, to the right on a T-s diagram). 6. Select the answer: Superheated Vapor → Answer D.
This is a foundational thermodynamics problem that tests your ability to determine the phase/state of water using the steam tables. It's simpler than most PE exam problems, but the underlying skill — identifying thermodynamic state — is used repeatedly in more complex problems. The approach: Look up the saturation temperature corresponding to the given pressure in the saturated steam tables. At 400 psi, the saturation temperature is approximately 444.6°F. This means that at 400 psi, water exists as a saturated mixture (part liquid, part vapor) only at exactly 444.6°F. Our water is at 700°F — far above 444.6°F. Since the temperature exceeds the saturation temperature at this pressure, the water must be in the superheated vapor region. It has passed completely through the two-phase (saturated) region and is now a single-phase superheated gas. You can think of it on a T-s (temperature-entropy) diagram: the saturation curve forms a dome. At a given pressure, points on the dome represent saturated conditions. Points to the right of the dome (higher temperature at the same pressure) are superheated. Points to the left (lower temperature) are subcooled/compressed liquid. The general rules: • If T_actual > T_sat at the given pressure → Superheated vapor • If T_actual = T_sat at the given pressure → Saturated (could be liquid, mixture, or vapor — need quality or another property to distinguish) • If T_actual < T_sat at the given pressure → Subcooled (compressed) liquid Equivalently, you can compare pressures: • If P_actual < P_sat at the given temperature → Superheated vapor • If P_actual = P_sat at the given temperature → Saturated • If P_actual > P_sat at the given temperature → Subcooled liquid

⭐ Key Takeaways:
• Determining thermodynamic state is a foundational skill — you'll do it as a sub-step in nearly every steam/water problem on the PE exam.

• Compare T_actual vs T_sat (at the given pressure), or P_actual vs P_sat (at the given temperature). Higher temperature or lower pressure → superheated.

• At the saturation line, you need a third property (quality, enthalpy, or entropy) to distinguish between saturated liquid, mixture, and saturated vapor.

• Subcooled liquid is sometimes called "compressed liquid" — same thing, just higher pressure or lower temperature than the saturation point.

Q: Water at 400 psi and 700°F is in what thermodynamic state? Could you provide examples of water in the other states — liquid, saturated mixture, and saturated vapor?
Dan: Great foundational question. Look in the saturated steam table organized by pressure — at 400 psi, the saturation temperature is 444.6°F. Our water is at 700°F, far above saturation, so it must be superheated vapor. More generally: any pair of values in the steam table (like 400 psi / 444.6°F) represents saturation conditions. At that exact temperature and pressure, you could be anywhere from saturated liquid to saturated vapor — you'd need quality, enthalpy, or entropy to pin down exactly where. If your temperature is higher than T_sat at your pressure (or your pressure is lower than P_sat at your temperature), you're superheated. If it's lower (or pressure is higher), you're subcooled/compressed liquid. For example, at 400 psi and exactly 444.6°F you could be a saturated mixture. This is a skill you'll use over and over — get comfortable with it early.
