Problem
HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 15
20,000 gpm of water flows through a turbine in a hydroelectric power application. The inlet pressure is 50 psig and the exit pressure is 1 atm. The turbines have an overall efficiency of 80%. What is the electrical capacity of the hydroelectric plant?
Answer Choices
A 350 KW  ✓
B 435 KW
C 470 KW
D 540 KW
Answer: A — 350 KW
Quick Overview
High-Level Solution Steps
  1. Recognize that exit pressure is 1 atm = 0 psig, so ΔP = 50 psig − 0 psig = 50 psi.
  2. Convert the flow rate from gpm to ft³/s: 20,000 gpm → 44.6 ft³/s.
  3. Convert ΔP from psi to lb/ft² (psf): 50 × 144 = 7,200 lb/ft².
  4. Calculate the available fluid power: W = Q × ΔP = 44.6 × 7,200 ≈ 321,000 ft·lb/s.
  5. Convert fluid power to kW: 321,000 ft·lb/s ÷ 737.56 ≈ 435 kW (available water power).
  6. Apply 80% overall efficiency to find electrical output: 435 × 0.80 ≈ 348 KW ≈ 350 KW (Answer A).
Key Takeaways
⚡ Exam Strategy
  • When exit pressure is given as "1 atm" or "atmospheric," treat it as 0 psig. The delta P is simply the gauge inlet pressure — no subtraction needed if you stay in gauge units throughout.
  • The 435 kW intermediate result is the available fluid power (Answer B). Applying the 80% efficiency to get the actual electrical output is the key final step — don't stop at the fluid power.
  • Unit conversions are the main pitfall here: gpm → ft³/s requires dividing by 7.48 and 60; psi → lb/ft² requires multiplying by 144; ft·lb/s → kW requires dividing by 737.56. Keep them organized.
  • For turbines, efficiency reduces output (output = fluid power × η). For pumps, efficiency reduces input — the direction of the efficiency penalty flips depending on the machine type.
Full Solution

This is a turbine power problem. Water flows through a hydraulic turbine, giving up pressure energy that gets converted to mechanical (and then electrical) power. The approach is: find the fluid power available from the pressure drop, then multiply by the overall efficiency to get the electrical output.

Step 1 — Identify the Pressure Difference

The inlet pressure is given as 50 psig (gauge). The exit pressure is given as 1 atm. This is the key unit trap in this problem.

By definition, gauge pressure is measured relative to atmospheric pressure. So:

1 atm (absolute) = 14.7 psia = 0 psig ΔP = P_inlet − P_exit = 50 psig − 0 psig = 50 psi

You can verify this using absolute pressures: 64.7 psia − 14.7 psia = 50 psi. Either route gives the same delta P — as long as you're consistent (all gauge or all absolute), the result is identical.

💡 Gauge vs. Absolute reminder: 1 atm = 14.7 psia (absolute) = 0 psig (gauge). When a problem says "exit pressure is 1 atm," that means the fluid discharges to atmosphere — which is the zero reference for gauge pressure. Don't add 14.7 to your delta P calculation.

Step 2 — Convert Flow Rate to ft³/s

The flow rate is given in gallons per minute. We need consistent units, so convert to ft³/s:

Q = 20,000 gpm × (1 ft³ / 7.48 gal) × (1 min / 60 s) Q = 20,000 / (7.48 × 60) = 20,000 / 448.8 Q = 44.57 ft³/s

Step 3 — Convert ΔP to lb/ft² (psf)

To use the power equation W = Q × ΔP in consistent US customary units, pressure must be in lb/ft² (psf), not psi:

ΔP = 50 psi × 144 in²/ft² = 7,200 lb/ft²

Step 4 — Calculate Available Fluid Power

The power delivered by the water to the turbine is flow rate times pressure drop:

W_fluid = Q × ΔP W_fluid = 44.57 ft³/s × 7,200 lb/ft² W_fluid = 320,900 ft·lb/s

Step 5 — Convert Fluid Power to kW

Convert from ft·lb/s to kilowatts using the conversion factor 1 kW = 737.56 ft·lb/s:

W_fluid = 320,900 ft·lb/s ÷ 737.56 (ft·lb/s)/kW W_fluid ≈ 435 kW ← this is the available water power (Answer B)

Note: 435 kW is the fluid power input to the turbine — what the water could theoretically deliver if the turbine were 100% efficient. This matches Answer B, but the problem specifies an 80% overall efficiency, so we're not done yet.

🔍 Alternative path using horsepower: You can also convert via horsepower as an intermediate step.
1 hp = 550 ft·lb/s → W_fluid = 320,900 / 550 = 583.5 hp
Then to kW: 583.5 hp × 0.7457 kW/hp = 435 kW ✓ (same result)

Step 6 — Apply Overall Efficiency

The turbine has an overall efficiency of 80%. This accounts for all losses between the incoming water power and the electrical output — mechanical friction, generator losses, etc. For a turbine, efficiency reduces the output:

W_electrical = W_fluid × η W_electrical = 435 kW × 0.80 W_electrical ≈ 348 kW → 350 KW

Answer: A — 350 KW.

📺 Key Teaching Insights
  • "1 atm exit" means 0 psig: This is the central trap in this problem. The problem gives exit pressure as "1 atm" to test whether you know that 1 atm = 14.7 psia = 0 psig. Students who mistakenly subtract 14.7 from the inlet pressure get the wrong delta P. Stay in gauge units and it simplifies immediately to ΔP = 50 psi.
  • The 435 kW result is the water power — not the answer: Answer B is placed to catch students who compute fluid power correctly but forget efficiency. The problem asks for electrical capacity, which requires the efficiency multiplier. Always re-read what the problem is asking before selecting.
  • Unit conversions are the backbone of this solution: gpm → ft³/s → ft·lb/s → kW is a chain of three conversions. The factors (÷ 7.48, ÷ 60, × 144, ÷ 737.56) must all be memorized or derivable from first principles for the exam.
  • Turbine efficiency multiplies output; pump efficiency divides input: For turbines, you get less out than what the fluid provides (output = fluid power × η). For pumps, you have to put more in than the fluid requires (input = fluid power / η). These are opposite directions — know which machine you're analyzing.
  • Hydroelectric problems are essentially pressure-drop power problems: No elevation change or velocity change is given here — the entire energy exchange is through the pressure drop across the turbine. Bernoulli simplifies to W = Q × ΔP when head and velocity terms cancel.
  • Cross-check with WHP formula: The water horsepower formula WHP = Q(gpm) × ΔP(psi) / 1714 gives a fast shortcut: 20,000 × 50 / 1714 = 583 hp → × 0.7457 = 435 kW (fluid power). Then × 0.80 = 348 kW. Same answer, fewer intermediate conversions.

Q: What are all the different ways to express 1 atmosphere? I keep getting confused by the different conversion factors. OH 74
A: Great thing to nail down. 1 atm has many equivalent expressions depending on what units you're working in:
  • 14.7 psi (absolute, in US customary)
  • 29.9 inches of mercury (Hg)
  • 33.9 feet of water (H₂O)
  • 101.325 kPa (SI)
The goal isn't to memorize every form — it's to have intuition around these numbers. If you know 1 atm ≈ 14.7 psi and 1 atm ≈ 34 ft of water, you can derive the others on the fly. For this problem, the key insight is simply that 1 atm = 0 psig — the reference point for gauge pressure.
Q: In the answer video, you calculated the delta P as 50 psig minus 1 atm. We mentioned that the exit pressure is 0. Why is the exit pressure not 14.7 psi converted from 1 atm? OH 68
A: This is about the difference between gauge pressure and absolute pressure. 1 atm = 14.7 psia (absolute) = 0 psig (gauge). These are the same physical pressure — just measured from different reference points.

When the problem says the exit is at "1 atm," that means the fluid discharges directly to the atmosphere — which is exactly where gauge pressure is zero. So in gauge units: ΔP = 50 psig − 0 psig = 50 psi.

You can also work in absolute: 64.7 psia − 14.7 psia = 50 psi. Either way, the delta P is 50 psi. Once you subtract, it doesn't matter whether you used gauge or absolute — it's just a delta, and the offset cancels out.
Q: Can you elaborate on the relationship between total pressure, gauge pressure, and absolute pressure? OH 41
A: Think of it like measuring height in a room with a raised stage. Absolute pressure is measured from the true zero — the floor. Gauge pressure is measured from the stage (the atmosphere). The atmosphere is like the height of the stage above the floor.

In this problem: the inlet is 50 psig — that's 50 psi above atmospheric. The outlet discharges to atmosphere — that's 0 psig. Delta P = 50 − 0 = 50 psi.

A second engineer who prefers absolute pressures would say: inlet is 50 + 14.7 = 64.7 psia, outlet is 14.7 psia, and ΔP = 64.7 − 14.7 = 50 psi. Same result — as long as you're consistent within a calculation, both approaches work perfectly. The common mistake is mixing gauge and absolute in the same equation without adjusting.
