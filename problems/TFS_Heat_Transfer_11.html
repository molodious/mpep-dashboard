Full Solution: In office building seeks to reduce heat losses through the windows by 75% by installing insulating shades. The design conditions for winter are 10 degrees outside, 68 degrees inside. The existing windows are 1-8 inch thick, single pane glass with thermal conductivity K equals 0.7. There will be an air gap between the windows and the shades, providing additional resistance of R equals 1.2. The shades have a thermal conductivity of K equals 0.05. The convective heat transfer coefficients inside and outside are 1.5 and 4 respectively. What is the minimum thickness of the shades required to achieve the desired reduction in energy loss? This sounds like a long problem and there's a lot of information here, but it's actually not too bad if we just break it down and take it step by step. So we're going to treat this situation with the shade as a composite wall and we'll compare the original state. Let's call it state 1 before any shades have been added to the new state after the shades have been added. So in the original condition, all we have is the glass which has some thickness and then we've got the inside and the outside conditions and this is the glass. So basically we've got 3 nodes here or 4 nodes rather from heat transfer perspective. We've got the node for convection from the inside condition to the glass and we've got some resistance going through the glass conduction and then we've got more convection from the outside of the glass to outside. So 3 layers of the composite wall so to speak 4 nodes. In the after state, we're going to be adding let's take away our little thermal picture here. We're going to be adding a shade so another layer, some distance away of some thickness. So now this is our shade, this is still our glass inside is here, outside is here and there's an air gap here down the middle. So now we've got more nodes, one for the inside, convection to the shade, conduction through the shade, we've got the resistance of an air gap which is mostly convection but the values actually given to us. We've got more resistance through the glass and then convection again to the outside. So now we've got 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 nodes and 5 layers to consider. But let's go back to that original case and ignore the shade really we only had 3 layers to consider. We'll be the resistance of just those 3 layers and actually before we even go into that, let's just think about from a high level what we expect the resistance to be in the new case as compared to the old. We should expect that the resistance is going to be greater because we're going to reduce the energy losses by 75% how much greater. Well to quantify that we can think about the heat losses after as compared to before. We might say Q.1 and Q.2 could both be expressed as UA Delta T, U1A Delta T and then we could divide by A to get a heat flux and we could also instead of multiplying by U the overall heat transfer coefficient we could divide by the total resistance R.
