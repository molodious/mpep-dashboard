Full Solution: A 10 centimeter solid steel ball initially at 400 degrees C is placed in a large volume of 25 degrees C liquid with unknown specific heat capacity. The average convective coefficient of heat transfer is 1,000 watts per meter squared degree Kelvin. How long does it take for the ball to cool to 100 C? So anytime you see a heat transfer problem is asking how long does it take? That's a transient heat transfer problem and there's two different ways of handling these sorts of problems. There's the lumped capacitance method. You can actually search the reference handbook for lumped capacitance and you'll find some of the formulas that we're about to use. And the other one is the distributed method. So the way I like to think of this is it's a comparison of which forces are a bigger, a more significant driver of what's happening from a heat transfer perspective. Is it the conductive forces inside the object or is it the convective forces outside the object? For example, if you've ever grilled a steak, you think about the thickness of that material using very high heat. So you're exposing the outer part of the meat to this incredibly high flame. That's going to drive a high surface temperature on the outside. Then there's conduction that heat has to propagate through the thickness of the meat to the center. We don't give it very much time to do that. Just three or four minutes on very high heat and then we flip it over and do the same thing on the other side and then it has to propagate down. The end result is that we get this nice sear on the outer surface and then we get some pink if you like your steak, medium or medium rare. This still trace a pink in the middle. So that's very much a distributed scenario where you have to be concerned with how much time and how high the heat is. You can contrast that with a suavee cooking method where you're literally just putting the meat exposing it to a large water bath of a fixed temperature. Let's say 135 degrees and maintaining the water at that temperature and raising the meat to that final temperature gradually. The conduction is almost negligible because that may happen over 24 hours and pretty much the entire thickness of the meat is at one constant temperature. Same temperature is the water 135 at the end. So this would be lumped and this would be distributed. And how do you know if you can use lumped or distributed? Well it depends on the particular situation but there's an analytical way to tell and we start by calculating what's called the BO number or BO number. V is the volume. A is the surface area and K is the thermal conductivity and sometimes V over A taken together is called the characteristic length. So let's just calculate that V over A as by itself first. The volume of a sphere is 4.3 pi R cubed and this is the diameter of 10 centimeters.
