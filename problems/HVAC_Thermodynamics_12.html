HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 12
Problem Statement
A 1200 ft³ room is filled with atmospheric air at 95°F. The room air is cooled to 70°F under constant pressure. How much work is done on the air in the room?
Answer Choices
A.2 Btu
B.150 Btu ✓
C.520 Btu
D.1330 Btu
Solution
✓ Answer: B — 150 Btu
Quick Overview:
  1. Identify the system as a closed system with no change in kinetic or potential energy; the process is constant pressure (atmospheric).
  2. Write the boundary work formula for a constant-pressure closed system: w = PΔv = P(v1 − v2), where v is specific volume [ft³/lbm].
  3. Apply the ideal gas law (Pv = RT) to substitute for specific volumes, then cancel the constant pressure: w = R(T1 − T2) [Btu/lbm].
  4. Calculate the specific work using Rair = 53.35 ft·lbf/(lbm·°R) and ΔT = 25 °R, converting to Btu.
  5. Find the total mass of air in the room using the ideal gas law at state 1: m = PV/(RT1).
  6. Multiply specific work by total mass to get total work: W = m × w ≈ 147 Btu → Answer B.
Full Solution:

The problem asks for the total work done on air inside a 1200 ft³ room as it cools from 95°F to 70°F at constant (atmospheric) pressure.

Step 1 — Identify the thermodynamic process. The room is a sealed, rigid enclosure — a closed system. The process is constant pressure (atmospheric air at 14.7 psia). There is no change in elevation or velocity, so ΔKE = ΔPE = 0. The applicable work expression is boundary work (flow work) for a constant-pressure process.

Step 2 — Boundary work per unit mass. For a closed system at constant pressure (MERM §4.3.1.1):

w = P(v1 − v2)   [ft·lbf/lbm]

Step 3 — Substitute the ideal gas law. For an ideal gas, Pv = RT, so v = RT/P. Substituting both states into the work formula and canceling the constant pressure P:

w = P(RT1/P − RT2/P) = R(T1 − T2)

Note: temperatures must be in absolute units (°R). The difference T1 − T2 is the same whether expressed in °F or °R (since 1°F = 1°R in difference).

Step 4 — Calculate specific work. Using Rair = 53.35 ft·lbf/(lbm·°R) (MERM Table 24.7) and ΔT = 95 − 70 = 25 °R:

w = 53.35 [ft·lbf/(lbm·°R)] × 25 [°R] / 778.16 [ft·lbf/Btu]
w = 1333.75 / 778.16 = 1.714 Btu/lbm

Step 5 — Find total mass of air. Use the ideal gas law at state 1 (T1 = 95 + 460 = 555 °R, P = 14.7 lbf/in², V = 1200 ft³). Convert pressure: 14.7 lbf/in² × 144 in²/ft² = 2116.8 lbf/ft².

m = PV / (R × T1) = (14.7 × 144 [lbf/ft²] × 1200 [ft³]) / (53.35 [ft·lbf/(lbm·°R)] × 555 [°R])
m = 2,540,160 / 29,609 = 85.8 lbm

Step 6 — Total work.

W = m × w = 85.8 [lbm] × 1.714 [Btu/lbm] = 147 Btu ≈ 150 Btu

This matches answer choice B (150 Btu). Note that state 1 is used to compute mass because all initial conditions are fully defined; since the room is a closed system, mass is constant throughout the process and the same value would result from state 2.

📹 Video Solution Synthesis:

  • The problem is set up as a closed system — air in a sealed room cooling at constant atmospheric pressure. Even though the room walls don't move, work is still done because the specific volume of the air changes as temperature drops.
  • The key formula comes from §4.3.1.1 of the reference handbook: for a constant-pressure closed system, boundary work is w = PΔv. Don't be misled by the rigid room — the system boundary concept here is about the air mass, not the walls.
  • Substituting the ideal gas law (Pv = RT) collapses the work expression neatly to w = RΔT, where R is the specific gas constant for air (53.35 ft·lbf/lbm·°R). This is a clean, one-step formula once you recognize the substitution.
  • To find total work, the mass of air must be calculated. The ideal gas law at any single state is sufficient — PV = mRT. Using state 1 (95°F, 14.7 psia, 1200 ft³) yields m ≈ 85.8 lbm.
  • An equally valid alternative to the ideal gas law for finding mass: look up air density at 95°F in the handbook tables (ρ ≈ 0.0716 lbm/ft³), then compute m = ρV. Both methods produce essentially the same mass and the same final answer.
  • Final multiplication gives W ≈ 147 Btu, which rounds to 150 Btu — answer B. The other distractors (2, 520, 1330 Btu) correspond to common errors: using zero work (constant volume assumption), using Q = mCpΔT for work, or skipping the Btu conversion entirely.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
  • A constant-pressure process still involves boundary work even when the room volume is fixed — what matters is that the specific volume (volume per unit mass) of the air changes as it cools, and that change represents work done.
  • Work and heat are not interchangeable. Using Q = mCpΔT gives you the heat transferred, not the work done. The two differ by the change in internal energy. Always match the formula to what the question is actually asking for.
  • For a closed system, mass is constant across all states — it doesn't matter which state you use when calculating mass from the ideal gas law, as long as you have complete information for that state.
  • The ideal gas law and air density tables are two completely valid paths to the same answer. When answer choices are spread apart, either method works; when they're close together, prefer the property that accounts for the actual temperature rather than assuming standard-condition density.

OH 18 · July 16th, 2021
Q: Instead of using PV = mRT, can I just use mass = density × volume with the standard density of air (0.075 lbm/ft³)? I got 90 lbm instead of 85.8 lbm.
Dan's response: Using density × volume is a valid approach — the real issue is which density you use. Standard air density (~0.075 lbm/ft³) is based on approximately 68–70°F, but this problem has air at 95°F. The warmer air is less dense, around 0.0709 lbm/ft³, so using the standard value overstates the mass by about 5%. In this problem the answer choices are spread out enough that either value leads you to B, so it doesn't change your selection. But if the choices were close together you'd want to sharpen your pencil — use PV = mRT or interpolate the property table at the actual temperature rather than defaulting to standard conditions.
OH 53 · February 27th, 2023
Q: Mass depends on temperature, yet we only calculate it at 95°F (state 1). Why not at 70°F (state 2), or at the average temperature?
Dan's response: Great question that ties into a fundamental concept. This is a closed system — no air enters or leaves the room during the cooling process. That means the mass is constant throughout; it doesn't change between state 1 and state 2. We use state 1 simply because we have complete information there (temperature, pressure, volume are all given). If we had complete data for state 2 instead, we could use that and get the same answer. The mass isn't a function of the state in a closed system — it's fixed. This closed-versus-open-system distinction is one worth hammering home because it determines which equations apply.
OH 77 · March 18th, 2024
Q (Part 1): Is using density from the air property tables (interpolated at 95°F, ρ ≈ 0.0716 lbm/ft³) a valid alternative to the ideal gas law for finding mass? I got the same answer (85.86 lbm).
Q (Part 2): I thought the work done equals the heat gain — why doesn't Q = mCpΔT give the right answer?
Dan's response: Part 1 — yes, completely valid. The reason both methods agree is that the ideal gas law P = ρRT is just a rearrangement of PV = mRT. If you use that relation to compute density from the known pressure and temperature, you'll get the exact same value as interpolating the table. Different paths, same destination — that's a nice cross-check to carry with you.

Part 2 — the error is equating work with heat. The first law of thermodynamics for a closed system is Q − W = ΔU + ΔKE + ΔPE. We can strike KE and PE changes for this problem, but we cannot strike the internal energy change ΔU. So work equals heat minus the change in internal energy, not just heat alone. Using Q = mCpΔT gives you the total heat transfer (enthalpy change), not the work. The correct approach is the boundary work formula W = PΔv, which you'll also encounter again in the hydraulics section with pumps.
OH 111 · January 5th, 2026
Q: Why can't I use W = mCpΔT for work? Both expressions involve mass and ΔT, so what's the fundamental difference?
Dan's response: The distinction comes down to Cp vs. R. For a constant-pressure ideal gas process, the correct specific work is w = RΔT, not CpΔT. The relationship between them is Cp = Cv + R, so Cp is larger than R by exactly Cv. That extra Cv piece represents the change in internal energy — the portion of heat transfer that goes into changing the internal energy of the gas rather than doing boundary work. If you use CpΔT you're lumping both the work and the internal energy change together, which overstates the work. So W = mRΔT for work; W = mCpΔT only for total heat transfer at constant pressure.
OH 115 · March 2nd, 2026
Q (Part 1): The volume of the room is constant on both sides — shouldn't the work be zero since W = PΔV and ΔV = 0?
Q (Part 2): Is there any reason we must use state 1 temperature when calculating mass, rather than state 2 or an average?
Dan's response: Part 1 — this is a genuinely subtle point. The room volume is constant, but that doesn't mean the air's specific volume is constant. As the room cools, air could leave through gaps or the density could change. The key is to think about the system: we're analyzing a fixed mass of air (closed system), and for that fixed mass, the specific volume (volume per unit mass) changes with temperature because temperature changes with constant pressure. So W = PΔv applies to the specific volume, not the room volume. The room being a rigid box is slightly misleading — what matters is the thermodynamic state of the air mass itself.

Part 2 — no particular reason beyond having complete information. This is a closed system, so mass is constant. You can use state 1 or state 2 to calculate m from PV = mRT; they should give the same result. The solution uses state 1 because all the initial conditions are explicitly given.
