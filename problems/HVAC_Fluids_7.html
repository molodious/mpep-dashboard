Problem Statement
Problem 7 — Tee Connection Outlet Velocity

A tee connection receives 4 gpm of water from a ¾ inch schedule 40 steel pipe and 3 ft/s of water from a 1¼ inch schedule 40 steel pipe. Water exits into a 1½ inch schedule 40 steel pipe.

What is the velocity in the outlet pipe? Ignore friction loss.
Answer Choices
A. 2.4 ft/s
B. 2.8 ft/s
C. 3.3 ft/s
D. 4.8 ft/s
✓ Answer: B — 2.8 ft/s

Applying the continuity equation at the tee junction, the combined volumetric flow rate through the 1½" outlet pipe results in a velocity of approximately 2.8 ft/s. See the Full Solution panel on the right for the complete worked solution.

Nominal Size Inside Dia. (in) Flow Area (ft²)
¾ inch 0.824" 0.003703 ft²

1 · Quick Overview

High-level steps to solve this tee-connection velocity problem:

  1. Identify the junction type. A tee connection that combines two inlets into one outlet is a combining-flow junction. Conservation of mass (continuity) governs: Qout = Qin1 + Qin2.
  2. Convert inlet 1 from gpm to ft³/s. The ¾" pipe delivers 4 gpm. Use the conversion 1 gpm = 1/448.83 ft³/s to get Q₁ in consistent units.
  3. Convert inlet 2 from velocity to ft³/s. The 1¼" pipe carries water at 3 ft/s. Multiply velocity by the pipe's cross-sectional area: Q₂ = V₂ × A₂.
  4. Sum the inlet flow rates. Qtotal = Q₁ + Q₂, using consistent units throughout (ft³/s).
  5. Look up the 1½" schedule 40 inside diameter. From standard pipe tables, ID = 1.610 inches. Compute the outlet area Aout = π/4 × (ID/12)².
  6. Divide to get velocity. Vout = Qtotal / Aout. Compare to the answer choices — the result rounds to 2.8 ft/s.
2 · Full Solution

This problem applies the continuity equation at a tee junction where two flow streams combine. Because friction loss is ignored and the fluid is water (incompressible), we use volumetric continuity: the total volume flow rate entering equals the volume flow rate leaving.

Governing Equation

Continuity at combining tee: Q_out = Q_in1 + Q_in2 Velocity in outlet pipe: V_out = Q_out / A_out

Step 1 — Identify the Schedule 40 Pipe Inside Diameters

Flow calculations always use the inside diameter — the nominal size is just a designation. From ASME B36.10M / standard pipe tables:

¾" Schedule 40: ID = 0.824 in → 0.0687 ft 1¼" Schedule 40: ID = 1.380 in → 0.1150 ft 1½" Schedule 40: ID = 1.610 in → 0.1342 ft (outlet)

Step 2 — Calculate the Cross-Sectional Areas

A = π/4 × D² A_¾ = π/4 × (0.0687 ft)² = 0.003703 ft² A_1¼ = π/4 × (0.1150 ft)² = 0.010387 ft² A_1½ = π/4 × (0.1342 ft)² = 0.014138 ft² ← outlet

Step 3 — Convert Inlet 1 from gpm to ft³/s

The ¾" pipe carries 4 gpm. The conversion factor is: 1 gal = 0.13368 ft³, 1 min = 60 s, so 1 gpm = 0.13368/60 ft³/s.

Conversion: 1 gpm = 1/448.83 ft³/s = 0.002228 ft³/s Q₁ = 4 gpm × 0.002228 ft³/s per gpm Q₁ = 0.008912 ft³/s

Step 4 — Convert Inlet 2 from Velocity to ft³/s

The 1¼" pipe carries water at 3 ft/s (velocity). Use Q = V × A:

Q₂ = V₂ × A_1¼ Q₂ = 3 ft/s × 0.010387 ft² Q₂ = 0.031161 ft³/s

Step 5 — Apply Continuity: Sum the Inlet Flows

Q_out = Q₁ + Q₂ Q_out = 0.008912 + 0.031161 Q_out = 0.040073 ft³/s

Step 6 — Calculate Outlet Velocity

Divide the total volumetric flow rate by the outlet pipe's cross-sectional area:

V_out = Q_out / A_1½ V_out = 0.040073 ft³/s ÷ 0.014138 ft² V_out = 2.834 ft/s Rounded to one decimal place: V_out ≈ 2.8 ft/s

Result

The outlet velocity is 2.8 ft/s → Answer B.

Why the Other Choices Are Wrong

  • A (2.4 ft/s): This would result if only one of the two inlet flows were included in the sum — e.g., using only the gpm inlet and forgetting to add Q₂ from the velocity inlet.
  • C (3.3 ft/s): A result near this value can arise if the wrong inside diameter is used for the outlet pipe — for example, using the 1¼" area instead of the 1½" area, which gives a falsely small denominator and inflated velocity.
  • D (4.8 ft/s): This would result from a unit conversion error — for example, using the 3 ft/s value directly as a flow rate (ft³/s) rather than multiplying by the pipe area first. That triples the inlet flow and over-estimates V_out significantly.

💡 Exam Tip: On PE exam tee problems, always confirm that all flow rates are in the same units before summing. A common mistake is adding gpm to ft³/s without converting first.

3 · Video Synthesis
📹 Key Insights — Tee Connections & Continuity
  • Tee junctions are simply continuity points. Whether you have a combining tee (two inlets, one outlet) or a splitting tee (one inlet, two outlets), the continuity equation always holds: the sum of flows in equals the sum of flows out. Friction loss is a separate calculation and doesn't change the flow balance.
  • Mixed unit problems are intentional exam traps. Providing one flow rate in gpm and another as a velocity (ft/s) forces you to recognize that you must convert everything to a single volumetric unit before applying continuity. Always check units at every step.
  • Schedule 40 pipe ID lookup is a required skill. The nominal pipe size is NOT the inside diameter — for 1½" sch 40, the ID is 1.610", not 1.500". Using the nominal size will give a slightly wrong area and therefore a slightly wrong velocity. The exam expects you to use the actual inside diameter from standard tables.
  • The gpm–ft³/s conversion appears constantly. Memorize: 1 gpm = 0.002228 ft³/s (or equivalently, 1 ft³/s = 448.83 gpm). This conversion bridges the common mixed-unit scenario on HVAC/Fluids problems where volumetric flow and velocity are given together.
  • Flow area scales as the square of diameter. Shifting from a 1¼" pipe to a 1½" pipe increases the area by roughly 36% — so even though the outlet pipe is only ¼" larger in nominal size, it carries significantly more cross-sectional area. Building intuition about area ratios helps sanity-check velocity calculations.
  • Ignoring friction loss simplifies the problem to pure geometry. When the problem says "ignore friction loss," it's telling you that only continuity (mass conservation) is needed — no Darcy-Weisbach, no minor loss coefficients. This is a signal to focus solely on Q = V × A.
4 · Key Takeaways
⭐ Key Takeaways
  • Tee connections conserve mass: total flow in equals total flow out. This is the continuity equation applied to a junction. It holds true regardless of the pipe sizes, velocities, or whether friction is present — mass (and for an incompressible fluid like water, volume) is always conserved.
  • Always use the inside diameter — not the nominal size — for schedule 40 pipe calculations. Nominal pipe sizes are designations, not actual dimensions. For accurate flow areas and velocities, look up the true inside diameter from standard pipe tables (e.g., ¾" sch 40 ID = 0.824", 1½" sch 40 ID = 1.610").
  • Converting between gpm and ft³/s is a common exam unit conversion. Many HVAC and fluids problems give flow rates in gallons per minute, but velocity and area calculations require ft³/s. Keeping 1 gpm = 0.002228 ft³/s on your reference sheet saves time and prevents unit errors.
  • Velocity can increase or decrease when flows combine, depending on the outlet pipe size. In this problem, combining two inlets into a slightly larger outlet pipe resulted in a moderate outlet velocity. If the outlet pipe were smaller, the velocity would be higher; if larger, it would be lower. The outlet pipe size is the key variable controlling exit velocity.
