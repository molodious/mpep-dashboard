Due to acoustical considerations, the compressor and condenser for a two-ton refrigerator with the COP of 5 are located remotely in a nearby storage closet. Neglecting lights and other ancillary heat sources, what is the required cooling capacity for the storage closet? So let's draw out the refrigeration cycle. So we know it's a two-ton refrigerator, so that's going to be our QN, that's the refrigeration effect or the heat absorbed by the evaporator. And then we know that the refrigerator has a COP of 5, well what is the COP for refrigerator? COP for refrigerator is QN over WN, the refrigeration effect divided by the energy that goes into the refrigerant because of the compressor, so that equals 5. So we can rearrange that for the compressor energy since we know QN, divided by 5 on both sides and multiply by WN on both sides and we'll find the work of the compressor is two tons over 5 which is 0.4 tons. So that's our WN that's now defined and now our QN out we can also find. And where are we going with this? Well the compressor and condenser have been relocated into a storage closet for a acoustical reasons, the compressor probably makes noise. So we can draw a box that just isolates this two components and says all right well, these two components, even though from refrigeration perspective it's the same cycle, the condenser and the compressor are in a storage closet in a separate room where is the evaporator is outside in the actual refrigerator and the expansion valve is probably local to the evaporator and just you know comes right before. So the irony here is we have a refrigerator, something that exists for the purpose of performing cooling and yet now we have to provide separate and additional cooling to the storage closet where the heat rejection takes place. So we either have to have some fan coil or some other cooling to handle that room and the question is how much? And it's really Q out that's going to tell us that. So how much heat is being rejected by the condenser? Q out is Q in plus WN. So that's two tons plus the point four tons which is two point four tons. Now you might be tempted to say hey the condenser is in the storage closet and so is the compressor so we have to add Q out and WN but that would be double counting because WN is already contained in Q out. So the energy that goes into the refrigerant by virtue of the fact that the compressor is doing work on the refrigerant and it's adding heat it's increasing the enthalpy and the process of changing the refrigerant from a saturated vapor to a super heat of vapor at high pressure and the process of doing that yes it's adding heat but it's not adding heat to the local environment it's adding that heat to the refrigerant itself so it's really Q out and only Q out that needs to be counted for how we're going to remove heat from this storage closet. So don't add another point four tons that would be double counting the compressor that's probably the easiest way to get this problem wrong it's just Q out by itself. And the answer choices are in B2's per hour so we'll just have to convert that by multiplying by the conversion factor 12000 B2's per hour per ton tons cancel and we end up with 28800 B2 per hour which is closest to answer choice C.
