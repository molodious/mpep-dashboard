Problem
HVAC — Psychrometrics — Problem 2
An outside air handling unit is equipped with a hot water coil to temper the fresh air intake during winter operation. The heating coil is sized for 80,000 Btu. The outside conditions are 20°F and 50% relative humidity. What is the humidity ratio after heating? Assume that the degree of saturation is roughly equivalent to relative humidity.
Answer Choices
A 0.001 lbw/lbda
B 0.002 lbw/lbda  ✓
C 0.003 lbw/lbda
D 0.004 lbw/lbda
Answer: B — 0.002 lbw/lbda
Quick Overview
  1. Identify given conditions: outside air at 20°F dry-bulb and 50% relative humidity. The heating coil capacity (80,000 Btu) is provided but is not needed to find humidity ratio.
  2. Look up saturation humidity ratio at 20°F from psychrometric data or steam tables. At 20°F, the saturation humidity ratio Ws is approximately 0.0036 lbw/lbda. Ws @ 20°F ≈ 0.0036 lbw/lbda
  3. Apply the relative humidity relationship (W = φ × Ws) since degree of saturation ≈ RH as stated in the problem: W = φ × Ws = 0.50 × 0.0036 = 0.0018 lbw/lbda
  4. Calculate the humidity ratio at intake conditions: W ≈ 0.0018 lbw/lbda, which rounds to 0.002 lbw/lbda.
  5. Recognize that sensible heating does NOT change humidity ratio — the hot water coil adds heat but no moisture. W stays constant. The process is a horizontal line moving right on the psychrometric chart.
  6. Report the humidity ratio after heating: same as before heating — W ≈ 0.002 lbw/lbda. Answer: (B)
📝 Key Insight: The 80,000 Btu heating coil capacity is a red herring for this question. It tells you how much the temperature will rise, but humidity ratio is completely unaffected by sensible heating. Always identify the psychrometric process type before calculating — sensible heating = constant humidity ratio.

Full Solution

This problem tests a fundamental concept in psychrometrics: sensible heating is a constant humidity ratio process. When a hot water coil heats air, it raises the dry-bulb temperature without adding or removing any moisture. On the psychrometric chart, this process is represented by a horizontal line moving to the right — the humidity ratio (W) stays perfectly flat.

Step 1 — Saturation Humidity Ratio at 20°F

At 20°F, the saturation pressure of water vapor from steam tables is very small. Using psychrometric data or the ASHRAE chart, the saturation humidity ratio at 20°F is approximately:

Ws @ 20°F ≈ 0.0036 lbw/lbda

This is the maximum moisture the air can hold at 20°F — very little, because cold air has a very low capacity for water vapor.

Step 2 — Apply the Humidity Ratio Relationship

The problem states to assume degree of saturation ≈ relative humidity. The key formula is:

W = φ × Ws

Where φ (phi) is the relative humidity expressed as a decimal:

W = 0.50 × 0.0036 W = 0.0018 lbw/lbda

Rounding to three decimal places: W ≈ 0.002 lbw/lbda

Step 3 — Sensible Heating Process

The hot water coil provides 80,000 Btu of sensible heat to the air. Sensible heating means the coil raises the dry-bulb temperature only. No moisture is added or removed from the airstream. Therefore:

W_before heating = W_after heating = 0.0018 lbw/lbda ≈ 0.002 lbw/lbda

On the psychrometric chart, the state point moves horizontally to the right — temperature increases, but the horizontal position of the humidity ratio axis remains unchanged.

Step 4 — Answer

The humidity ratio after heating is the same as before heating: W ≈ 0.002 lbw/lbda.

W_after = 0.002 lbw/lbda → Answer: (B)

The 80,000 Btu heating capacity would be used to calculate the exit temperature of the air (using q = ṁ × cp × ΔT), but that is not what the problem asks. Always read the question carefully — it asks for humidity ratio, not temperature.

📺 Video Synthesis
  • Sensible heating changes temperature only — humidity ratio stays constant on the psychrometric chart. This is a horizontal process line moving to the right.
  • The 80,000 Btu capacity is a red herring for finding humidity ratio — it determines how much the exit temperature rises, not the moisture content. Don't let it distract you.
  • Degree of saturation vs. relative humidity: they are approximately equal at low humidity ratios, which is why the problem tells you to make this assumption. At cold, dry conditions the two values converge.
  • At 20°F, air holds very little moisture — Ws ≈ 0.0036 lbw/lbda. At 50% RH, only about half that is present. Cold outside air is inherently dry, even at high relative humidity.
  • Always identify the psychrometric process first (sensible heating = horizontal line, cooling with dehumidification = diagonal down-left, humidification = nearly vertical) before starting any calculation.
⚡ Key Takeaways
  • Sensible heating never changes the moisture content of air — only the temperature changes. The humidity ratio is identical before and after a hot water or electric heating coil.
  • At low temperatures, even saturated air holds very little moisture. Cold outside air in winter is dry by nature, regardless of its relative humidity percentage.
  • On the psychrometric chart, sensible heating moves horizontally to the right — temperature rises, humidity ratio stays flat, relative humidity drops.
  • Don't be distracted by heating capacity values when the question asks about humidity ratio — capacity data is only needed to find exit temperature or energy calculations.
