HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 11
Problem Statement
An outside air handling unit delivers 5,000 cfm of outside air. On a winter day, the outside air is 18°F and is heated to 68°F using a hot water coil. Hot water is supplied at 165°F and returned at 140°F. What is the required flow rate of hot water?
Answer Choices
A.0.4 gpm
B.3 gpm
C.22 gpm
D.173 gpm
Solution
✔ Answer: C — 22 gpm
Quick Overview — How to Approach This Problem:
  1. Recognize that this is a sensible heating problem only: outside air is heated from 18°F to 68°F, with no mention of humidification, so latent heat plays no role.
  2. Calculate the heat load on the air side using the sensible heat rule of thumb: Q = 1.08 × CFM × ΔTair.
  3. Set the air-side heat gain equal to the heat given up by the hot water: Qair = Qwater.
  4. Apply the hot water rule of thumb for the water side: Q = 500 × GPM × ΔTwater.
  5. Solve algebraically for GPM, the unknown flow rate of hot water.
  6. Verify the answer is dimensionally consistent and matches a reasonable answer choice (~22 gpm).
Full Solution:

This problem connects the air side and the water side of an air handling coil using an energy balance. The hot water coil is the heat source; outside air is the heat sink. At steady state, energy in equals energy out.

Step 1 — Identify the process.
Outside air at 18°F is heated to 68°F. No humidification is mentioned, so this is purely sensible heating: dry bulb temperature increases, moisture content stays the same. A horizontal line on the psychrometric chart.

Step 2 — Calculate heat load on the air side.
The sensible heat rule of thumb for air at standard conditions (density ≈ 0.075 lb/ft³, cp ≈ 0.24 Btu/lb·°F) gives:

Qair = 1.08 × CFM × ΔTair
Qair = 1.08 × 5,000 cfm × (68°F − 18°F)
Qair = 1.08 × 5,000 × 50 = 270,000 Btu/hr

Step 3 — Set up the energy balance.
The hot water must supply exactly that 270,000 Btu/hr to the air. For hot water systems, the rule of thumb is:

Qwater = 500 × GPM × ΔTwater

Where 500 = ρ × cp × conversion factors for water (density ≈ 8.34 lb/gal, cp = 1 Btu/lb·°F, 60 min/hr → 8.34 × 1 × 60 ≈ 500).

Step 4 — Solve for GPM.

Qair = Qwater
270,000 Btu/hr = 500 × GPM × (165°F − 140°F)
270,000 = 500 × GPM × 25
270,000 = 12,500 × GPM
GPM = 270,000 ÷ 12,500 = 21.6 gpm

Rounded to the nearest answer choice: 22 gpm → Answer C.

Alternate method (first principles): You can also solve this using mass flow rates and specific heats directly. The mass flow of air = 5,000 cfm ÷ 13.5 ft³/lb (specific volume at standard conditions) ≈ 370 lb/min. Heat added to air = 370 lb/min × 0.24 Btu/lb·°F × 50°F ≈ 4,444 Btu/min = 266,640 Btu/hr. Then mass flow of water = Q ÷ (cp,water × ΔTwater) = 266,640 ÷ (1 × 25) = 10,666 lb/hr ÷ (8.34 lb/gal × 60 min/hr) ≈ 21.3 gpm. Slight differences are due to specific volume rounding — both methods confirm answer C.

📹 Video Solution Synthesis:
  • Energy balance is the core setup: Dan frames this problem as Qwater = Qair. The hot water coil gives up heat; the outside air receives it. Writing that balance explicitly is the entire structure of the solution.
  • Rule of thumb 1.08 × CFM × ΔT: This shorthand combines air density (0.075 lb/ft³), specific heat of air (0.24 Btu/lb·°F), and a 60 min/hr conversion into a single constant. It's exact at standard conditions and the PE exam expects you to use it fluently.
  • Rule of thumb 500 × GPM × ΔT: The water-side counterpart. 8.34 lb/gal × 1 Btu/lb·°F × 60 min/hr ≈ 500. These two equations are your primary tools for hot water coil problems.
  • No latent heat — here's why: The problem gives only dry bulb temperatures (no wet bulb, no relative humidity change). Latent heat by definition does not change the dry bulb temperature — only sensible heat does. If humidification were happening, the problem would say so explicitly.
  • Sanity check the answer: The four answer choices span three orders of magnitude (0.4, 3, 22, 173 gpm). If your arithmetic is even close, 22 stands out. Learning to sanity-check magnitude protects you from unit errors under exam pressure.
  • First-principles verification: Dan demonstrates the M·cp·ΔT approach as a cross-check, arriving at essentially the same answer. Knowing two routes to the same answer builds confidence and catches errors.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
  • Sensible heating changes dry bulb temperature only — no moisture content change, no latent heat. If a problem gives inlet and outlet temperatures without any humidity data, use the sensible heat formula and nothing else.
  • The two rule-of-thumb equations (1.08 × CFM × ΔT for air and 500 × GPM × ΔT for water) are essential exam tools. Know them, know where they come from, and know their limits (standard air density conditions).
  • For heating coil problems, the energy balance Qair = Qwater is the governing equation. The air side tells you how much heat is needed; the water side tells you how much flow is required to deliver it.
  • Rule-of-thumb equations and first-principles M·cp·ΔT approaches should give the same answer. If you distrust rules of thumb under pressure, build the first-principles version so you have a mental backup on exam day.

Q: Why is it acceptable to ignore the latent heat component for outside air in this problem? Real outside air always has moisture. How do I know when to use the sensible heat formula versus accounting for latent heat?
A: The key insight: the air does have moisture, but the process doesn't have a latent component. This problem is pure sensible heating — dry bulb temperature rises from 18°F to 68°F, and there's no humidification happening. Latent heat, by definition, doesn't change dry bulb temperature; it changes moisture content. If humidification were part of the problem, the problem statement would say so (and would likely give wet bulb or humidity data). The tell-tale keyword to watch for is "humidification." Absent that, use 1.08 × CFM × ΔT and move on. (OH 79)
