HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 11
Problem Statement
An outside air handling unit delivers 5,000 cfm of outside air. On a winter day, the outside air is 18°F and is heated to 68°F using a hot water coil. Hot water is supplied at 165°F and returned at 140°F. What is the required flow rate of hot water?
Answer Choices
A.0.4 gpm
B.3 gpm
C.22 gpm ✓
D.173 gpm
Solution
Answer: C
Quick Overview: 1. Identify this as a sensible heating problem — air is heated with no humidification, so use sensible heat formulas only. 2. Set up the energy balance: the heat released by the hot water equals the heat absorbed by the air (Q_water = Q_air). 3. Apply rule-of-thumb formulas: Q_water = 500 · GPM · ΔT_water and Q_air = 1.08 · CFM · ΔT_air. 4. Plug in values: CFM = 5,000, ΔT_air = 68 − 18 = 50°F, ΔT_water = 165 − 140 = 25°F. 5. Solve for GPM by setting the two expressions equal: GPM = (1.08 × 5,000 × 50) / (500 × 25). 6. Select the closest answer choice: GPM ≈ 21.6 → Answer C (22 gpm). Full Solution: Method 1 — Rule-of-Thumb Approach (fastest on exam day): This is a sensible heating problem. The air is heated from 18°F to 68°F — dry bulb temperature rises with no mention of humidification, so latent heat is not involved. The hot water coil transfers heat from the water loop to the airstream. Energy balance: Q_water = Q_air Apply the standard rule-of-thumb equations for standard-density conditions: Q_air = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT_air Q_water = 500 × GPM × ΔT_water Given: CFM = 5,000 cfm ΔT_air = 68°F − 18°F = 50°F ΔT_water = 165°F − 140°F = 25°F Set Q_water = Q_air and solve for GPM: 500 × GPM × 25 = 1.08 × 5,000 × 50 12,500 × GPM = 270,000 GPM = 270,000 / 12,500 GPM = 21.6 gpm → Answer C (22 gpm) Method 2 — Mass-Flow (First Principles) Approach: For those who want to work from specific heat and mass flows rather than memorized constants, the same result can be reached this way. Energy balance in terms of mass flows: ṁ_water × cp_water × ΔT_water = (V̇_air / v_air) × cp_air × ΔT_air Where: V̇_air = 5,000 cfm (volumetric flow of air) v_air = 13.5 ft³/lbm (specific volume of air at ~68°F, standard conditions) cp_air = 0.24 BTU/lbm·°F ΔT_air = 50°F cp_water = 1.0 BTU/lbm·°F ΔT_water = 25°F Solve for mass flow of water: ṁ_water = (5,000 / 13.5) × 0.24 × 50 / (1.0 × 25) ṁ_water = 370.4 × 0.24 × 50 / 25 ṁ_water = 370.4 × 0.48 ṁ_water = 180.4 lbm/min Convert to volumetric flow using water density (8.34 lbm/gal): GPM = 180.4 / 8.34 ≈ 21.6 gpm → Answer C (22 gpm) Both methods agree. The rule-of-thumb approach is faster, but the mass-flow approach builds deeper understanding and confirms the result — a powerful combination for exam confidence.

⭐ Key Takeaways:
• Rule-of-thumb equations for sensible heat are powerful shortcuts for standard-density conditions — they're fast, reliable, and exam-ready.

• Knowing two methods (rule-of-thumb AND mass-flow from first principles) builds confidence and flexibility — if your mind second-guesses one approach, the other provides verification.

• Sensible heating means no moisture change — the absence of the word "humidification" in the problem is your signal to use sensible heat formulas only.

• Always set up an energy balance first: what the water gives up, the air absorbs. This framing works regardless of which formula you apply.

• Self-trust on the exam is built during study. Solving problems multiple ways earns confidence that memorizing shortcuts alone cannot.

Q: Why is it okay to assume that the outside air in the problem doesn't have a latent component? It seems like a 50-50 chance using the sensible heat formula versus the total heat formula. In the real world, wouldn't outside air virtually always have latent heat?
Dan's response: Yes — outside air does carry moisture and therefore has a latent component. But the process described in the problem is sensible heating only. On the psychrometric chart, this is a horizontal line: dry bulb temperature increases while the humidity ratio stays constant. The key signal is the absence of the word "humidification" — if the problem involved adding moisture, latent heat would come into play. Since it's just heating, use Q = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT. Dan's core insight: "Latent heat by definition does not impact dry bulb temperature. This problem was about increasing dry bulb temperature. So that's sensible heating."
