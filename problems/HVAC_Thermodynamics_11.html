HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 11
Problem Statement
An outside air handling unit delivers 5,000 CFM of outside air. On a winter day, the outside air is 18°F and is heated to 68°F using a hot water coil. Hot water is supplied at 165°F and returned at 140°F. What is the required flow rate of hot water?
Answer Choices
A.0.4 gpm
B.3 gpm
C.22 gpm ✓
D.173 gpm
Solution
Answer: C — 22 gpm
Quick Overview:
  1. Recognize that heating air with a hot water coil is a sensible-only process — no humidification means no latent component.
  2. Apply the sensible heating rule of thumb for air: Qs = 1.08 × CFM × ΔTair (BTU/hr), with CFM in ft³/min and ΔT in °F.
  3. Apply the sensible heating rule of thumb for water: Qw = 500 × GPM × ΔTwater (BTU/hr), with GPM in gal/min and ΔT in °F.
  4. Set Qair = Qwater — the heat gained by the air equals the heat given up by the hot water coil.
  5. Solve for GPM, the only unknown, by substituting known values.
Full Solution:

The air handling unit is pulling in cold outside air at 18°F and heating it to a supply temperature of 68°F. The heat source is a hot water coil, with water entering at 165°F and leaving at 140°F. The goal is to find how many gallons per minute of hot water are required to accomplish this heating load.

Since there is no humidification involved — only a dry-bulb temperature increase — this is a purely sensible heating process. Latent heat by definition does not change dry-bulb temperature, so no latent component needs to be considered here. This means we can use the two standard sensible-heat rules of thumb (ASHRAE-derived approximations valid for standard air density near sea level).

Step 1 — Sensible heat added to the air:

Qair = 1.08 × CFM × ΔTair  [BTU/hr]

Where CFM = 5,000 ft³/min and ΔTair = 68°F − 18°F = 50°F.

Qair = 1.08 × 5,000 × 50 = 270,000 BTU/hr

Step 2 — Sensible heat released by the hot water:

Qwater = 500 × GPM × ΔTwater  [BTU/hr]

Where ΔTwater = 165°F − 140°F = 25°F.

Step 3 — Energy balance (Qair = Qwater):

1.08 × CFM × ΔTair = 500 × GPM × ΔTwater

Step 4 — Solve for GPM:

GPM = (1.08 × CFM × ΔTair) / (500 × ΔTwater)
GPM = (1.08 × 5,000 × 50) / (500 × 25) = 270,000 / 12,500 = 21.6 GPM

Rounding to the nearest answer choice gives 22 GPM → Answer C. Note that no unit conversions are required when using these rules of thumb, provided all inputs are already in CFM, GPM, and °F. The constant 1.08 implicitly accounts for air density (0.075 lb/ft³) and specific heat of air (0.24 BTU/lb·°F) at standard conditions; the constant 500 accounts for the density and specific heat of water (8.34 lb/gal × 60 min/hr × 1.0 BTU/lb·°F).

Handbook reference: ASHRAE Fundamentals Handbook — sensible heat rate equations for air and water are commonly tabulated or derivable from Q = ṁ cp ΔT. The PE Reference Handbook includes the underlying thermodynamic relationships (specific heat, energy balance) in the Heat Transfer and Thermodynamics sections.

📹 Video Solution Synthesis:
  • Dan identifies two rules of thumb that make this problem efficient to solve: Q = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT for air sensible heat (BTU/hr) and Q = 500 × GPM × ΔT for water sensible heat (BTU/hr) — same formulas used throughout the HVAC module.
  • The energy balance is set up as Qair = Qwater, recognizing that 100% of the heat given up by the water goes into heating the air (an ideal coil assumption common in PE problems).
  • GPM is isolated as the single unknown: GPM = (1.08 × 5,000 × 50) / (500 × 25) = 21.6 GPM, which rounds directly to answer choice C (22 GPM).
  • Dan emphasizes that the inputs must be in CFM, GPM, and °F for the rules of thumb to output BTU/hr correctly — no additional unit conversions are needed when conditions are right.
  • He also notes that a first-principles approach using ṁ = ρ × Q̇, cp,air = 0.24 BTU/lb·°F, ρair ≈ 1/13.5 lb/ft³, and cp,water = 1.0 BTU/lb·°F works equally well and yields the same 21.6 GPM result.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
  • Heating air from a cold to a warm temperature — with no change in moisture content — is sensible heating only; do not include a latent term just because outside air contains moisture.
  • The sensible heating rules of thumb (1.08 for air, 500 for water) are powerful time-savers on the PE exam, but they're only valid when all inputs are in standard units (CFM, GPM, °F).
  • An energy balance across the coil connects the air side and the water side — the heat gained by the air equals the heat lost by the hot water, making this a single-equation solve once you recognize the setup.
  • The rules of thumb and the first-principles ṁcpΔT approach are two roads to the same answer; knowing both builds the exam confidence to check your work quickly.

OH 79 · April 15th, 2024
Q: Why is it okay to assume the outside air doesn't have a latent component? In the real world, outside air virtually always has moisture — shouldn't we use a total heat formula rather than sensible only? Is it just because wet-bulb temperature data isn't given?
Dan's response: It's not that we're assuming the air has no moisture — it certainly does. The key is distinguishing the process from the state of the air. Latent heat by definition does not change dry-bulb temperature. This problem is only about raising the dry-bulb temperature from 18°F to 68°F, which is a horizontal line on the psychrometric chart — sensible heating only. If the problem involved humidification, it would say so explicitly and give you wet-bulb or humidity data. The absence of that data is your cue that moisture content isn't changing. So the correct formula is Qs = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT for the air side, full stop. The keyword to watch for when a latent component does apply is "humidification."
OH 89 · October 14th, 2024
Q: I tried to solve this using specific heat and mass flow rates (ṁ cp ΔT) instead of the rules of thumb, but couldn't reconcile the answer. Can you work through the first-principles approach to confirm it gives the same result?
Dan's response: Absolutely — and this is a great instinct. Setting up the mass-flow energy balance gives: ṁwater = (ṁair × cp,air × ΔTair) / (cp,water × ΔTwater). The air volume flow rate is 5,000 CFM. To get mass flow, divide by specific volume: 5,000 ft³/min ÷ 13.5 ft³/lb = 370.4 lb/min of air. Multiply by cp,air = 0.24 BTU/lb·°F and ΔTair = 50°F: 370.4 × 0.24 × 50 = 4,444 BTU/min. Divide by cp,water = 1.0 BTU/lb·°F and ΔTwater = 25°F: 4,444 / 25 = 177.8 lb/min of water. Convert to GPM using 8.34 lb/gal × 1 gal/min: 177.8 / 8.34 = 21.3 GPM — essentially the same as 21.6 GPM from the rule of thumb (small difference from rounding the specific volume). Either approach correctly selects Answer C. Dan encourages knowing both: rules of thumb are faster on exam day, but the first-principles method is the foundation that proves you understand why the shortcut works.
