HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 5
Problem Statement
A pump designed to operate at 100 gpm and 50 ft of head is determined to be oversized. The pump's impeller diameter is decreased by 20%. What is the resulting pressure developed by the pump after this change?
Answer Choices
A.26 ft
B.32 ft ✓
C.40 ft
D.50 ft
Solution
Answer: B
Quick Overview: 1. Recognize this as an Affinity Laws (pump similarity laws) problem — specifically the relationship between impeller diameter and developed head. 2. Identify the relevant Affinity Law: head varies as the SQUARE of the diameter ratio when speed is held constant. 3. Calculate the new impeller diameter: D₂ = 0.80 × D₁ (a 20% decrease means the new diameter is 80% of the original). 4. Apply the head-diameter law: H₂ = H₁ × (D₂/D₁)² = 50 × (0.80)² = 50 × 0.64. 5. Compute the result: H₂ = 32 ft — matching answer choice B. Full Solution: This problem tests knowledge of the Pump Affinity Laws (also called the Fan Laws or Similarity Laws), which describe how a pump's performance changes when either its rotational speed or its impeller diameter is changed. Background — The Three Affinity Laws (at constant speed, varying diameter): When impeller diameter changes from D₁ to D₂ (at constant rotational speed n): Flow rate: Q₂/Q₁ = D₂/D₁ Head: H₂/H₁ = (D₂/D₁)² Power: P₂/P₁ = (D₂/D₁)³ The key insight: head scales with the SQUARE of the diameter ratio, while flow scales linearly. This square relationship is why reducing the impeller diameter significantly reduces the developed head. Step 1 — Identify known values: Original operating point: Q₁ = 100 gpm H₁ = 50 ft of head D₁ = original impeller diameter (exact value not needed — only the ratio matters) The impeller diameter is decreased by 20%, so: D₂ = D₁ × (1 − 0.20) = 0.80 × D₁ Therefore the diameter ratio is: D₂/D₁ = 0.80 Step 2 — Apply the head Affinity Law: Using the head-diameter relationship at constant speed: H₂/H₁ = (D₂/D₁)² Solving for H₂: H₂ = H₁ × (D₂/D₁)² H₂ = 50 ft × (0.80)² H₂ = 50 ft × 0.64 H₂ = 32 ft Step 3 — Verify the answer: A 20% reduction in impeller diameter results in a (0.80)² = 0.64 factor — meaning the new head is 64% of the original. H₂ = 0.64 × 50 ft = 32 ft ✓ This matches answer choice B. Why the other choices are wrong: • Choice A (26 ft): This would correspond to approximately (0.72)² ≈ 0.52 — a much larger diameter reduction than stated. • Choice C (40 ft): This result comes from applying a LINEAR relationship (H₂ = 0.80 × 50 = 40 ft) — a common mistake when students forget that head scales as the SQUARE of the diameter, not linearly. • Choice D (50 ft): No change — incorrect because the problem clearly states the impeller was trimmed. Important exam note: The Affinity Laws apply to both speed changes AND diameter changes. On the PE exam, always check whether the problem is changing speed, diameter, or both. The mathematical form is identical — only the physical quantity being changed differs. When changing diameter at constant speed, use D₂/D₁ exactly as you would use n₂/n₁ when changing speed at constant diameter.
📹 Video Solution Synthesis:

• The Pump Affinity Laws are a high-frequency PE exam topic — memorize all three relationships: flow scales with D (or n), head scales with D² (or n²), and power scales with D³ (or n³). These appear in multiple problem types across the Fluids chapter.

• The most common trap on diameter-trimming problems is applying a linear relationship to head instead of a squared one. If you get 40 ft, you used Q's law (linear) instead of H's law (squared) — a classic wrong-answer trap that the exam writers deliberately include.

• Impeller trimming is a real-world technique used to "right-size" a pump that is oversized for its system — instead of replacing the entire pump, the impeller is machined down to reduce output. The Affinity Laws predict the resulting performance change accurately for trim ratios up to about 20%.

• Notice that the original flow rate (100 gpm) is provided in the problem but is NOT needed to solve for the new head. This is intentional — exam problems often include extra data to test whether you know which variables are relevant to each law.

• When checking your answer, the squared relationship means even a modest diameter reduction creates a significant head drop. A 20% trim → 36% reduction in head. A 10% trim → only 19% head reduction. Keeping this intuition in mind helps catch arithmetic errors quickly.

• Exam strategy: identify what is changing (speed vs. diameter), write the relevant Affinity Law ratio, substitute the known ratio, and solve. The entire problem can be solved in under 60 seconds once you recognize the pattern.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
• Head is much more sensitive to diameter changes than flow rate is — a 20% reduction in diameter cuts the head by 36%, while only cutting flow by 20%. This non-linear behavior is why impeller trimming is an effective way to reduce a pump's pressure output.

• Extra data in a problem is not always needed — the original flow rate of 100 gpm plays no role in finding the new head. Recognizing which variables matter to which Affinity Law is as important as knowing the law itself.

• Impeller trimming and speed reduction are interchangeable mathematically — the Affinity Law exponents are the same whether you are scaling diameter or rotational speed. Master one form and you've mastered both.

• In practice, oversized pumps waste energy and can cause system instability. Trimming the impeller is a cost-effective solution that keeps the same pump casing and motor while bringing performance in line with actual system requirements.
