Problem Statement

A 20,000 ft² factory uses LED lighting with a power density of 0.7 W/ft² for the LED strips plus 15% for drivers. The space contains eight 93%-efficient 7.5-hp motors operating at an average of 70% of their rated capacity. The machinery driven by the motors is located within the conditioned space. There are 120 factory employees performing light machine work.

What is the total internal heat gain?

Answer Choices
  • A 180,000 Btu/hr
  • B 185,000 Btu/hr
  • C 280,000 Btu/hr ✓
  • D 330,000 Btu/hr
Quick Overview
  1. Lighting: Multiply floor area × power density to get LED strip wattage, then add 15% for drivers. Convert total watts → Btu/hr using 3.412.
  2. Motors — shaft output: Eight motors at 70% load gives the combined shaft horsepower actually delivered to the machinery.
  3. Motors — electrical input: Because the machinery is inside the conditioned space, ALL electrical input (not just shaft output) becomes heat. Divide shaft hp by motor efficiency to get electrical input hp.
  4. People: Use the ASHRAE heat-gain value for "light machine work" (~1,000 Btu/hr per person) and multiply by headcount.
  5. Sum the three components (lighting + motors + people) to get total internal heat gain.
  6. Select answer — compare the calculated total to the given choices.
Full Solution
Step 1 — Lighting Heat Gain

1a. LED Strip Wattage

Start with the base load: area times the LED power density.

qstrips = 20,000 ft² × 0.7 W/ft²
qstrips = 14,000 W

1b. Add 15% for LED Drivers

LED drivers (power supplies) consume additional electricity that is entirely dissipated as heat inside the space. The problem specifies 15% on top of the strip wattage.

qlighting = 14,000 W × 1.15
qlighting = 16,100 W

1c. Convert to Btu/hr

Use the standard electrical-to-thermal conversion factor: 1 W = 3.412 Btu/hr.

qlighting = 16,100 W × 3.412 Btu/(W·hr)
qlighting = 54,933 Btu/hr
Tip: The 15% driver adder is easy to forget on exam day. The problem explicitly calls it out — always read carefully for adders, ballast factors, or power-supply losses in lighting calculations.
Step 2 — Motor / Equipment Heat Gain

2a. Key Principle: Machinery Inside the Conditioned Space

This is the most conceptually important part of the problem. Ask yourself: where does the energy go?

  • The motor converts electrical energy → shaft work + motor heat losses.
  • The shaft work goes into the machinery. The machinery converts that work into product motion, friction, heat, etc. — all of it ends up as heat inside the space.
  • The motor's own inefficiency losses also stay inside the space.

Therefore, 100% of the electrical input power to the motor becomes a heat gain to the conditioned space — not just the shaft output, but the full electrical draw including motor losses.

Contrast: If only the motor were inside the space (machinery outside), only the motor losses would contribute to heat gain. If only the machinery were inside (motor outside), only the shaft output would count.

2b. Calculate Combined Shaft Output

Eight motors, each rated at 7.5 hp, operating at 70% of rated capacity:

Pshaft = 8 motors × 7.5 hp/motor × 0.70
Pshaft = 42 hp

2c. Calculate Electrical Input (= Heat Gain)

The motor is 93% efficient, so it draws more electrical power than it delivers as shaft work:

Pelectrical = Pshaft ÷ ηmotor
Pelectrical = 42 hp ÷ 0.93
Pelectrical = 45.16 hp

Convert to Btu/hr using 1 hp = 2,545 Btu/hr:

qmotors = 45.16 hp × 2,545 Btu/(hp·hr)
qmotors = 114,932 Btu/hr
Why divide by efficiency? Motor efficiency = shaft out / electrical in. Rearranging: electrical in = shaft out / efficiency. Since ALL electrical input ends up as heat (both motor losses and shaft work done inside the space), use the electrical input — not the shaft output — as the heat gain.
Step 3 — Occupant Heat Gain

3a. ASHRAE Heat Gain for Light Machine Work

ASHRAE Fundamentals provides total heat gain rates (sensible + latent) by activity level. For light machine work in a factory setting, the applicable value is approximately 1,000 Btu/hr per person.

This is notably higher than sedentary office work (~450 Btu/hr) because factory workers exert more effort, generating more metabolic heat. Both the sensible component (raising air temperature) and the latent component (perspiration / humidity) contribute to the total cooling load.

3b. Multiply by Headcount

qpeople = 120 people × 1,000 Btu/(hr·person)
qpeople = 120,000 Btu/hr
Step 4 — Sum All Components & Select Answer

4a. Summary Table

Component Calculation Summary Heat Gain (Btu/hr)
💡 Lighting (LED + Drivers) 20,000 × 0.7 × 1.15 × 3.412 54,933
⚙️ Motors (Electrical Input) (8 × 7.5 × 0.70 / 0.93) × 2,545 114,932
👷 People (Light Machine Work) 120 × 1,000 120,000
Total Internal Heat Gain ≈ 289,865 Btu/hr

4b. Select the Best Answer

The calculated total of approximately 289,865 Btu/hr rounds closest to 280,000 Btu/hr. The small discrepancy reflects rounding in the ASHRAE occupant heat-gain table and intermediate calculations. Answer choices A and B are far too low, while D (330,000) would require significantly higher heat rates.

✓ Answer: C — 280,000 Btu/hr
Calculated ≈ 289,865 Btu/hr → closest to 280,000 Btu/hr
Key Takeaways
  • ⚙️
    Machinery location determines what fraction of motor power is heat gain. When both the motor and the driven equipment are inside the conditioned space, ALL electrical input — including the motor's own inefficiency losses — becomes a heat gain. Nothing escapes the space.
  • 💡
    LED driver losses must be added to lighting heat gain. The driver's 15% adder in this problem is not cosmetic — it represents real power dissipated as heat inside the building. Always check the problem statement for adders, ballast multipliers, or power-supply overhead.
  • 👷
    Occupant heat gain scales with activity level. "Light machine work" generates roughly twice the heat of a sedentary office worker. Identify the correct ASHRAE activity category — it can swing the occupant load by hundreds of Btu/hr per person.
  • 🔄
    Know your unit conversions cold. In internal heat gain problems you'll chain between W, Btu/hr, and hp constantly. The two key factors: 1 W = 3.412 Btu/hr and 1 hp = 2,545 Btu/hr.
