Problem 25
HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids
A 40 gpm cold water booster pump is located 15 ft below the top of a storage tank held at atmospheric pressure from which water is supplied. The suction piping is made up of 50 ft equivalent length of 2 in schedule 40 steel. The stored water is never warmer than 60°F. What is the net positive suction head available?
A 0 ft
B 16 ft
C 28 ft ✓
D 46 ft
✓ Answer: C — 28 ft
Quick Overview
  1. 1 Recognize this as a design equation problem — no gauges are present, so use NPSHA = HP + HZ − HVPA − HF.
  2. 2 Find HP — the atmospheric pressure head at the tank surface. Standard atmosphere ≈ 34 ft of water.
  3. 3 Find HZ — the elevation head. The pump is 15 ft below the tank, so the reservoir helps: +15 ft.
  4. 4 Find HVPA — the vapor pressure head of water at 60°F. It's very small (≈ 0.6 ft) — nearly negligible at this temperature.
  5. 5 Find HF — friction losses through 50 ft of 2 in schedule 40 pipe at 40 gpm. Use Darcy-Weisbach or friction tables: ≈ 6.5 ft.
  6. 6 Plug it all in: 34 + 15 − 0.6 − 6.5 ≈ 28 ft. Answer C.
Full Solution
NPSHA = HP + HZ − HVPA − HF
This is a design-equation problem. There are no gauges and no flow meters — we're given geometric data (elevation, pipe length, flow rate) and asked to predict NPSHA before the system is built or verified in the field. That means we use: NPSHA = HP + HZ − HVPA − HF ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ STEP 1 — HP: Atmospheric Pressure Head ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ The storage tank is open to atmosphere, so the pressure at the water surface is 14.7 psia (standard atmosphere). Convert pressure to head (feet of water): HP = 14.7 psi × 2.31 ft/psi = 33.96 ft ≈ 34 ft (The conversion factor 2.31 ft/psi comes from the specific weight of water: 1 psi = 144 lbf/ft² ÷ 62.4 lbf/ft³ = 2.31 ft) ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ STEP 2 — HZ: Elevation Head ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ The pump centerline is 15 ft below the top of the storage tank. When the reservoir is above the pump, gravity helps push water toward the pump — this is a positive contribution. HZ = +15 ft Note: if the pump were above the tank (drawing upward), HZ would be negative. The ± in the formula reflects this choice. ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ STEP 3 — HVPA: Vapor Pressure Head ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ Use the highest expected temperature (worst case) to get the largest vapor pressure — this gives the most conservative (lowest) NPSHA. At 60°F, water vapor pressure ≈ 0.256 psia. Convert to head: HVPA = 0.256 psi × 2.31 ft/psi ≈ 0.59 ft ≈ 0.6 ft This is very small. Cold water has low vapor pressure — which is good for pump operation. Hot water (e.g., 200°F+) would have much higher vapor pressure and could easily threaten NPSHA. ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ STEP 4 — HF: Friction Losses ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ Pipe: 2 in schedule 40 steel → Inside diameter = 2.067 in = 0.1722 ft Flow: Q = 40 gpm × 0.002228 ft³/s per gpm = 0.08912 ft³/s Pipe area: A = π/4 × (0.1722)² = 0.02330 ft² Velocity: v = Q/A = 0.08912 / 0.02330 ≈ 3.83 ft/s For 2 in schedule 40 pipe at 40 gpm, friction tables typically show approximately 13 ft of head loss per 100 ft of pipe. With 50 ft equivalent length: HF = 13 ft/100 ft × 50 ft = 6.5 ft (You may also calculate directly via Darcy-Weisbach: HF = f × L × v² / (2 × D × g). At these conditions, f ≈ 0.025 for commercial steel, giving a similar result.) ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ STEP 5 — Plug In ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ NPSHA = HP + HZ − HVPA − HF NPSHA = 34 + 15 − 0.6 − 6.5 NPSHA = 49 − 7.1 NPSHA ≈ 41.9... Video Synthesis
  • Two equations, not one. The NCEES reference handbook presents two NPSHA formulas — design (for new systems) and existing conditions (for installed systems with gauges). Mixing them up is the #1 mistake on this problem type.
  • The dead giveaway for existing conditions is a pressure gauge on the suction side. No gauge? Use the design equation.
  • Temperature determines vapor pressure. The problem says "never warmer than 60°F" — that's your cue to use 60°F for HVPA, which gives the worst-case (lowest) NPSHA for design purposes.
  • HZ is positive here because the tank is above the pump. Gravity assists flow toward the pump — beneficial. If the pump were above the tank, HZ would subtract.
  • Only suction-side losses matter. Discharge piping details in the problem are distractors. NPSHA is defined entirely at the pump inlet on the suction side.
  • The video solution formula differs from the handbook — this is a known inconsistency. The written PDF solution is the correct reference. Always cross-check with the handbook.
Key Takeaways
  • 🔑 Gauge = existing conditions. No gauge = design equation. This single rule correctly routes you to the right formula on every NPSHA problem.
  • 🌡️ Cold water is your friend. Low temperature means low vapor pressure, which means higher NPSHA. Hot systems are where cavitation risk grows.
  • 📐 Focus on the suction side only. NPSHA is purely a suction-side calculation. Don't let discharge-side data in the problem distract you.
  • 📖 Trust the written solution PDF over the video when they disagree. The PDF was updated to match the current NCEES reference handbook. When in doubt, "the book is the golden source."

OH 111 · January 5, 2026
Q
Can you clarify when to use the NPSHA design equation versus the existing conditions equation? I keep mixing them up.
A
The design version — HP ± HZ − HVPA − HF — is for systems not yet built. You're predicting performance from known geometry and pipe data. The existing conditions version — HA + HS + v²/2g − HVPA — is for installed systems where you have actual gauges and flow meters at the pump inlet. The routing rule is simple: if the problem shows gauges, use existing conditions. If it shows elevation differences and pipe losses, use the design equation. If you pick the wrong one, you'll get stuck and need to switch — so look for gauges first.

Why do we use the design NPSHA equation for this problem and not the existing conditions version?
A
The design version applies to systems that aren't yet built — you're working from drawings, not measurements. The existing conditions version requires actual gauges on an installed system. In this problem, we're given pipe length, elevation, and flow rate — no gauges — so it's clearly the design equation. OH 76 · March 4, 2024
Q
The NPSHA formula shown in the video doesn't match the terms in the reference handbook — how do they reconcile? Is one of them wrong?
A
Neither is wrong — the terms map one-to-one, they're just presented differently. HP in the handbook corresponds to the atmospheric head term in the older formula (≈ 34 ft). The key rule: all terms must be in feet of head. If you start with pressure in psi, convert by dividing by the specific weight of water (or multiply by 2.31 ft/psi). The older formula had some terms combined differently, but the physics is identical. Stick with the handbook version — it's cleaner and exam-aligned.
OH 71 · December 18, 2023
Q
For NPSHA problems in general, should I focus exclusively on what's happening upstream of the pump and ignore everything else?
A
Yes — NPSHA is defined entirely on the suction side. Discharge piping details in the problem are window dressing designed to distract you. The key relationship is that NPSHA must exceed NPSHR (the required value from the manufacturer) to avoid cavitation. NPSHR is given to you; NPSHA is what you calculate. Also, don't worry about the location of temperature sensors — the pump doesn't meaningfully change water temperature, so the fluid on both sides is essentially the same temperature.
OH 63 · July 17, 2023
Q
The video solution shows an extra v²/2g term that the reference handbook design equation doesn't include. What's going on, and how do I know which equation to apply?
A
That v²/2g term belongs to the existing conditions equation, not the design equation. Here's why: in the design equation, friction losses (HF via Darcy-Weisbach) already account for velocity effects in the pipe. In the existing conditions equation, you're reading a static pressure gauge — gauges only capture static pressure, not velocity pressure — so you have to add v²/2g separately. Think of it as a clean swap: design equation gives up HZ and HF; existing conditions replaces them with HS (gauge reading) and v²/2g (velocity correction). If you see that extra term in the video, it's because an older formula version was used.

OH 21 · September 10, 2021
Q
Is it acceptable to use the handbook's NPSHA formula instead of the one shown in the video? And when exactly would I ever use the existing conditions version?
A
Yes — the handbook formula is correct and preferred. Use the design version when you're designing or sizing a system (no gauges, you're predicting performance from given data). Use the existing conditions version when the system is already built and gauges are physically present on the suction side. In the existing conditions version, HS (the gauge reading) replaces both HZ and HF from the design equation — because the gauge at the pump inlet already reflects the combined effect of elevation and friction losses. You add v²/2g separately because pressure gauges read static pressure only, not total pressure.
