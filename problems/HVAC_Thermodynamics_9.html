HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 9
Problem Statement
Atmospheric air at 77°F undergoes isentropic compression to 100 psia and 470°F. How much work is done to the system during the process?
Answer Choices
A.0.5 Btu/lb
B.70 Btu/lb ✓
C.90 Btu/lb
D.50,000 Btu/lb
Solution
Quick Overview:
  1. Recognize this as a closed system — the problem says "air undergoes compression," not "a compressor compresses air," so no mass crosses the boundary. Use the closed-system isentropic work formula.
  2. Organize the two states: State 1 — P₁ = 14.7 psia (atmospheric), T₁ = 77°F; State 2 — P₂ = 100 psia, T₂ = 470°F.
  3. Pull the isentropic work formula from the NCEES Handbook (search "isentropic process"): w = R(T₂ − T₁) / (1 − k)
  4. Look up values: Rair = 53.35 ft·lbf / (lbm·°R) and k = 1.4 for air (ratio of specific heats, valid at these conditions).
  5. Compute: ΔT = 470 − 77 = 393°F = 393°R (temperature differences are equal in °F and °R, so no conversion needed). Plug in to get −52,416 ft·lbf/lbm.
  6. Convert to Btu using 1 Btu = 778 ft·lbf: −52,416 / 778 ≈ −67 Btu/lbm. The magnitude matches Answer B (70 Btu/lb); the negative sign simply confirms work is done to the system.
Full Solution:

Step 1 — Identify the system type. The problem states that air "undergoes isentropic compression" — there is no mention of a compressor or any mass flow rate. This language implies a closed system (think: a piston-cylinder device). For a closed system, no mass crosses the boundary; only work does. This distinction matters because the NCEES Handbook lists separate work formulas for open and closed systems, and choosing the wrong one leads directly to Answer C (the open-system result).

Step 2 — Identify known states.

State 1: P₁ = 14.7 psia (atmospheric), T₁ = 77°F
State 2: P₂ = 100 psia, T₂ = 470°F
Process: isentropic (s = constant)

Step 3 — Select the correct formula. The NCEES Handbook (search "isentropic process") gives two equivalent expressions for specific work in a closed isentropic process:

w = (P₂v₂ − P₁v₁) / (1 − k)    [form 1, uses specific volumes]
w = R(T₂ − T₁) / (1 − k)          [form 2, uses temperatures only]

Both forms are equivalent. Starting from Form 1 and substituting the ideal gas relation Pv = RT → v = RT/P, the pressure terms cancel and Form 2 follows directly. Since we know both temperatures, Form 2 is the faster path.

Step 4 — Look up constants.
From the NCEES Handbook (gas properties table):
Rair = 53.35 ft·lbf / (lbm·°R)
kair = 1.4 (ratio of specific heats, CP/CV, valid for air up to ~1000°F)

Step 5 — Calculate work.

ΔT = T₂ − T₁ = 470 − 77 = 393°F = 393°R  (differences are equal in °F and °R)

w = [53.35 ft·lbf/(lbm·°R)] × 393°R ÷ (1 − 1.4)
w = 20,967 ft·lbf/lbm ÷ (−0.4)
w = −52,416 ft·lbf/lbm

Step 6 — Convert units and interpret the sign.

w = −52,416 ft·lbf/lbm ÷ 778 ft·lbf/Btu ≈ −67 Btu/lbm

The negative sign means work is done to the system (compression), which aligns with the question's framing. The question asks for the work done to the system, so we report the magnitude. The closest answer choice is B — 70 Btu/lb.

Handbook references: Isentropic process work equations; Ideal gas law; Gas properties table (R and k for air); Measurement relationships (1 Btu = 778 ft·lbf).

  • The NCEES Handbook's isentropic process section offers two work formulas: one using pressure and specific volume (P·v), and one using only the gas constant R and temperatures. Dan shows algebraically that substituting the ideal gas law (v = RT/P) into the P·v form causes the pressures to cancel, yielding the temperature-only form directly.
  • Rather than computing specific volumes, the video goes straight to w = R(T₂ − T₁) / (1 − k) — a shortcut that's valid whenever both temperatures are known, and faster on exam day.
  • The specific gas constant for air, R = 53.35 ft·lbf/(lbm·°R), is pulled from the NCEES Handbook and multiplied by the 393° temperature difference (no Rankine conversion needed for a delta). With k = 1.4, the result is −52,416 ft·lbf/lbm.
  • The unit conversion from ft·lbf/lbm to Btu/lbm (dividing by 778) is explicitly shown, yielding approximately −67 Btu/lbm — close enough to Answer B (70 Btu/lb) once rounding is accounted for.
  • The negative sign is highlighted as physically meaningful: it confirms that work is done on the system (compression takes work in), and the question frames the answer as "work done to the system," so the sign is consistent with choosing Answer B.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
  • Language determines system type. "Air undergoes compression" implies a closed system; "a compressor processes air" implies an open system. These use different work formulas, and mixing them up leads directly to the wrong answer (Choice C).
  • Isentropic work simplifies nicely for ideal gases. When both temperatures are known, the R·ΔT form is faster than computing specific volumes — the two approaches are mathematically identical, so choose the one that requires fewer lookup steps.
  • Temperature differences don't need unit conversion. Since 1°F and 1°R are the same size, ΔT in °F equals ΔT in °R. You only need to convert absolute temperatures (e.g., °F to °R), not differences.
  • k = 1.4 is a reliable constant for air across typical exam conditions. While k technically varies with temperature, it changes slowly and stays near 1.4 well into the hundreds of degrees Fahrenheit — well within the range of most PE exam problems.

OH 32 · March 7th, 2022
Q: Can you break down the unit conversion from ft·lbf/(lbm·°R) to Btu/lbm in this problem?
Dan's response: The key conversion lives in the NCEES Handbook under Measurement Relationships: 1 Btu = 778 ft·lbf. Once you've computed work in ft·lbf/lbm using the isentropic formula, divide by 778 to convert to Btu/lbm. Units-wise: the ft·lbf cancels, the °R cancels against the temperature difference, and you're left with Btu/lbm. Writing out every unit step by step is strongly recommended when you're learning this — it's easy to lose track of what's in the numerator versus the denominator when multiple terms are involved.
OH 53 · February 27th, 2023
Q: The handbook has an extra k in the work equation under open systems. Which formula is correct — and is k = 1.4 valid here even though conditions change?
Dan's response: The problem says there is no mass crossing the boundary, so we're dealing with a closed system — use the closed-system isentropic formula, not the open-system one. On k = 1.4: technically k varies with temperature, but it changes very slowly. According to engineering data, k for air stays close to 1.4 across a wide range of temperatures and doesn't deviate meaningfully until you get well above 1000°F. At 470°F, you're well within safe territory. This is exactly the kind of judgment call you'll sharpen through practice problems — knowing when an assumption holds and when you need to be more careful.
OH 63 · July 17th, 2023
Q: I used the formula W = CP·ΔT from the handbook (Section 4.5.3) and got 90 Btu/lb — Answer C. Why doesn't that formula work here?
Dan's response: That formula is for a constant-pressure process, not an isentropic one. The problem explicitly states "isentropic compression," so you need to search the handbook for "isentropic process" and use those formulas. The answer you get from the isentropic formula is 67 Btu/lb (Answer B), which is less than 90 Btu/lb — that's expected because an isentropic process is ideal (reversible), meaning it requires less work than a real process. If a problem mentions isentropic, that's your cue to locate the isentropic work equation. The process qualifier in the problem statement is a key word, every time.
OH 78 · April 1st, 2024
Q: I assumed this was an open system (isentropic, no PE or KE change). How do you know it's closed?
Dan's response: The absence of a named flow device is the tell. "Air undergoes isentropic compression" is passive — there is no compressor mentioned, no mass flow rate given, nothing that requires an open system. In an open system, you would need a mass flow rate to solve the problem, which isn't provided. So we treat it as a fixed mass of air being compressed (like a piston-cylinder device) — a closed system. For a closed system, the change in potential energy and kinetic energy is zero, which simplifies things considerably. Takeaway: if a specific flow device like a compressor is named, go open system. If it's not, default to closed system.

That said, I'd encourage you to also know the R·ΔT formula well — it's what the handbook points you toward for isentropic work, and on exam day you want to recognize it immediately. Having multiple routes to the answer builds flexibility, and flexibility is a real asset under time pressure.
OH 92 · November 25th, 2024
Q: I couldn't find the rules of thumb (like the 1.08 rule) in the handbook — do we need to memorize them?
Dan's response: They are in the handbook — search "heat gain calculations." The handbook writes them slightly differently (using 1.1 instead of 1.08, which is a rounding difference that won't affect your answer). You will almost certainly memorize these values naturally just from solving practice problems repeatedly — you won't need to force it. If you have a volume flow rate in CFM and a delta T, use Qs = 1.1 × CFM × ΔT for sensible heat. For latent, there's a similar form. For water loop problems, there's 500 × GPM × ΔT. All of these ultimately come from q = m·CP·ΔT — the rules of thumb just bake in the density and specific heat for standard conditions.

OH 115 · March 2nd, 2026
Q: I got the same answer. But I was hesitant about k = 1.4 — the air changes both pressure and temperature here. Is k = 1.4 still safe to use?
Dan's response: Yes, k = 1.4 is safe to use here. The k value for air does vary with temperature, but it changes very gradually — it essentially walks down a long, gentle slope. It doesn't fall off a cliff at any particular temperature. For most PE exam problems, you will use k = 1.4 and it will be fine. If you ever need to compute k from first principles, you'll do it as CP/CV, and there are problems in the module that work through that. But unless the problem is specifically asking you to find k, just use 1.4 and move on confidently.
