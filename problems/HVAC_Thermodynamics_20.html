HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 20
A heat pump with a 3 kW compressor has a heating capacity of 50,000 Btu/hr and a cooling capacity of 40,000 Btu/hr. What is the COP when the unit is operated in heating mode?
Answer Choices
✓ Answer: C
A. 1.0
B. 3.9
C. 4.9
D. 5.0
Quick Overview
  1. In heating mode, a heat pump delivers heat to the space — so the relevant output is the heating capacity, QH = 50,000 Btu/hr.
  2. The compressor work input is given in kW. Convert it to Btu/hr using 1 kW = 3,412.14 Btu/hr: W = 3 × 3,412.14 = 10,236.4 Btu/hr.
  3. Apply the COP formula for heating: COPH = QH / Win.
  4. Divide: 50,000 ÷ 10,236.4 ≈ 4.88, which rounds to 4.9 → Choice C.
  5. Verify with the energy balance: QH = QL + W → 40,000 + 10,236 = 50,236 ≈ 50,000 ✓
  6. Watch the trap: Choice B (3.9) is the cooling COP — don't mix up QH and QL.
Key Takeaways
  • A heat pump can deliver more heat energy than the electrical energy it consumes — that's why COPH is greater than 1.0.
  • Heating COP always uses the heat delivered as the output, not the heat absorbed from outside — these are easy to confuse under exam pressure.
  • The cooling COP (3.9) and heating COP (4.9) differ by exactly 1 — this is a fundamental thermodynamic identity: COPH = COPC + 1.
  • Always convert compressor work to the same unit system as the capacity before dividing — a unit mismatch is the most common source of error here.

Full Solution

This problem tests the definition of Coefficient of Performance (COP) for a heat pump operating in heating mode. The key is to correctly identify which heat transfer quantity — QH or QL — belongs in the numerator, and to handle the unit conversion for the compressor work.

Step 1 — Identify the given values:

  • Compressor power: Win = 3 kW
  • Heating capacity: QH = 50,000 Btu/hr
  • Cooling capacity: QL = 40,000 Btu/hr

Step 2 — Convert compressor work to Btu/hr:

The capacities are in Btu/hr, so convert Win to match:

W_in = 3 kW × 3,412.14 Btu/hr per kW = 10,236.4 Btu/hr

Step 3 — Apply the COP formula for heating mode:

In heating mode, the heat pump delivers QH to the conditioned space. The COP is the ratio of useful heat output to work input:

COP_H = Q_H / W_in COP_H = 50,000 / 10,236.4 COP_H ≈ 4.88 → rounds to 4.9 ✓ (Choice C)

Step 4 — Verify with the energy balance:

For a heat pump: QH = QL + Win. Check:

Q_L + W_in = 40,000 + 10,236 = 50,236 Btu/hr ≈ 50,000 ✓

The slight difference (236 Btu/hr) is due to rounding in the kW conversion factor. The energy balance confirms we have the right numbers.

Why the distractors are wrong:

  • Choice A (1.0): COP = 1 would mean all input work becomes heat — that ignores the heat absorbed from outdoors. No real heat pump works this way.
  • Choice B (3.9): This is COPcooling = QL/W = 40,000/10,236 ≈ 3.91. A classic trap — it uses the wrong capacity for the given operating mode.
  • Choice D (5.0): Results from rounding Win too aggressively — e.g., using 3 kW × 3,000 Btu/hr = 9,000 Btu/hr → 50,000/10,000 = 5.0. The correct conversion factor is 3,412.14, not 3,000.

Note also the elegant identity: COPH = COPC + 1. Here: 3.9 + 1 = 4.9 ✓ — a quick sanity check you can use on the exam.

Video Synthesis
  • Heat pump COP in heating mode = QH / Win; always use the heat delivered, not absorbed.
  • Unit conversion is essential: 1 kW = 3,412.14 Btu/hr — memorize this for the exam.
  • The energy balance QH = QL + W is your best verification tool and catches unit errors instantly.
  • COPH = COPC + 1 is a derived identity — use it as a quick cross-check when both capacities are given.
  • Choice B (3.9) is the cooling COP — the exam will offer it as a distractor whenever heating mode is specified.
  • Heat pumps are thermodynamically "efficient" precisely because COP > 1; they move heat rather than generate it.
