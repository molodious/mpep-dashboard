Full Solution: In a counterflow heat exchanger, the cold fluid increases in temperature from 50 to 100 degrees, and the warm fluid is cooled from 180 to 145 degrees. What is the logarithmic mean temperature difference? So you can look in the reference handbook for a log mean temperature difference, and you can also search for L-M-T-D. There's an equation in there, but if you've already done the previous chapter on heat transfer, and you've seen my videos on L-M-T-D, you may know that I'm not a huge fan of the equation the way it's presented in the reference handbook. I think by putting the individual temperatures in there and trying to have two separate equations, one for counterflow, and one for parallel flow, just creates more opportunities for confusion. I think it's more important to understand the concept of counterflow versus parallel flow, and to define the differences in temperatures, what I'll call delta-T-A and delta-T-B. This aligns better with the old version of doing it in the murm, and I think it allows you to memorize the formula for L-M-T-D. It's very simple, and then by understanding how these two heat exchangers work, we can define those two temperature differences, and then the quantitative part of the problem actually plugging in and doing the algebra is the same once you've defined those two temperature differences. So let's talk about what we mean by that in this particular situation. Here we have a counterflow heat exchanger, which means that the two fluids are moving in opposite direction, so on the left side we have the temperature, on the vertical axis, and then on the horizontal axis we have the length of the physical device, which is assuming that it's a single pass counterflow heat exchanger. So the cold fluid is going from 50 to 100, that might look something like this, and then the warm fluid is going from 180 to 145. Now if it was a parallel flow heat exchanger, we would draw that like this, and we'd say that both streams have an arrow to the right. But in fact, we're going to keep the first flow to the right for the cold stream, but for the hot stream, we're going to have a go in the other direction. So this stream is going to the left from 180 to 145. And that's what makes it a counterflow heat exchanger, because they're running counter to each other as opposed to running in parallel. Hopefully that makes sense. If you can remember that, you've stripped all of the complexity out of LMT decalculation, and you're not going to have any issues after that, because all we've got to do is define delta TA on the left side of this thing as the difference between these two temperatures, and delta TB as the difference between these two. So let's quantify those two temperatures. Delta TA is going to be 145 minus 50, which is 95, and delta TB is going to be 180 minus 100, which is 80 degrees. And then from there, we finish with the log-mean temperature difference equation, which says that delta TA minus delta TB over the natural log of the ratio of delta TA to delta TB. And now that difference is 95 minus 80 degrees F over the natural log of 95 over 80. And if you put all that into your calculator, you will get 87.3 degrees. And the best answer choice is B. You might be wondering, hey, what if I defined it the other way? The natural log of the number less than 1 turns out to be negative. And the answer algebraicly comes out to be exactly the same.
