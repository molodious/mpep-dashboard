Full Solution: Fluids 19, a makeup pump that cycles on and off intermittently as required has a service factor of 1.25 and provides a water horsepower of 15 horsepower. The pump efficiency is 84 percent. The pump is driven by electric motor with an efficiency of 93 percent. What is the smallest motor that could be used? So let's be reminded about what a typical pump set looks like. We're talking about an AC motor which is driving a pump. The pump produces hydraulic horsepower or water horsepower. But in order to do that, it needs to be driven by a rotating shaft which is brake horsepower or BHP. So the motor produces brake horsepower by turning a shaft. How does it do that while it uses electrical energy? So that's KW or electrical power. And in the process of turning mechanical energy BHP into hydraulic horsepower, WHP, the pump has some efficiency, which we call the efficiency of the pump. And the motor has some efficiency in changing electrical power into mechanical power. And we call that the efficiency of the motor. And if we want to express those all together, we can say the overall efficiency and go all the way from electricity to hydraulic horsepower. And we can abstract away the fact that this is a pump set of a motor and a pump. Sometimes we need to unpack this and go a bit deeper and understand this on a detailed level and sometimes it's useful to abstract it away. This is a case where we want to make the distinction. We've been given separate efficiencies here. We have the pump efficiency 84% and the motor efficiency is 93. But the question is what's the smallest motor that could be used? And when it comes to sizing a motor, the motor is described based on the output power that it can produce. The mechanical power, the BHP. So this question is really asking, what is the size of the motor that can produce the required BHP that will drive this pump based on the situation that we have? So we know the efficiency of the pump can be expressed as WHP over BHP, which can be rearranged for BHP equals WHP over efficiency. And we know that the pump provides a water horsepower of 15, so we know WHP. Now we also know that the pump set has a service factor of 1.25, or we're going to incorporate that later. For now, we know the required water horsepower delivered by the pump is 15, and the pump efficiency is 84%. So the BHP then is 17.9. So at this point, you may be tempted to choose answer choice C and be done with it. And you would be right to ignore the motor efficiency. That's extra information that we don't need. But we do have to account for this service factor. A service factor is a bit like overclocking your CPU in your computer. It's saying the pump set can run a little bit harder, but not all the time. It can do 25% more as long as it's running intermittently. For continuous use, you would typically see a service factor of 1, meaning you can't run it any harder if it's going to be running all the time. So then the question becomes looking at the answer choices with the 15 horsepower motor, being enough to produce a BHP of 17.9 on an intermittent basis if it has a service factor of 1.25. So we can multiply or examine case B, 15 horsepower times the service factor of 1.25, and that works out to 18.75. And 18.75 is greater than 17.9. So that means the 15 horsepower motor is actually good enough. So the efficiency of the pump hurts because it means you need more power to produce 15 horsepower of hydraulic horsepower, but there's inefficiency. So you actually need more brake horsepower. But then the fact that there's a service factor and it's intermittent use that helps and that allows us to choose the 15 horsepower motor anyway. And the motor efficiency 93% gets ignored. Since we're not working out the electrical power required to run the pump set, we're working out the size of the motor, which is based on BHP.
