HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 1
Problem Statement
A 10hp pump with 90% efficiency transports water to an elevation of 200ft above the pump. What volume of water is transported in a 1 hour period?
Answer Choices
A.300 gallons
B.1,400 gallons
C.10,700 gallons ✓
D.11,900 gallons
Solution
Answer: C
Quick Overview: 1. Identify given information: 10 hp pump, 90% efficiency, water pumped to 200 ft elevation, find volume in 1 hour. 2. Account for pump efficiency: The useful (water) power is the brake horsepower times efficiency → Ẇ_water = 10 hp × 0.90 = 9 hp. 3. Convert power to consistent units: 9 hp × 550 ft·lbf/s per hp = 4,950 ft·lbf/s. 4. Apply the work-energy principle: Equate the pump work to gravitational potential energy. Power = Work / Time, and Work = m·g·h. So Ẇ = ṁ·g·h, which gives the mass flow rate ṁ = Ẇ / (g·h). 5. Solve for mass flow rate and convert to volume: ṁ = 4,950 ft·lbf/s ÷ (200 ft) = 24.75 lbm/s. Over one hour: m = 24.75 × 3,600 = 89,100 lbm. Convert to gallons using water density (8.34 lbm/gal): V = 89,100 / 8.34 ≈ 10,680 gallons. 6. Select closest answer: 10,680 gallons ≈ 10,700 gallons → Answer C. Full Solution: This is the very first problem in the Thermodynamics module. The solution video approaches it from a foundational energy perspective rather than using a specialized pump formula. The key idea is that power equals work over time (Ẇ = W/t). The work done by the pump goes entirely into raising the water's gravitational potential energy, so we can write: Ẇ = m·g·h / t where m is the mass of water, g is gravitational acceleration (32.2 ft/s²), h is the elevation (200 ft), and t is time. First, account for efficiency. The pump input is 10 hp, but only 90% of that is delivered to the water: Ẇ_water = 10 × 0.90 = 9 hp Convert to ft·lbf/s: 9 hp × 550 ft·lbf/s/hp = 4,950 ft·lbf/s. Now solve for the mass flow rate. Since Ẇ = ṁ·g·h and using g/gc = 1 lbf/lbm in imperial units: ṁ = 4,950 / 200 = 24.75 lbm/s Over one hour (3,600 seconds): m = 24.75 × 3,600 = 89,100 lbm Convert mass to volume using water's density (≈ 8.34 lbm/gal): V = 89,100 / 8.34 ≈ 10,680 gallons This rounds to approximately 10,700 gallons → Answer C. Alternative approach (Water Horsepower formula): You can also use WHP = Q × H / 3960, where Q is in GPM and H is in feet. Setting WHP = BHP × η = 9 hp: Q = 9 × 3960 / 200 = 178.2 GPM Over one hour: V = 178.2 × 60 = 10,692 gallons ≈ 10,700 gallons. Both approaches yield the same answer. The water horsepower formula is faster but comes from the Fluids section. The energy-based approach is more fundamental and is the method used in the solution video.
📹 Video Solution Synthesis:

• The solution video starts from the foundational definition: Power = Work / Time (Ẇ = W/t).

• The work-energy principle is applied: the pump's work equals the gravitational potential energy gained by the water (PE = m·g·h).

• Pump efficiency is applied first to get the useful water power: 10 hp × 0.90 = 9 hp, then converted to 4,950 ft·lbf/s.

• The mass flow rate is solved algebraically, then multiplied by time (3,600 s) to get total mass, then divided by water density (8.34 lbm/gal) to get volume.

• The final answer is approximately 10,680 gallons in one hour, matching answer choice C (10,700 gallons).

• This foundational approach builds problem-solving flexibility rather than relying on a single memorized formula.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
• Know multiple solution paths — the energy-based approach (Power = Work/Time with PE = mgh) and the water horsepower formula (WHP = Q·H/3960) both work, and knowing both gives you flexibility on the exam.

• Watch your units relentlessly — the water horsepower formula gives GPM, not gallons per hour. Forgetting to multiply by 60 gives an answer off by a factor of 60, a trap multiple students have fallen into.

• Practicing the "harder" or less familiar approach during study builds deeper understanding and connects concepts across modules, even if you'd use the faster formula on exam day.

• This is the first problem of the program — it sets the tone for being flexible, careful with units, and willing to try things multiple ways.

OH 67 · September 11, 2023
Q: I solved this using the pump power equation (water horsepower = Q × H / 3960) and I'm getting a drastically different answer — 178 instead of ~10,680. What's going wrong?
Dan's response: The answer of 178 is actually gallons per minute, not gallons per hour. The two numbers are related by a factor of 60 (minutes per hour). The water horsepower formula naturally gives Q in GPM, so you need to multiply by 60 to answer the question which asks for volume in a 1-hour period: 178 × 60 = 10,680 gallons. This is a classic unit-conversion trap. "The takeaway is to just be really careful answering the question that's being asked." Dan strongly recommends tracking units at every step: "The one minute you take your eye off the ball is going to be the time that you're off by a factor of a large number." Also try solving it both ways — the fluids approach (WHP formula) and the foundational potential energy approach — so you're comfortable with either method.
