HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 1
Problem Statement
A 10hp pump with 90% efficiency transports water to an elevation of 200ft above the pump. What volume of water is transported in a 1 hour period?
Answer Choices
A.300 gallons
B.1,400 gallons
C.10,700 gallons ✓
D.11,900 gallons
Quick Overview
1. Identify that this is a pump power / flow rate problem using the relationship between power, flow rate, and head.

2. Determine the useful (water) horsepower by applying the pump efficiency: WHP = 10 hp × 0.90 = 9 hp.

3. Recall the pump power formula: WHP = Q × γ × h, where Q is flow rate, γ is the specific weight of water, and h is the elevation head.

4. Rearrange to solve for Q (flow rate) and convert units consistently — power in ft·lbf/s, specific weight in lbf/ft³, head in ft.

5. Convert the flow rate from ft³/s to ft³/hr, then to gallons using 1 ft³ = 7.481 gallons.

6. Verify the result is approximately 10,700 gallons, matching answer choice C.
Solution
Answer: C
This problem asks us to find the volume of water a pump can lift to a given elevation in one hour. The key relationship connects pump power, flow rate, specific weight of water, and elevation head. Step 1 — Find Water Horsepower (WHP): The pump is rated at 10 hp, but it is only 90% efficient. The useful power actually delivered to the water is: WHP = Input Power × Efficiency = 10 hp × 0.90 = 9 hp Step 2 — Convert to ft·lbf/s: 1 hp = 550 ft·lbf/s, so: WHP = 9 hp × 550 ft·lbf/s per hp = 4,950 ft·lbf/s Step 3 — Apply the Power-Flow-Head Relationship: Power = Q × γ × h Where: Q = volumetric flow rate (ft³/s) γ = specific weight of water = 62.4 lbf/ft³ h = elevation head = 200 ft Rearranging: Q = Power / (γ × h) Q = 4,950 / (62.4 × 200) Q = 4,950 / 12,480 Q = 0.3966 ft³/s Step 4 — Convert to Volume in 1 Hour: Volume per hour = 0.3966 ft³/s × 3,600 s/hr = 1,427.7 ft³/hr Step 5 — Convert to Gallons: 1 ft³ = 7.481 gallons Volume = 1,427.7 × 7.481 = 10,681 gallons ≈ 10,700 gallons The answer is C. Note: A common mistake is forgetting to apply efficiency (which would give 11,900 gallons — answer D) or confusing input power with water horsepower. Always multiply by efficiency first to get the actual power delivered to the water.

⭐ Key Takeaways:
• Always apply pump efficiency before calculating flow — the water receives less power than the pump's rated input.
• The relationship between power, flow rate, specific weight, and head is fundamental to pump problems on the PE exam.
• Watch out for unit conversions: hp to ft·lbf/s (×550), ft³ to gallons (×7.481), and seconds to hours (×3600).
