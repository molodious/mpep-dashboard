Alright, thermal 14. Why do we have to use the absolute temperature here? So this problem was about a Carnot heat pump and the COP for a Carnot heat pump. And the equation for the COP of a Carnot heat pump is based only on the temperatures. So it's TH over TH minus TH T L where TH is the temperature of the high temperature reservoir and T L is the low temperature reservoir. But it's absolutely crucial when you do these problems, especially the Carnot in particular because normally if we're dealing with COP of a refrigerator or a heat pump, we're going to be dealing with enthalpies. But when we do the Carnot COP, we're dealing with the assumption that what's happening is the refrigeration process or in this case it's a heat pump but either way that cycle is ideal is one way to say it or another way to say the same thing is that it's reversible. And when that's the case and only when that's the case, this is what's special about the Carnot cycle. It's kind of a fictitious ideal cycle. It can't exist in real life but we can think about it in theory. The COP is based on the temperatures. But we absolutely need to use absolute temperatures. And this isn't really an explanation exactly why that is but a way to help you see that that must be true is that if you were to do this calculation with Fahrenheit, you'd get some answer, right? But then if you were to go and do it with Celsius, you'd get a different answer. And how can that be? The COP for a Carnot heat pump can't be different just because we chose to use different temperatures. But if we use absolute temperatures, like Rankin, so we got to add 460 to all of these, or Kelvin if we're working in SI units, then we get the same answer. So when we do it in absolute temperature it aligns. And if you think about just comparing Fahrenheit and Rankin, the delta is going to be the same. So the denominator doesn't change whether you're in Fahrenheit or Rankin. Only the numerator changes. So the key idea is that these ratios within heat engines are proportional to absolute temperatures. They're not proportional to the temperature scales Fahrenheit and Celsius, which are, I won't say arbitrary, but they're based on a specific reference point, specifically water. You know, we defined in Celsius the freezing point of water zero and the boiling point is 100. And Fahrenheit, we do it as 32 and 212. And that's what we've chosen to do and that works. But when we need to do ratios, it's got to be a reference against absolute temperatures for a to work. And this comes up in chemistry. There's a few different occasions when this comes up. If you're nervous about that, you can just use absolute temperatures all the time. It'll never steer you wrong. But it's a little unusual. And I think once you've done enough problems, you'll kind of get clear on when you need to go absolute and when it's not required. Because most cases you don't need to, but this is the case where you do.
