HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 14
Problem Statement
A Carnot heat pump operates between a hot reservoir at 70°F and a cold reservoir at 20°F. What is the coefficient of performance?
Answer Choices
A.0.4
B.1.4
C.9.6
D.10.6
1 · Quick Overview
Topic Carnot Cycle — Coefficient of Performance (COP) for a Heat Pump
Key Concepts Carnot COP formula, absolute temperature (Rankine), hot/cold reservoir temperatures
Reference PE Reference Handbook — Carnot Cycle / Coefficient of Performance section
Difficulty Moderate
Given TH = 70°F  |  TL = 20°F  |  Carnot (ideal) heat pump
Find COPHP,Carnot
Critical Note Temperatures must be converted to absolute (Rankine) before substituting into the COP formula.
2 · Full Solution
Step 1 — Identify the Formula

Look up the Carnot Cycle in the PE Reference Handbook. Find the Coefficient of Performance formulas for heat pumps and refrigerators. The Carnot heat pump COP is given by:

COP_HP,Carnot = T_H / (T_H - T_L)

This formula applies specifically when the cycle is ideal (Carnot / reversible). For real cycles, COP is computed from enthalpy differences, not temperatures directly.

Step 2 — Convert Temperatures to Absolute (Rankine)

The Carnot COP formula requires absolute temperatures. In US customary units, convert °F → °R by adding 460:

T_H = 70°F + 460 = 530 R T_L = 20°F + 460 = 480 R

Note: The denominator (TH − TL) numerically equals the °F difference since the +460 offsets cancel. However, always use Rankine for TH in the numerator — this is the most common source of error on exam day.

Step 3 — Substitute and Solve

Plug the absolute temperatures into the formula:

COP_HP,Carnot = T_H / (T_H - T_L) = 530 R / (530 R - 480 R) = 530 R / 50 R = 10.6
✅ COPHP,Carnot = 10.6  —  Answer D
Step 4 — Sanity Check the Other Choices

Let’s see where the wrong answers come from:

  • A (0.4): Possibly (TH − TL) / TH in Rankine — that’s the Carnot efficiency, not COP. 50/530 ≈ 0.094; doesn’t match A exactly but confirms sign inversion is a trap.
  • B (1.4): Likely TH / (TH − TL) computed in °F: 70 / (70 − 20) = 70 / 50 = 1.4. Classic error — forgot to convert to absolute.
  • C (9.6): May arise from using Kelvin conversions incorrectly (e.g., mixing Kelvin and Rankine values).
  • D (10.6): ✅ Correct — Rankine temperatures used properly.
Physical Interpretation

A COP of 10.6 means the heat pump delivers 10.6 units of heat to the hot space for every 1 unit of work input. This seems large, but it makes sense for a Carnot (ideal) heat pump operating across only a 50°F temperature difference. Real heat pumps have much lower COPs due to irreversibilities.

4 · Key Takeaways
🌡️ Always use absolute temperature (Rankine) in the Carnot COP formula. Adding 460 to °F is required. If you use °F, you get a plausible-looking wrong answer (1.4 — choice B) — exactly what the exam wants.
📖 Know when to use temperature-based vs. enthalpy-based COP. The temperature-based Carnot COP formula only applies to ideal (Carnot/reversible) cycles. For real refrigeration/heat pump cycles, you use COP = Q / W, typically computed from enthalpy differences on a P-h or T-s diagram.
🔍 The denominator is the temperature difference — physically meaningful. (TH − TL) represents the “spread” between reservoirs. A small spread = high COP. A large spread = lower COP. This relationship helps you instantly sanity-check any answer.
⚠️ Common mistake: Using the Carnot efficiency formula instead of COP. Thermal efficiency = 1 − TL/TH, which gives the fraction of heat converted to work in a heat engine — not the COP of a heat pump.
💡 Exam tip — COP > 1 is expected for heat pumps. A COP below 1 is physically impossible for a heat pump. If your answer is less than 1, you’ve likely either inverted the formula or used the refrigerator COP version.
📐 Relationship between heat pump and refrigerator Carnot COP: COPHP = COPR + 1. For this problem: COPR = 480/50 = 9.6 (choice C), and COPHP = 9.6 + 1 = 10.6. Recognizing this relationship provides a fast cross-check on both answers.

OH 106 · July 7th, 2025
Q: Why do we have to use absolute temperature (Rankine) in the Carnot COP formula? Why can’t we just use °F?
Dan’s response:

This is a great conceptual question that gets at something fundamental about the Carnot cycle. When we deal with COP of a real refrigerator or heat pump, we’re working with enthalpies — and temperature units don’t matter directly. But for the Carnot COP, we’re using temperature ratios, and that’s the key difference.

Here’s a simple way to see why: If you calculate COP using °F, you get one answer. If you calculate the same problem using °C, you’d get a completely different answer. That’s physically impossible — the performance of a heat pump can’t depend on which temperature scale we chose to use. The answer has to be the same regardless of units.

When you use absolute temperatures (Rankine or Kelvin), you get the same answer either way. That consistency is the proof that absolute temperatures must be used. The Rankine and Kelvin scales are “true zero” scales — they don’t have arbitrary reference points like the freezing point of water.

One practical note: the denominator (TH − TL) is numerically the same in °F and Rankine because the +460 offsets cancel. So the trap is subtle — only the numerator TH changes when you convert. That’s exactly what makes answer B (1.4) such an appealing wrong answer: it’s what you get if you forget that TH in the numerator needs to be 530 R, not 70°F.

If you’re ever uncertain on the exam, just use absolute temperatures for all Carnot problems. It will never steer you wrong.

📌 Related Concept: COP of Refrigerator vs. Heat Pump

For the same Carnot cycle operating between TH = 530 R and TL = 480 R:

Refrigerator COP:
COP_R = T_L / (T_H - T_L) = 480 / 50 = 9.6

Heat Pump COP:
COP_HP = T_H / (T_H - T_L) = 530 / 50 = 10.6

Notice that COPHP = COPR + 1 always holds for Carnot cycles. This is because the heat pump delivers both the work input and the heat absorbed from the cold reservoir to the hot reservoir. Choice C (9.6) is the refrigerator COP for this exact setup — a deliberate distractor.
