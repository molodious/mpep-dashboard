Problem 14 — Carnot Heat Pump COP
Thermodynamics · Carnot Cycle · COP
A Carnot heat pump operates between 20°F and 70°F. What is the coefficient of performance?
A. 0.4
B. 1.4
C. 9.6
D. 10.6 ✓
Answer: D — 10.6
1. Identify the formula: COP (heat pump) = T_H / (T_H − T_L) 2. Convert temperatures to absolute scale (Rankine): add 460 to °F. T_H = 70 + 460 = 530 °R T_L = 20 + 460 = 480 °R 3. Compute the denominator: T_H − T_L = 530 − 480 = 50 °R 4. Compute COP: 530 / 50 = 10.6 5. Answer: D — 10.6
The Carnot heat pump COP is defined as: COP_HP = T_H / (T_H − T_L) The temperatures given are in Fahrenheit — but this formula requires ABSOLUTE temperatures. Any ratio of temperatures (as opposed to a difference) is only physically meaningful on an absolute scale. Convert to Rankine by adding 460: T_H = 70°F + 460 = 530 °R T_L = 20°F + 460 = 480 °R Now plug in: COP = 530 / (530 − 480) = 530 / 50 = 10.6 ✓ — — — Why the wrong answers are wrong — — — A (0.4): Uses T_L / T_H in Fahrenheit = 20 / 70 ≈ 0.286 ≈ 0.4 — this is neither the correct formula nor the correct temperature scale. B (1.4): Uses the correct formula but with raw Fahrenheit: 70 / (70 − 20) = 70 / 50 = 1.4. The formula structure is right, but without converting to absolute temperature the ratio is meaningless. The denominator (a delta) is the same in Fahrenheit or Rankine, but the numerator — a raw temperature — is not. C (9.6): Converts Fahrenheit to Celsius first (70°F ≈ 21.1°C, 20°F ≈ −6.67°C), then applies the formula in Celsius without converting to Kelvin: (21.1 + 273) / (21.1 − (−6.67)) = 294.1 / 27.77 ≈ 9.6. Closer, but Celsius is not an absolute scale — you must use Kelvin (or Rankine). Always convert to absolute temperatures before applying Carnot formulas.

🎓 Key Insight from OH 106 (July 7, 2025)

Dan walks through exactly why absolute temperatures are non-negotiable here. The tell is consistency: plug in Fahrenheit and you get 1.4; convert those same temperatures to Celsius and apply the formula — you get 9.6. A physical quantity like COP can't have two different values depending on which non-absolute scale you pick. That contradiction is proof the formula only works with absolute temperatures (Rankine or Kelvin).

The denominator T_H − T_L is a difference, so it's the same in Fahrenheit and Rankine (both 50°). The numerator T_H is a raw temperature, so it changes (70°F vs 530°R). That's exactly where Fahrenheit breaks the calculation.

Dan's blanket advice: just always use absolute temperatures on the PE exam — Rankine for imperial problems, Kelvin for SI. It will never steer you wrong, and it sidesteps this trap entirely.
Key Takeaways
  • Carnot COP formulas involve temperature ratios, which are only physically meaningful on an absolute scale — always convert to Rankine (imperial) or Kelvin (SI) before plugging in.
  • Temperature differences are the same in Fahrenheit and Rankine, but raw temperature values are not — that asymmetry is exactly what breaks the formula when you forget to convert.
  • A quick sanity check: if the same formula gives different answers in Fahrenheit vs. Celsius, that's a sign you're working off a non-absolute scale and must convert.
  • When in doubt on the exam, always use absolute temperatures — it will never give you a wrong answer, and it protects you from this class of error.
