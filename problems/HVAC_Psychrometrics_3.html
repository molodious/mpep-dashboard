Problem
HVAC — Psychrometrics — Problem 3
A 12 ft × 15 ft room with a 9 ft ceiling has a relative humidity of 25% and a dry bulb temperature of 70°F. A humidifier is turned on and run continuously adding 0.04 gal of water per hour to the room. Assuming the additional moisture content has no impact on the dry bulb temperature and the room is sealed, how long will it take to achieve 50% relative humidity?
Answer Choices
A 0.75 hrs
B 1 hr
C 1.5 hrs  ✓
D 2.75 hrs
Answer: C — 1.5 hrs
Quick Overview
  1. Find the volume of the room: 12 × 15 × 9 = 1,620 ft³
  2. Look up psychrometric properties at 70°F DB for both states using the psychrometric chart or tables — read at 25% RH (State 1) and 50% RH (State 2).
  3. Determine humidity ratios (ω) at both states: ω₁ ≈ 0.0039 lbw/lbda at 25% RH; ω₂ ≈ 0.0078 lbw/lbda at 50% RH.
  4. Calculate the mass of dry air in the room using specific volume: mda = V / v ≈ 1620 / 13.5 ≈ 120 lbda.
  5. Find the total mass of water needed: mda × (ω₂ − ω₁) = 120 × (0.0078 − 0.0039) ≈ 0.468 lb water.
  6. Convert the humidifier rate to lb/hr and divide to get time: 0.04 gal/hr × 8.34 lb/gal = 0.334 lb/hr; time = 0.468 / 0.334 ≈ 1.4 hrs → Answer: (C) 1.5 hrs.
📝 Key Insight: This is a fixed-volume humidification problem — not a coil process. The room is sealed, so the mass of dry air is constant. You are simply adding water vapor to the same air mass. Because the dry air mass doesn't change, you can use the specific volume from either State 1 or State 2 and get the same result.

Full Solution

This problem involves humidifying a sealed, fixed-volume room. A humidifier adds water vapor at a constant rate, and we need to find how long it takes to raise the relative humidity from 25% to 50% while the dry bulb temperature stays constant at 70°F.

Step 1 — Room Volume

V = 12 ft × 15 ft × 9 ft = 1,620 ft³

Step 2 — Psychrometric Properties at 70°F DB

Using the psychrometric chart at 70°F dry-bulb, read the humidity ratio at each relative humidity condition. The specific volume at State 1 (or State 2 — either works for a fixed-volume problem) is approximately:

State 1 (25% RH): ω₁ ≈ 0.0039 lbw/lbda, v ≈ 13.5 ft³/lbda State 2 (50% RH): ω₂ ≈ 0.0078 lbw/lbda

Step 3 — Mass of Dry Air in the Room

The specific volume tells us how many cubic feet of moist air are occupied per pound of dry air. Since the room is sealed and fixed-volume, this mass of dry air is constant throughout the process:

m_da = V / v = 1,620 ft³ / 13.5 ft³/lbda ≈ 120.0 lbda

Step 4 — Mass of Water Vapor Needed

The humidity ratio is the mass of water vapor per pound of dry air. To raise ω from State 1 to State 2, the total additional water vapor required is:

Δω = ω₂ − ω₁ = 0.0078 − 0.0039 = 0.0039 lbw/lbda m_water = m_da × Δω = 120.0 × 0.0039 ≈ 0.468 lb water

Step 5 — Humidifier Mass Flow Rate

The humidifier adds 0.04 gal/hr of liquid water. Convert to lb/hr using the density of water (8.34 lb/gal):

ṁ_humidifier = 0.04 gal/hr × 8.34 lb/gal = 0.334 lb/hr

Step 6 — Time Required

t = m_water / ṁ_humidifier = 0.468 lb / 0.334 lb/hr ≈ 1.40 hrs

Rounding to the nearest answer choice: 1.5 hrs → Answer: (C)

Note: The values ω₁ and ω₂ are read from the psychrometric chart and may vary slightly depending on the chart used. The calculation consistently yields an answer between 1 hr and 1.5 hrs, confirming choice (C). Using v from State 2 instead of State 1 gives essentially the same mass of dry air and the same time.

  • Use the psychrometric chart at 70°F DB to read humidity ratios at 25% and 50% RH. The dry bulb temperature stays constant throughout, so both states lie on the same vertical line on the chart.
  • The specific volume at State 1 gives the mass of dry air — and since the room is sealed, this mass stays constant. No air enters or leaves; only water vapor is added.

  • The time calculation is straightforward: (total mass of water needed) ÷ (mass rate of water addition). Simple division once you have the right quantities in the right units.

  • Humidification of a sealed room means the mass of dry air is constant — only water vapor changes as the humidifier runs.
  • The psychrometric chart gives you everything you need: humidity ratios at both conditions and the specific volume for finding dry air mass.
  • Always convert units consistently — gallons to pounds using water's density (8.34 lb/gal) before applying any psychrometric formula.
  • For fixed-volume problems with no airflow, you can use specific volume properties from either the initial or final state — the mass of dry air is the same either way.

Q: For this humidifier problem, should I use the specific volume at State 1 or State 2 when finding the mass of dry air?

A: Either one works for a fixed-volume room.
