Problem Statement
A building has 8 oce oors and a basement chiller plant, with 15ft of vertical spacing per oor. The chiller plant houses a set of pumps distributing chilled water via a central core riser to air handling units located in the building core on each oor of the building. Each oor has one air handling unit designed for 100gpm and 8ft of head pressure drop for the coil and 4psi of pressure drop for the control valve. The chillers are piped in parallel and have a maximum pressure drop of 15ft of head in all modes of operation. The head loss for the piping at the maximum ow rate is 4ft per 100ft of pipe. What is the head pressure that must be added by the pump set? 105

Full Solution: A building has 8 office floors and a basement-chiller plant with 15 feet of vertical spacing per floor. The chiller plant houses a set of pumps distributing chilled water via a central core riser to air handling units located in the building core on each floor of the building. Each floor has one air handling unit designed for 100 GPM and 8 feet of head pressure drop for the coil and 4 PSI of pressure drop for the control valve. The chillers are piped in parallel and have a maximum pressure drop of 15 feet of head in all modes of operation. The head loss for the piping at the maximum floor rate is 4 feet per 100 feet of pipe. What is the head pressure that must be added by the pump set? So we're looking for how much pressure it needs to be added by a pump set that needs to serve this building, which has air handling on each floor and chillers in the basement and it has some height and there are some losses associated with pumping the water through that total length of pipe. So let's draw a picture just to frame this out. I'm not going to draw the whole building. I'm just going to draw the building core. So this represents a vertical slice out of the middle of the building. You can imagine if you've ever been in a high-rise building that there is a core and the elevators and a lot of the services are located in that central core. This represents that core and then out to the sides there would be desks and other usable space for the tenants, but the landlord oftentimes controls a lot of what happens inside the core. It's not always the case there are exceptions, but it's a typical design. So let's have that represent the entire eight floors plus the basement. So I'm going to call attention to the basement and also to the top floor. So this will represent the eighth floor. You can imagine one, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight all the way up and this is the top floor. In the basement we have the pump set. Now it could be multiple pumps. It probably is two or three or more pumps in parallel for redundancy or just to achieve the required flow rate. But I'm just going to draw this as one pump because if you have pumps in parallel, the delta P across the pumps is constant. So the head pressure provided by a single pump or pump set would have to be the same. So no sense in doing extra drawing there and this will be the return piping and then on the supply side. That pump is going to drive to chilled water through some number of chillers. We haven't actually been told how many chillers, but it did say chillers, so we know it's plural. And they're piped in parallel. And the reason that's important is because the delta P or the head loss through the chillers, regardless of there's two or three or more, is going to be the same. So we can have the supply header and this return header. And we're not adding more pressure loss, more head loss by adding more chillers because they're piped in parallel. We are adding more flow, but we're not being asked to find out what the flow rate is. We're just being asked to find out what the head pressure is that must be added by the pump set and having more chillers in parallel does not add more pressure loss. So continuing with the supply piping, this is now going to go up the building, up the riser. And then it's going to go through an HU on each floor. So I could draw an HU on floors one through seven as well, but it's really the top floor that we're most interested in. Not because it's any different from the other floors from the perspective of the air handler, the air handler is presumably the same. Same coil, same control valve. I'm drawing the control valve as though it's as big as the air handler which certainly isn't true, but just representing it as a source of pressure loss. So we have information about the head loss due to the HU and it's control valve.
