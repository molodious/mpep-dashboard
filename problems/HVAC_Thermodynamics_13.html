HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 13
Problem Statement
Steam at 350 psia with a quality of 0.9 undergoes an isentropic expansion to 120 psia. What is the change in enthalpy?
Answer Choices
A.40 Btu/lb
B.80 Btu/lb
C.90 Btu/lb
D.120 Btu/lb
Solution
✓ Answer: B — 80 Btu/lb

💡 Quick Overview

Process Type
Isentropic Expansion
Working Fluid
Steam (Wet Mixture)
Key Concept
Steam Tables + Quality
Difficulty
★ Intermediate

This problem involves a two-state steam analysis. At State 1 we have steam at known pressure and quality. The steam expands isentropically (constant entropy) to a lower pressure at State 2. We use the steam tables to find enthalpy at both states, then compute Δh. The key insight: isentropic means s₂ = s₁, which lets us find the quality at State 2 and therefore h₂.

📖 Full Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1 — Identify State 1 Conditions
Given: P₁ = 350 psia, quality χ₁ = 0.9 (wet steam mixture). Look up the Properties of Saturated Water and Steam table organized by pressure in the PE Reference Handbook at P = 350 psia.

From the steam table at 350 psia:
  • hf = 409.8 Btu/lb  (saturated liquid enthalpy)
  • hfg = 794.62 Btu/lb  (enthalpy of vaporization)
  • sf = 0.6059 Btu/(lb·°F)  (saturated liquid entropy)
  • sfg = 0.8914 Btu/(lb·°F)  (entropy of vaporization)
Step 2 — Calculate Enthalpy at State 1
For a wet mixture, enthalpy is found using quality:
h₁ = hf + χ · hfg
h₁ = 409.8 + (0.9)(794.62) = 409.8 + 715.16 ≈ 1125 Btu/lb
Step 3 — Calculate Entropy at State 1
Same approach with entropy:
s₁ = sf + χ · sfg
s₁ = 0.6059 + (0.9)(0.8914) = 0.6059 + 0.8023 ≈ 1.408 Btu/(lb·°F)
Step 4 — Apply Isentropic Condition
Since the expansion is isentropic (constant entropy), entropy is conserved:
s₂ = s₁ = 1.408 Btu/(lb·°F)
Now look up the steam table at P₂ = 120 psia to get sf and sfg at State 2:
  • sf,2 = 0.4919 Btu/(lb·°F)
  • sfg,2 = 1.0965 Btu/(lb·°F)
Step 5 — Find Quality at State 2
Rearrange the quality equation to solve for χ₂:
χ₂ = (s₂ − sf,2) / sfg,2
χ₂ = (1.408 − 0.4919) / 1.0965 = 0.9161 / 1.0965 ≈ 0.835
The steam is still a wet mixture at State 2 (quality between 0 and 1 — confirms we stay on the vapor dome).
Step 6 — Calculate Enthalpy at State 2
From the steam table at 120 psia:
  • hf,2 = 312.55 Btu/lb
  • hfg,2 = 878.2 Btu/lb
h₂ = hf,2 + χ₂ · hfg,2
h₂ = 312.55 + (0.835)(878.2) = 312.55 + 733.30 ≈ 1046 Btu/lb
Step 7 — Calculate Change in Enthalpy
Δh = h₁ − h₂ = 1125 − 1046 ≈ 79 Btu/lb ≈ 80 Btu/lb
This matches answer choice B.

⭐ Key Takeaways
  • Isentropic = constant entropy: s₂ = s₁ is the key constraint that connects the two states. Always write this down first.
  • Use the pressure-based steam table when you know pressures (not temperatures). It's the second of the two saturated water/steam tables in the Reference Handbook.
  • Quality formula works in both directions: forward (χ known → find h or s) and backward (s known → find χ). Know both forms.
  • Stay organized: This problem has many intermediate values. Label each: hf,1, hfg,1, sf,1, sfg,1, etc. One transcription error cascades into a wrong answer.
  • Mollier diagram as a check: An isentropic process appears as a vertical line on the h-s (Mollier) diagram. Use it to visually confirm your State 2 enthalpy is lower than State 1 (expansion drops enthalpy).
  • Common mistake — wrong table: Using the temperature-based steam table when pressure is given (and vice versa). The pressure table is the one to use here since P₁ and P₂ are given directly.
  • Exam tip: A wet mixture (0 < χ < 1) at both states means you stay inside the vapor dome. If χ₂ came out > 1, that would signal you'd crossed to superheated steam and would need a different table.

OH 115 · March 2nd, 2026
Q: For State 1 we only have pressure and quality — not two independent thermodynamic properties. Why are we able to look up everything we need from just pressure alone?
Dan's response: Excellent fundamental question. When quality is between 0 and 1, the steam is a saturated mixture — it sits inside the vapor dome on a T-s or P-h diagram. Inside the vapor dome, temperature and pressure are not independent: knowing one gives you the other. So pressure alone (or temperature alone) fully determines the saturation conditions: hf, hfg, sf, sfg, and specific volumes. What pressure (or temperature) does not tell you is where along the saturation curve the steam sits — that's what quality tells you. Quality is defined as χ = (s − sf) / sfg, and graphically represents how far you are between the saturated liquid line (χ=0) and the saturated vapor line (χ=1). Together, one of {T, P} plus quality fully specifies the state. This principle is identical for finding enthalpy and specific volume — it's the same formula structure every time. Steam tables for the win.
