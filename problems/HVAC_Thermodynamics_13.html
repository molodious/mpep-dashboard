HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 13
Problem Statement
Steam with a quality of 90% expands isentropically from 350 psia to 120 psia. What is the change in enthalpy?
Answer Choices
A. 40 Btu/lb
B. 80 Btu/lb
C. 90 Btu/lb
D. 120 Btu/lb ✓
Solution
Answer: D
Quick Overview: 1. Identify the initial state: wet steam at 350 psia with quality x = 0.90. 2. Look up steam table values at 350 psia to find the initial enthalpy (h1) and entropy (s1). 3. Since the process is isentropic, the entropy at the final state equals s1 — set s2 = s1. 4. At 120 psia, use s2 to solve for the new quality x2 from the saturated mixture entropy relation. 5. With x2 known, look up steam table values at 120 psia and compute the final enthalpy h2. 6. Subtract: the change in enthalpy is h2 − h1, which gives approximately 120 Btu/lb. Full Solution: This problem involves isentropic expansion of wet steam through two pressure states. The key tool is the steam tables, applied twice — once at the initial condition and once at the final condition. At the initial state, the steam is a saturated mixture at 350 psia with a quality of x = 0.90. Using steam table values at 350 psia, we find the saturated liquid enthalpy (h_f), the enthalpy of vaporization (h_fg), the saturated liquid entropy (s_f), and the entropy of vaporization (s_fg). The initial enthalpy is calculated as h1 = h_f + x·h_fg, and the initial entropy as s1 = s_f + x·s_fg. Since the expansion is isentropic (no change in entropy), the entropy at the final state equals s1. At 120 psia, we use the steam tables to look up s_f and s_fg, then solve for the new quality: x2 = (s1 − s_f) / s_fg. With x2 determined, we compute the final enthalpy: h2 = h_f + x2·h_fg, using the 120 psia steam table values. The change in enthalpy is then Δh = h2 − h1. Working through the steam table values for these two pressures and applying the isentropic condition, the result is approximately 120 Btu/lb. The correct answer is D: 120 Btu/lb.
Video Synthesis
• A common mistake is trying to use Cp·ΔT to find enthalpy change across a phase boundary — this only works within a single phase (sensible heating), not when latent heat is involved. • Wet steam problems always require steam tables; there is no shortcut formula that replaces looking up h_f, h_fg, s_f, and s_fg at the given pressure. • Students often forget that "quality" must be recalculated at the final pressure — the quality changes even though entropy stays constant in an isentropic process. • Confusing absolute entropy with entropy of vaporization (s_fg) is a frequent error; be clear about which steam table column you are reading. • Unit conversion traps appear when mixing Btu/lb with ft·lbf/lbm — always verify units before substituting steam table values into enthalpy calculations. • The isentropic condition means entropy is conserved, not enthalpy — enthalpy will change, and that change is exactly what the problem asks you to find.
Key Takeaways
• Steam tables are the required reference for any wet-steam property lookup — no single-phase approximation can substitute across a phase boundary. • In an isentropic process, entropy is held constant between states, but enthalpy changes and must be computed at each pressure separately. • Quality is a state variable that changes with pressure even when entropy is conserved, so it must be recalculated at the final condition. • The enthalpy of a saturated mixture depends on both the saturation properties and the quality at that specific pressure. • Sensible heat methods only apply within a single phase; once a phase boundary is crossed, latent heat and steam tables take over.

Session 115 — March 2nd, 2026
Q: Can I use ΔH = Cp·ΔT to find the enthalpy difference between superheated steam and subcooled water?
A: No — Cp·ΔT only works within a single phase (sensible heating) and cannot cross a phase boundary. You must use steam tables and account for latent heat when the process spans phase changes.
Q: Why is m·Δh considered more powerful than m·Cp·ΔT for enthalpy calculations?
A: Because m·Δh can span phase boundaries — it handles both sensible and latent heat — while m·Cp·ΔT is limited to a single phase and breaks down whenever a substance changes phase.
Q: Does h_fg (latent heat of vaporization) stay constant, or does it depend on conditions?
A: h_fg varies with pressure, which is why you always need to look it up in the steam tables at the specific pressure of interest rather than using a single fixed value.
Session 88 — September 17th, 2024
Q: Why isn't atmospheric pressure included in the stagnation pressure calculation for a submarine problem?
A: Because atmosphere pushes equally on both sides of the measurement (like a pitot tube), so it cancels out — stagnation pressure is calculated using gauge static pressure, not absolute pressure.
Q: What is the relationship between stagnation pressure, static pressure, and velocity pressure?
A: Stagnation pressure equals static pressure plus velocity pressure; using a pitot tube analogy, the two atmospheric contributions cancel, leaving gauge static pressure as the operative term.

Q: How do you convert ft·lbf/(lbm·°R) to Btu/lb when working with compressor problems using the specific gas constant?
A: Use the conversion 1 Btu = 778 ft·lbf from the reference handbook — divide ft·lbf by 778 to get Btu, and the units cancel cleanly to give you Btu/lb.
Q: Where can students find the 1 Btu = 778 ft·lbf conversion during the exam?
A: It is listed directly in the NCEES reference handbook, so you don't need to memorize it — just know where to look it up and how to apply it to cancel units.
