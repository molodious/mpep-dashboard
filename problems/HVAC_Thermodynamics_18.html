HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 18
Problem Statement
A refrigeration cycle using R-123 operates between 25 psia and 75 psia. Refrigerant enters the compressor with 20°F of superheat and leaves the condenser as a saturated liquid. What is the coefficient of performance?
Answer Choices
✓ Answer: B
A. 5.8
B. 6.7 ✓

Note: Only 2 choices available due to original OCR limitations.

Quick Overview
  1. Identify the four states of the refrigeration cycle on the P-h diagram for R-123.
  2. Find State 3: saturated liquid at the high side (75 psia) — read h₃ directly from the sat. liquid table.
  3. Find State 4: after the expansion valve — it's isenthalpic, so h₄ = h₃.
  4. Find State 1: at the low side (25 psia), traveling 20°F past the saturation curve into superheated vapor — read h₁ and s₁ from superheated R-123 tables.
  5. Find State 2: isentropic compression to 75 psia — match s₂ = s₁ at 75 psia and read h₂.
  6. Calculate COP = (h₁ − h₄) / (h₂ − h₁) and confirm the answer is approximately 6.7.
Key Takeaways
  • Real refrigeration cycles require enthalpies from property tables, not temperatures — Carnot COP formulas do not apply here.
  • The compressor work term (h₂ − h₁) is very small relative to the cooling effect, making the COP highly sensitive to small errors in reading h₂.
  • Being off by even 1 Btu/lb in h₂ can swing the final COP by ~10% — precision on the P-h diagram matters greatly on the exam.
  • The P-h (pressure-enthalpy) diagram is the fundamental visualization tool for real refrigeration cycles — practice navigating it fluently.

Full Solution
Step-by-step walk through the R-123 P-h diagram to find COP.
SETUP — Understand the Cycle This is a standard vapor-compression refrigeration cycle using R-123 as the working fluid. Because it's a REAL (non-Carnot) cycle, we cannot use temperature-based COP formulas. Instead, we must locate each state point on the R-123 pressure-enthalpy (P-h) diagram or property tables. The four state points are: • State 1 — Compressor inlet (low pressure, superheated vapor) • State 2 — Compressor outlet (high pressure, superheated vapor) • State 3 — Condenser outlet (high pressure, saturated liquid) • State 4 — Expansion valve outlet (low pressure, two-phase mixture) Operating pressures: P_low = 25 psia, P_high = 75 psia ───────────────────────────────────────────── STEP 1 — State 3: Condenser Outlet (Saturated Liquid at 75 psia) The problem states the refrigerant leaves the condenser as a saturated liquid. On the P-h diagram, this places State 3 exactly on the saturated liquid curve (left dome boundary) at 75 psia. From R-123 saturated properties at 75 psia: • Saturation temperature ≈ 84°F (read from sat. table) • h₃ ≈ 40 Btu/lb (saturated liquid enthalpy at 75 psia) h₃ ≈ 40 Btu/lb (sat. liquid @ 75 psia) ───────────────────────────────────────────── STEP 2 — State 4: Expansion Valve Outlet (Isenthalpic Process) The expansion valve is an isenthalpic (constant enthalpy) throttling device — no work, no heat transfer. Therefore: h₄ = h₃ ≈ 40 Btu/lb On the P-h diagram, State 4 lies at the same horizontal position as State 3, but drops down to the 25 psia line — well inside the two-phase dome, indicating a mixture of liquid and vapor. ───────────────────────────────────────────── STEP 3 — State 1: Compressor Inlet (Superheated Vapor at 25 psia) State 1 is at 25 psia, with 20°F of superheat above the saturation temperature at 25 psia. From R-123 sat. properties at 25 psia: • Saturation temperature T_sat ≈ 36°F Therefore, the actual temperature at State 1: T₁ = T_sat + 20°F = 36°F + 20°F = 56°F State 1 is in the superheated vapor region. From R-123 superheated vapor tables at 25 psia and 56°F: h₁ ≈ 107 Btu/lb s₁ ≈ 0.2210 Btu/(lb·°R) (entropy at state 1) On the P-h diagram: start at the saturation curve at 25 psia and move right (increasing enthalpy) by the amount corresponding to 20°F of superheat. ───────────────────────────────────────────── STEP 4 — State 2: Compressor Outlet (Isentropic Compression to 75 psia) The ideal compressor is isentropic (constant entropy, adiabatic). Therefore: s₂ = s₁ ≈ 0.2210 Btu/(lb·°R) On the P-h diagram: from State 1, follow a constant-entropy line upward and to the right until it intersects the 75 psia line. This is State 2. From R-123 superheated vapor tables at 75 psia, interpolating at s = 0.2210 Btu/(lb·°R): h₂ ≈ 117 Btu/lb (solution video value — see OH 44 note) ⚠️ Note: This is the most sensitive state point. Students in OH 44 found h₂ = 118 Btu/lb and got COP ≈ 5.99. Dan acknowledged the ambiguity and emphasized precision. ───────────────────────────────────────────── STEP 5 — Calculate COP (Refrigeration) For a refrigeration cycle, COP is the ratio of the desired output (cooling) to the required input (compressor work): COP_refrig = Q_L / W = (h₁ − h₄) / (h₂ − h₁) Substituting values: Numerator (cooling effect): h₁ − h₄ = 107 − 40 = 67 Btu/lb Denominator (compressor work): h₂ − h₁ = 117 − 107 = 10 Btu/lb COP = 67 / 10 = 6.7 ✓ ───────────────────────────────────────────── ANSWER: B — COP ≈ 6.7 Key observation: the compressor work (denominator) of only 10 Btu/lb is tiny compared to the cooling effect of 67 Btu/lb. A 1 Btu/lb error in h₂ changes the denominator by 10%, which directly changes the COP by ~10%. This is why Dan repeatedly emphasizes precision when reading h₂ from the P-h diagram.
Video Synthesis

  • The superheat condition means State 1 is to the right of the saturation dome at the low-side pressure; follow constant entropy upward to find State 2.
  • The expansion valve is isenthalpic: h₄ = h₃ — this is always true regardless of refrigerant.
  • The compressor work term (h₂ − h₁) is unusually small for R-123, amplifying any table-reading error into a large COP swing.
  • Mass flow rate cancels in the COP formula — you only need specific enthalpies (Btu/lb), not flow rates.

Office Hours 35 — May 2022
Q: Why can't we just use temperatures to calculate COP, like we did in earlier problems?
A: Earlier problems used the Carnot (ideal, theoretical) COP formula: COP = T_L / (T_H − T_L), which is only valid for an idealized cycle. This problem is a REAL cycle — the actual enthalpy changes don't follow Carnot behavior. For a real refrigerator: COP = (h₁ − h₄) / (h₂ − h₁), where Q_L = h₁ − h₄ (evaporator cooling) and W = h₂ − h₁ (compressor work). Dan drew the full P-h diagram showing all four states and explained: mass flow rate cancels in numerator and denominator, so specific enthalpies alone are sufficient. Energy balance: Q_H = Q_L + W, where Q_H = h₂ − h₃. (OH 35)
