What is the dew point of the product gas? So first of all, what is the dew point in general? The dew point is the temperature at which when you cool air or you cool a mixture of gases, the water vapor that's present in that mixture will condense and fall out of the mixture. So it depends on how much water vapor is in the mixture. And we can find that out by analyzing the combustion reaction and figuring out what the mole fraction of water vapor is and then finding the partial pressure of water vapor and then using the steam table to figure out the dew point. But it's a little harder to get started because if we search the reference handbook for combustion reactions of common fuel constituents and we can find the table there and there are some common hydrocarbons with the reactions already pre-written for us. Unfortunately, Hexane is not one of them. So we've got to write the reaction ourselves and attempt to balance it. Unfortunately, it's not too much trouble to do that, so let's dive right in. We've got C6H14 and now we're burning that in stoichiometric air which means there's no excess air. It's just the right amount so that all the oxygen gets used up and all the Hexane is able to burn. So we're just right air which is 0 to plus n to. But the nitrogen always exists in exactly the same proportion which is to say, for every 1 molecule of 0 to, there will be 3.77 molecules of n to. And we just need to allow for that amount of air to scale up or down in order to make sure it's the stoichiometric quantity. So we'll apply an arbitrary constant A out in front of that and we're going to find out what A is along with the mole coefficients for the other constituents in the products coming up now. What are the products? We've got CO2 as usual. We don't know how much so it's going to be arbitrary constant B plus H2O. These are the things we would expect to see when burning a hydrocarbon in air. We're going to have CO2 and H2O. And then the nitrogen is just along for the ride. However much is on the left is also going to appear on the right and we'll calculate what that mole coefficient should be. We'll call it D. So let's start by balancing the carbon and this always goes in the same order. It's going to be carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen. For the hydrogen, that only appears in the hexane on the left as well. There are 14 of them. Now for the oxygen, on the left it only shows up in air. So 2 times A. But on the right it shows up in both CO2 and H2O. So it's going to be 2 times B plus 1 times C for the H2O. And now we know B and C. But we've got to get A by itself. So A equals 2B plus C over 2, which is 2 times 6 plus 7 over 2 is 12 and 7 is 19 over 2 is 9 and a half.
