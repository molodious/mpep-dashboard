Full Solution: A 7-pound cast iron pan, specific heat capacity of 0.1, is heated uniformly to 500 degrees during cooking, then placed in a sink filled with four gallons of 60 degree water. Now, collecting losses, what is the final equilibrium temperature? Back in high school chemistry class, this was called calorimetry. You take some heated metal object, which was heated in a water bath till it was a constant temperature throughout, and then drop it into a water bath of some known temperature usually colder, and then you'd stir it around and you'd cover it with a styrofoam lid to try to eliminate some of the losses, and you'd stir it until it reached some equilibrium temperature, and in doing so, you were able to work backwards to figure out the mass of that object. In this case, we know the mass of the cast iron pan, what we're looking for is to work forward to calculate what that final equilibrium temperature will be, and the way we approach this is to just write down the heat that's lost by the pan, that's 500 degrees when it starts is added to the water, which is 60 degrees when it starts, and at the end of the heat exchange, both the pan and the water are at the same temperature. So we can write MCP delta T for the cast iron pan is equal to MCP delta T for the water, and we know the specific heat capacity of iron that's been given, and we know for water, that's a typical value, and then the delta T is just going to be the starting temperature minus the finishing temperature for the iron because it's cooling off, and then for the water it'll be the finish minus the start because it's heating up, want to make sure that delta T works out to be positive, and then we can find out the mass of water by starting from the volume and just multiplying by the density of water, and doing the necessary unit conversion there, and the mass of the cast iron pan was given already, so that is known. So let's work out the mass of the water. That's going to be the density of water times the volume of water, which is 62.4 pounds per cubic foot times the volume which is 4 gallons, and there are 7.4 eight gallons per cubic foot, so we'll do that unit conversion cancel out cubic feet, cancel out gallons, and we just end up with pounds, which is a mass that's what we want, 33.4 pounds. So that is now known, and now let's make all the substitutions of 7 pounds for the cast iron times the specific heat capacity 0.1 BTU per pound degree Fahrenheit, and then the delta T it's starting at 500 minus the final temperature, which is what we want to know.
