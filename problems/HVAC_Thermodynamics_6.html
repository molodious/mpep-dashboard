HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 6
Problem Statement
Saturated liquid water at 50 psia is cooled to 80°F at constant pressure. What is the change in enthalpy during cooling?
Answer Choices
A.50 Btu/lbm
B.200 Btu/lbm
C.660 Btu/lbm
D.920 Btu/lbm
Solution
✔ Answer: B — 200 Btu/lbm
Quick Overview — How to Approach This Problem:
  1. Identify the initial thermodynamic state: saturated liquid at a known pressure means quality = 0, so the state is fully defined.
  2. Look up the saturation temperature corresponding to 50 psia using the saturated steam table (organized by pressure).
  3. Recognize that cooling at constant pressure takes the water from the saturated liquid line into the subcooled (compressed) liquid region.
  4. Use the relationship Δh = cp · ΔT to calculate the change in specific enthalpy, since liquid water has a nearly constant specific heat capacity.
  5. Plug in cp = 1 Btu/(lbm·°F) for liquid water, and compute the temperature difference from the saturation temperature to 80°F.
  6. Match the result to the closest answer choice.
Full Solution:

We start at State 1: saturated liquid water at 50 psia. "Saturated liquid" means quality x = 0, placing us right on the left edge of the vapor dome. With pressure known and quality defined, the state is fully determined.

Step 1 — Find T1 from the steam table.
Open the Properties of Saturated Water and Steam table organized by pressure (PE Reference Handbook). At 50 psia, the saturation temperature is:

Tsat at 50 psia = 281°F → T1 = 281°F

Step 2 — Define State 2.
The water is cooled to 80°F at constant pressure (still 50 psia). Since 80°F is well below the saturation temperature of 281°F, the water is now subcooled (compressed) liquid. On a T-s diagram, we have moved left along the 50 psia isobar from the saturated liquid line into the subcooled region.

Step 3 — Calculate the change in enthalpy.
For liquid water at moderate pressures, the specific heat capacity is essentially constant at cp = 1 Btu/(lbm·°F). This holds well below approximately 400°F. We use the relationship:

Δh = cp · ΔT

This comes from equating Q = m·cp·ΔT and Q = m·Δh, then dividing both sides by mass m to get Δh = cp·ΔT.

Step 4 — Substitute and solve.

Δh = 1 Btu/(lbm·°F) × (281°F − 80°F) = 201 Btu/lbm

This rounds to approximately 200 Btu/lbm, matching answer choice B.

Note: An alternative approach is to look up hf values at both states directly from the steam table (hf at 50 psia ≈ 250 Btu/lbm, and hf at 80°F ≈ 48 Btu/lbm), giving Δh ≈ 202 Btu/lbm. This works because liquid water is nearly incompressible, so the enthalpy of subcooled liquid at a given temperature is approximately equal to hf at that temperature regardless of pressure. Both methods are valid on the exam.

📹 Video Solution Synthesis:
  • Visualize on the vapor dome: Dan sketches the T-s diagram, placing State 1 on the saturated liquid line and showing the constant-pressure cooling path moving left into the subcooled region. This visual helps you see why State 2 is compressed liquid.
  • Key formula derivation: Start with Q = m·cp·ΔT and Q = m·Δh. Divide both by mass to get Δh = cp·ΔT. Dan emphasizes this derivation so you understand where the formula comes from, not just how to use it.
  • Why cp = 1 works: The specific heat capacity of liquid water is nearly constant at 1 Btu/(lbm·°F) for temperatures below about 400°F. Above that, it increases and the approximation breaks down.
  • Saturated liquid means quality = 0: This is a defined state — knowing pressure plus quality gives you the temperature from the steam table in one lookup.
  • Units drive the answer: Because we are dividing by mass (not given), the result is in Btu/lbm (specific enthalpy), not total Btu. Read the answer choices carefully to confirm units.
⭐ Key Takeaways:
  • When you see "saturated liquid" in a problem, immediately recognize that quality equals zero and the state is fully defined by a single additional property (pressure or temperature). This lets you go straight to the steam table.
  • The relationship Δh = cp·ΔT is one of the most frequently used building blocks in PE exam thermodynamics — know it cold, and know where it comes from.
  • Liquid water is nearly incompressible, which means subcooled liquid properties at a given temperature are approximately equal to the saturated liquid properties at that same temperature. This is a useful shortcut for steam table lookups when the subcooled tables are not available.
  • On the exam, there are often multiple valid paths to the answer. Being comfortable with both the cp·ΔT method and the direct steam table subtraction method gives you flexibility and a way to verify your result.

OH 115 · March 2nd, 2026
Q: The problem says saturated liquid water — quality equals zero. If the problem stated it was a saturated mixture (inside the dome), could we still use pressure alone to find the temperature? Also, where do you find Q = m·Δh, and is it safe to use cp = 1 every time we have liquid water?
Dan's response: Yes — for any quality between 0 and 1 (inside the vapor dome), there is only one temperature for a given pressure. That's the defining feature of the saturation region and why the steam tables are organized both by pressure and by temperature. As for Q = m·Δh, it's such a fundamental building block that you will internalize it through practice — it's one of a small handful of relationships I'd call "must-know." And yes, cp = 1 Btu/(lbm·°F) is safe for liquid water even at elevated pressures because liquid water is essentially incompressible. The specific heat is primarily a function of temperature, and it stays very close to 1 up to several hundred degrees. You can check Engineering Toolbox to see where it starts to deviate, but for exam purposes you're good. (OH #115)

For State 2, you're in the subcooled region, but since liquid water is nearly incompressible, hf at 80°F is a close approximation of the actual enthalpy at 80°F and 50 psia. The small difference between 201 and 202 is just rounding. That said, I recommend knowing both methods. The cp·ΔT approach is slightly more precise and is the better tool when the steam table shortcut doesn't work as cleanly. Know it both ways so you have maximum flexibility. (OH #71)
OH 51 · January 30th, 2023
Q: Can you just look up the enthalpy in the steam table and subtract to get the change in enthalpy?
Dan's response: Yes, you can — and you'll get very close. But technically you're making a simplifying assumption. State 2 is subcooled liquid, not saturated, so you can't look it up directly in the saturated steam table. What you're really doing is assuming that the enthalpy of compressed water at a given temperature is approximately equal to hf at that temperature. This works because water is incompressible — the pressure doesn't meaningfully change the enthalpy. You'll get about 202 vs. the 201 from the cp·ΔT method. I'm fine with you using this shortcut, but I want you to also absorb the Δh = cp·ΔT relationship from the solution video. It's a more general tool. The question of "how much accuracy do I need?" is itself part of the work of studying — playing on that line of precision vs. speed is exactly what the PE exam rewards. (OH #51)
