HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 7
Problem Statement
In a refrigeration cycle, R134A leaves the evaporator at 20 psia with 20°F of superheat. What is the entropy of the refrigerant entering the compressor?
Answer Choices
A.0.03 Btu/(lb·°F)
B.0.22 Btu/(lb·°F)
C.0.24 Btu/(lb·°F) ✓
D.106 Btu/(lb·°F)
Solution
✓ Answer: C — 0.24 Btu/(lb·°F)
Quick Overview: 1. Open the PE Reference Handbook and search "Properties of Refrigerants" to find the Pressure vs. Enthalpy (P-H) diagram for R134A. 2. Draw a horizontal line at 20 psia across the chart until it intersects the right boundary of the saturation curve — this is the saturated vapor condition (State 1 with no superheat). The corresponding saturation temperature is approximately −2.9°F (interpolated from the saturation table). 3. From that intersection, continue horizontally to the right along the constant-pressure line (evaporator pressure does not change) until you reach the 20°F temperature isotherm — this represents 20°F of superheat. This point is State 1, the condition entering the compressor. 4. Identify the lines of constant entropy on the P-H diagram — they run diagonally from lower-left to upper-right (southwest to northeast) and are shown as dotted lines with labeled values along the bottom of the chart. 5. Find which constant-entropy line passes closest to State 1. The state point falls between the s = 0.22 and s = 0.24 lines, but noticeably closer to 0.24. Interpolating gives s ≈ 0.235 Btu/(lb·°F). 6. Round to the nearest answer choice: s ≈ 0.235 Btu/(lb·°F) → Answer C (0.24 Btu/(lb·°F)). Full Solution: This problem requires you to use the Pressure-Enthalpy (P-H) diagram for Refrigerant 134A found in the NCEES PE Reference Handbook. Search "Properties of Refrigerants" or "Pressure Versus Enthalpy" to locate the correct chart. The refrigeration cycle has four process states. State 1 is the condition at the evaporator exit and compressor inlet — exactly what this problem asks about. We are told the evaporator pressure is 20 psia and the refrigerant has 20°F of superheat above the saturation temperature at that pressure. Step 1 — Locate the saturation condition at 20 psia. On the P-H diagram, draw a horizontal line at a pressure of 20 psia (note the pressure axis is logarithmic, so 20 psia is not at the midpoint between any round numbers — be careful). Follow this line to the right until it touches the saturated vapor boundary of the vapor dome. This point represents pure saturated vapor. Using the saturation table for R134A, the saturation temperature at 20 psia can be interpolated as approximately −2.9°F. Step 2 — Account for superheat. The refrigerant has 20°F of superheat, meaning its actual temperature is −2.9 + 20 = +17.1°F. Since pressure remains constant in the evaporator, State 1 lies on the same horizontal line at 20 psia, but to the right of the saturation curve in the superheated vapor region. Follow the line horizontally to the right until it intersects the 20°F temperature isotherm (temperature lines inside the vapor dome are horizontal, but curve downward on the superheated side — they drop steeply once you exit the dome). Step 3 — Read entropy at State 1. Lines of constant entropy on the P-H diagram run diagonally from lower-left to upper-right. Values are printed along the bottom or sides of the chart in units of Btu/(lb·°F). State 1 falls between the s = 0.22 and s = 0.24 lines, but is visibly closer to 0.24. Interpolating between those two lines: s ≈ 0.235 Btu/(lb·°F) This value is closer to answer C (0.24) than to answer B (0.22), making C the correct choice. Common traps: Choice B (0.22) is the entropy at the saturated vapor point, which you would get if you forgot to apply the 20°F superheat. Choice D (106) looks like an enthalpy value in Btu/lb — a common slip if you read the horizontal axis (enthalpy) instead of the diagonal entropy lines. Choice A (0.03) would correspond to a subcooled liquid state. Reference: NCEES PE Reference Handbook — "Properties of Refrigerants: Pressure Versus Enthalpy, Refrigerant 134A" (search term: "134A" or "Pressure Versus Enthalpy").

⭐ Key Takeaways:
• For superheated refrigerant states, the P-H diagram is the only tool available — the saturation tables only cover the saturated region, so you cannot interpolate a superheated entropy from the table alone.
• Superheat shifts State 1 to the right of the vapor dome along a constant-pressure line; forgetting to account for it will land you on the wrong answer choice (B instead of C).
• The pressure axis on P-H diagrams is logarithmic — be precise when locating 20 psia, as linear interpolation will give you the wrong position.
• Always confirm which property you are reading: entropy lines are diagonal on the P-H chart, while enthalpy is the horizontal axis — confusing the two leads directly to distractor answer D.

OH 35 · May 2nd, 2022
Q: Can you go over the pressure vs. enthalpy diagram for refrigerants? What parameters are shown on the chart?
Dan's response: The vertical axis is pressure (in psia, on a logarithmic scale) and the horizontal axis is enthalpy (in Btu/lb). The vapor dome divides the chart into three regions: subcooled liquid on the left, saturated mixture inside the dome, and superheated vapor to the right. Temperature lines run nearly vertical in the subcooled and superheated regions and become horizontal inside the dome. Quality lines (0.1, 0.2, …) run inside the dome and represent the fraction of vapor by mass. Entropy lines are the diagonal dotted lines that slope from lower-left to upper-right. Density lines also appear as curved dotted lines. Because the pressure axis is logarithmic, 150 psia is not the midpoint between 100 and 200 — keep that in mind for any graphical interpolation.
OH 77 · March 18th, 2024
Q: I interpolated from the R134A saturation table to find the saturation temperature at 20 psia (−2.86°F), added 20°F superheat to get 17.54°F, then looked up entropy at that temperature in the table and got 0.22381 Btu/(lb·°F). But that turned out to be wrong. Can you use the table to get the same result, or must you use the chart?
Dan's response: For steam we have both a saturated and a superheated table, so we can analyze states in both regions precisely. For R134A, the handbook only provides the saturation table — there is no superheated table. Once you move to the right of the vapor dome into the superheated region, the table no longer applies and you must use the P-H chart. The entropy value you found from the table at 17.54°F is correct for a saturated condition at that temperature, but State 1 is in the superheated region at 20 psia — those are different states. The only option is a graphical read from the chart, which gives s ≈ 0.235 Btu/(lb·°F), closest to answer C. The interpolation to find the saturation temperature was fine; it's the next step — reading entropy outside the dome — that requires the chart.
OH 92 · November 25th, 2024
Q: Can you show step-by-step how to read the entropy off the R134A P-H chart after locating State 1?
Dan's response: Start by drawing the refrigeration cycle on the P-H diagram: evaporator is the horizontal bottom line (constant pressure), compressor goes diagonally up-right, condenser is the horizontal top line, and the expansion valve drops vertically. State 1 is at the end of the evaporator line, with some superheat taking it to the right of the vapor dome. To find entropy at State 1, locate the diagonal dotted constant-entropy lines on the chart — they are not evenly spaced and their curvature changes across the diagram. Find the entropy line (or pair of lines) that passes closest to your State 1 point. If the point falls between two labeled lines, interpolate between them. Remember that inside the vapor dome the isotherms are horizontal, but outside (in the superheated region) they curve downward. The evaporator line is always horizontal because pressure is constant in the evaporator. Go back and watch the solution video — the graphical steps are covered clearly there.
