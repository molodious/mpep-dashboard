Problem
HVAC & Refrigeration · Fluids · Problem 2
A pump delivers 200 gpm of water at 130 ft of total dynamic head, operating from 7am–7pm Monday through Friday. The pump is 80% efficient and the motor is 93% efficient. What is the annual cost of operation at $0.13 per kWh?
Answer Choices
A $2,670  ✓
B $3,490
C $3,750
D $5,030
Answer: A — $2,670
Quick Overview
  1. Calculate the Water Horsepower (WHP) — the theoretical hydraulic power needed to move the fluid — using the flow rate (gpm) and total dynamic head (ft).
  2. Divide WHP by the pump efficiency (80%) to find the actual shaft power (Brake Horsepower, BHP) the motor must deliver to the pump.
  3. Divide BHP by the motor efficiency (93%) to find the total electrical input power drawn from the power grid.
  4. Convert the electrical power from horsepower to kilowatts (1 hp = 0.7457 kW).
  5. Determine annual operating hours: 12 hrs/day × 5 days/week × 52 weeks/year = 3,120 hrs/year.
  6. Multiply electrical power (kW) × annual hours × energy rate ($/kWh) to get the total annual cost.
📝 Key formula: WHP = (gpm × TDH) / 3,960 — this shortcut works because 1 WHP moves 3,960 gallons per minute through 1 ft of head (derived from the specific weight of water, 62.4 lb/ft³, and unit conversions). Always divide by pump efficiency first, then motor efficiency — they stack multiplicatively because losses occur at each stage.

Full Solution

To find the annual operating cost of a pump system, we must trace power from the fluid itself back through the pump and motor to the electrical grid. Each component has an efficiency that increases the power we must draw at the previous stage.

Given:

Flow rate Q = 200 gpm Total Dynamic Head (TDH) = 130 ft Pump efficiency η_pump = 80% = 0.80 Motor efficiency η_motor = 93% = 0.93 Energy rate = $0.13 per kWh Operating schedule: 7 am–7 pm, Mon–Fri → 12 hrs/day × 5 days/week

Step 1 — Water Horsepower (WHP):

WHP is the theoretical hydraulic power imparted to the fluid. For water (specific weight ≈ 62.4 lb/ft³) the formula simplifies to:

WHP = (Q × TDH) / 3,960 WHP = (200 gpm × 130 ft) / 3,960 WHP = 26,000 / 3,960 WHP = 6.566 hp

Step 2 — Brake Horsepower (BHP) at the pump shaft:

The pump is only 80% efficient, meaning it requires more shaft power than the water actually receives:

BHP = WHP / η_pump BHP = 6.566 hp / 0.80 BHP = 8.207 hp

Step 3 — Motor input power (electrical horsepower):

The motor is 93% efficient, so the electrical power drawn from the grid must be higher still:

Motor input = BHP / η_motor Motor input = 8.207 hp / 0.93 Motor input = 8.825 hp

Step 4 — Convert to kilowatts:

Power (kW) = 8.825 hp × 0.7457 kW/hp Power (kW) = 6.581 kW

Step 5 — Annual operating hours:

Hours/year = 12 hrs/day × 5 days/week × 52 weeks/year Hours/year = 3,120 hrs/year

Step 6 — Annual energy cost:

Annual Cost = Power (kW) × Annual Hours × Rate ($/kWh) Annual Cost = 6.581 kW × 3,120 hrs × $0.13/kWh Annual Cost = 6.581 × 405.60 Annual Cost = $2,669 ≈ $2,670

Answer: A — $2,670.

A useful sanity check: combining pump and motor efficiency gives an overall system efficiency of 0.80 × 0.93 = 0.744 (74.4%). This means the grid must supply 1/0.744 = 1.344× more power than the water actually receives. The relatively low combined efficiency is typical for older, fixed-speed pump systems — modern VFD-driven pumps with premium-efficiency motors can exceed 90% combined efficiency.

📺 Video Synthesis
  • The 3,960 constant in the WHP formula is specific to water — it's derived from water's specific weight (62.4 lb/ft³) and unit conversions (gal → ft³, min → hr). For other fluids, use the general formula: WHP = (Q × H × γ) / 33,000.
  • Efficiency losses stack multiplicatively, not additively — the pump and motor efficiencies multiply together (0.80 × 0.93 = 0.744) to give overall system efficiency. You cannot simply add them.
  • When calculating annual operating hours, always account for the actual schedule: a 12-hour/day, 5-day/week pump runs 3,120 hrs/year, not 8,760 hrs/year (which would be continuous operation).
  • The hp-to-kW conversion (1 hp = 0.7457 kW) is a frequently tested unit conversion. Memorize it — it appears in pump, motor, and refrigeration problems alike.
  • Power flows from grid → motor → pump shaft → fluid. To find grid power, start at the fluid (WHP) and work backward through each efficiency: divide by pump efficiency, then divide by motor efficiency.
  • Annual cost problems combine power calculations with time and rate. Make sure your schedule calculation matches the problem description — weekday-only vs. 24/7 operation makes a dramatic difference in cost.
⚡ Key Takeaways
  • For pump energy problems, always establish the chain of efficiencies: fluid power → pump shaft power → electrical input. Each step divides by the next efficiency value.
  • The WHP shortcut formula (gpm × TDH ÷ 3,960) is only valid for water. For other liquids or when the problem gives specific gravity, use the full formula with specific weight.
  • Always read the operating schedule carefully — the pump in this problem runs 12 hrs/day on weekdays only, which gives 3,120 hrs/year, not the full 8,760 hrs of continuous operation.
  • On the exam, if your answer doesn't match a choice, check whether you divided by each efficiency separately or combined them first — either approach is valid, but careless multiplication of efficiencies is a common error trap.
