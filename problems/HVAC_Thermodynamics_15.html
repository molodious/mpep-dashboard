Problem 15 — Carnot Heat Pump with R-22
Thermodynamics · Carnot Cycle · COP · Refrigerant Properties
In a Carnot heat pump, R-22 evaporates at 31 psia and condenses at 211 psia. What is the coefficient of performance?
A. 0.9
B. 4.3
C. 5.1 ✓
D. 6.8
Answer: C — 5.1
1. Identify the formula: COP (heat pump) = T_H / (T_H − T_L) This requires ABSOLUTE temperatures (Rankine for imperial units). 2. Look up R-22 saturation temperatures from refrigerant tables: • At 31 psia → T_sat ≈ 5°F (evaporator = cold side, T_L) • At 211 psia → T_sat ≈ 116°F (condenser = hot side, T_H) 3. Convert to Rankine (add 460): T_L = 5 + 460 = 465 °R T_H = 116 + 460 = 576 °R 4. Compute COP: COP = 576 / (576 − 465) = 576 / 111 ≈ 5.19 5. Answer: C — 5.1
This problem is identical in structure to the basic Carnot heat pump COP problem — but instead of giving you temperatures directly, it gives you pressures and expects you to look up the corresponding saturation temperatures from R-22 refrigerant tables. STEP 1 — Identify the operating temperatures from the R-22 tables In a vapor-compression (or Carnot) cycle: • The refrigerant evaporates at the low-pressure side → T_L • The refrigerant condenses at the high-pressure side → T_H From standard R-22 saturation property tables: • P = 31 psia → T_sat ≈ 5°F (low side, evaporator) • P = 211 psia → T_sat ≈ 116°F (high side, condenser) STEP 2 — Convert to absolute temperature (Rankine) COP formulas involve temperature ratios, which are only valid on an absolute scale. T_L = 5°F + 460 = 465 °R T_H = 116°F + 460 = 576 °R STEP 3 — Apply the Carnot heat pump COP formula COP_HP = T_H / (T_H − T_L) = 576 / (576 − 465) = 576 / 111 ≈ 5.19 Closest answer: C — 5.1 ✓ — — — Why the wrong answers are wrong — — — A (0.9): This is less than 1, which is impossible for a heat pump COP (COP_HP ≥ 1 always, since a heat pump delivers both the work input AND the heat absorbed from the cold source). Likely results from inverting the formula or using temperature differences only. B (4.3): This is the result you get if you use approximate or incorrect R-22 table values — for example, reading T_L ≈ −10°F and T_H ≈ 120°F and converting to Rankine: 580/(580−450) = 580/130 ≈ 4.46. The table lookup is the key step here; a careful reading yields ~5°F and ~116°F, not −10°F and 120°F. D (6.8): This could result from applying the Carnot refrigerator COP formula (T_L / (T_H − T_L)) instead of the heat pump formula, or from using pressure ratios rather than temperature ratios. COP_refrigerator = T_L/(T_H−T_L) = 465/111 ≈ 4.2 — still not 6.8 — suggesting a wrong table value or formula confusion. — — — Key concept: Heat pump vs. refrigerator COP — — — Both operate on the same Carnot cycle. The only difference is what you're counting: • Refrigerator: you care about the heat REMOVED from the cold space → COP_R = T_L / (T_H − T_L) • Heat pump: you care about the heat DELIVERED to the hot space → COP_HP = T_H / (T_H − T_L) Notice: COP_HP = COP_R + 1. Always. So if you compute the refrigerator COP first, just add 1 to get the heat pump COP. This is a useful cross-check on the exam.

🎓 Related Thermodynamics Concepts from the Course

This problem has not been covered in office hours yet — but it connects directly to several themes Dan has emphasized throughout the course.

On absolute temperatures: Dan has repeatedly stressed that any Carnot formula involving a temperature ratio (not just a difference) requires absolute temperatures. Problem 14 covered this exact trap: using Fahrenheit gives the wrong COP because the numerator is a raw temperature, not a difference. The same logic applies here — always convert to Rankine before plugging into COP = T_H/(T_H−T_L).

On refrigerant table lookups: This problem adds one layer — you must look up the saturation temperature from a pressure. Dan covers refrigerant property tables in the refrigeration section, and this is a standard PE exam move: give pressures, expect you to find temperatures. On the exam you'll have the ASHRAE reference tables or the NCEES-provided R-22 data — take the time to read them carefully.

On heat pump vs. refrigerator COP: Remember COP_HP = COP_R + 1. If you find yourself getting an answer around 4.1–4.2, you may have applied the refrigerator formula. A heat pump COP is always ≥ 1, and typically much higher than 1 for reasonable temperature lifts.
Key Takeaways
  • When pressures are given instead of temperatures, the first step is always a refrigerant table lookup — find the saturation temperature at each pressure before doing any math.
  • Use absolute temperature (Rankine) in all Carnot COP formulas. The Rankine conversion is simple: add 460 to °F. Never plug in raw Fahrenheit values.
  • For a heat pump: COP_HP = T_H / (T_H − T_L). For a refrigerator at the same conditions: COP_R = T_L / (T_H − T_L). The relationship COP_HP = COP_R + 1 is always true — use it as a cross-check.
  • R-22 (Freon 22) is a common refrigerant in PE exam problems. Key landmarks: at ~5°F, saturation pressure ≈ 31 psia; at ~116°F, saturation pressure ≈ 211 psia. Know how to read the tables, not just these values.
