HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 15
Problem Statement
In a Carnot heat pump, R-22 evaporates at 31 psia and condenses at 211 psia. What is the coefficient of performance?
Answer Choices
A 0.9
B 4.3
C 5.1 ✓
D 6.8
Solution
Answer: C
Quick Overview: 1. Identify that this is a Carnot heat pump operating with refrigerant R-22, and that the given information is evaporator and condenser pressures. 2. Look up the R-22 refrigerant tables or pressure-enthalpy chart in the reference handbook to find the saturation temperatures at each pressure. 3. Convert both temperatures from Fahrenheit to absolute (Rankine) by adding 460. 4. Apply the Carnot heat pump COP formula using the absolute high and low temperatures. 5. Divide the high-side absolute temperature by the difference between the high and low temperatures to get the COP. Full Solution: Lookup the Pressure Versus Enthalpy Curves for Refrigerant 22 and read from the chart the temperatures for the evaporator and condenser based on the pressures given. These correspond to the low and high temperature reservoirs, respectively, for the heat pump: T_L = T_evaporator@P=31psia ≈ −10°F + 460 = 450°R, T_H = T_condenser@P=211psia ≈ 100°F + 460 = 560°R. Look up the Carnot Cycle in the reference handbook and find the coefficient of performance formula: COP_HP,carnot = T_H / (T_H − T_L) = 560°R / (560°R − 450°R) = 5.1. Answer C.
Video Synthesis
• A common trap is trying to apply the COP formula directly using pressures instead of temperatures — you must first convert pressures to saturation temperatures using the R-22 refrigerant data. • Students sometimes search "R22" in the reference handbook and miss the section; searching "refrigerant 22" (spelled out) navigates directly to the correct tables and curves. • The pressure-enthalpy chart can be hard to read precisely at pressures that fall between labeled grid lines (like 31 psia between 20 and 40); the saturation tables often give a cleaner read. • A critical unit trap: temperatures must be in absolute Rankine (°F + 460) before plugging into the Carnot COP formula — using °F directly will give a wrong answer. • The denominator of the COP formula is the temperature difference T_H − T_L; watch out for sign errors when T_L is negative in Fahrenheit (100 − (−10) = 110, not 90). • Both the pressure-enthalpy curve and the saturation tables are valid lookup methods — use whichever gives a cleaner reading for the given pressure values.
Key Takeaways
• For a Carnot heat pump, you need saturation temperatures at each pressure — not the pressures themselves — to calculate COP. • Refrigerant saturation temperatures can be found from either the pressure-enthalpy diagram or the saturation tables in the reference handbook. • All temperatures in Carnot cycle calculations must be expressed in absolute units (Rankine in the US customary system). • The Carnot heat pump COP is always greater than 1, since it equals T_H divided by the temperature difference, not the difference alone. • Careful reading of refrigerant charts at intermediate pressures is a practical skill — the saturation tables provide a more precise alternative when chart interpolation is uncertain.

Session 106 — July 7th, 2025
Q: Are there scenarios where it's appropriate to assume that the enthalpy of a compressed liquid equals HF?
A: The H ≈ HF assumption really only holds true when the liquid is right at the saturation boundary (quality = 0); as the liquid becomes further subcooled below its boiling point, the assumption increasingly breaks down and the more accurate approach is to use ΔH = Cp·ΔT.
Session 106 — July 7th, 2025
Q: What makes water a convenient fluid when working with compressed liquid enthalpy?
A: Water has a specific heat capacity of 1 BTU/lb·°F, so the reduction in enthalpy below the saturation point numerically equals the number of degrees the liquid is below its boiling point.
Session 88 — September 17th, 2024
Q: Is there any reason why atmospheric pressure is not included in the calculation for stagnation pressure?
A: Atmospheric pressure acts equally on both sides of the system, so it cancels out; stagnation pressure is therefore based only on gauge static pressure plus velocity pressure, not absolute pressure.
Session 88 — September 17th, 2024
Q: What is stagnation pressure conceptually equivalent to?
A: Stagnation pressure is the same as Pitot pressure — it is the sum of the static pressure and the velocity (dynamic) pressure, representing the total pressure at a point where the flow is brought to rest.
