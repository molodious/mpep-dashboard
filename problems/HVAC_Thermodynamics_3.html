HVAC & Refrigeration · Thermodynamics · Problem 3
Problem Statement
Water at 400 psi and 700°F is in what thermodynamic state?
Answer Choices
A.Saturated liquid
B.Saturated vapor
C.Superheated vapor ✓
D.Two-phase mixture
Solution
Answer: C
1. Identify the given conditions: water at P = 400 psi and T = 700°F. 2. Look up the saturation temperature (T_sat) at 400 psi in the steam tables. From the saturated steam table (pressure entry): T_sat at 400 psi ≈ 444.59°F. 3. Compare the actual temperature to the saturation temperature: T_actual = 700°F vs. T_sat = 444.59°F. 4. Since T_actual (700°F) is significantly greater than T_sat (444.59°F), the water exists above the saturation curve at this pressure. 5. Apply the state determination rule: if T > T_sat at a given pressure, the substance is a superheated vapor. 6. Select Answer C: Superheated vapor.
This problem tests one of the most fundamental skills in thermodynamics: determining the phase (thermodynamic state) of a substance given its pressure and temperature. Given: • Pressure: P = 400 psi • Temperature: T = 700°F Approach — Compare T to T_sat at the given pressure: The key principle is that every pressure has a unique saturation temperature. At this saturation temperature, liquid and vapor coexist in equilibrium. The state of the substance is determined by comparing the actual temperature to this saturation temperature. Step 1: Look up T_sat at 400 psi Open the NCEES PE Reference Handbook to the saturated steam tables (pressure-based entry). At P = 400 psia: T_sat ≈ 444.59°F This means that at 400 psi, water boils (transitions between liquid and vapor) at 444.59°F. Step 2: Compare T_actual to T_sat T_actual = 700°F T_sat = 444.59°F T_actual − T_sat = 700 − 444.59 = 255.41°F of superheat Since T_actual (700°F) is well above T_sat (444.59°F), the water has already fully vaporized and continued to heat beyond the boiling point. It is superheated vapor. Step 3: Rule out other answer choices • Saturated liquid (A): Would require T = T_sat with quality x = 0. Not the case here — T is far above T_sat. • Saturated vapor (B): Would require T = T_sat with quality x = 1. Again, T exceeds T_sat. • Two-phase mixture (D): Exists only when T = T_sat (the substance is in the process of boiling). Since T ≠ T_sat, this is ruled out. • Superheated vapor (C): T > T_sat at the given pressure. ✓ Answer: C — Superheated vapor The water at 400 psi and 700°F is approximately 255°F above its saturation temperature, placing it firmly in the superheated vapor region. You could also verify this by looking up properties in the superheated steam table at 400 psi and confirming that 700°F appears as a valid entry.

OH 94
Q: Can you also approach this by looking in the superheated steam table first to see if 400 psi / 700°F has an entry, instead of going through the saturation table?
Dan: You absolutely can. If you go to the superheated steam table and find an entry at 400 psi and 700°F, that confirms the state is superheated vapor — the table wouldn't have an entry for it otherwise. But I'd recommend getting comfortable with the saturation table comparison approach too, because that's the more general method. It works for any state determination, not just superheated. If you're given conditions that land in the two-phase region, the superheated table won't help you — you'd need to recognize that T = T_sat and then work with quality. So the saturation comparison is the universal tool.
OH 87
Q: How do I know whether to use the pressure-based or temperature-based saturation table for this kind of problem?
Dan: Great question. It depends on what you're given and what you need to look up. In this problem, you're given both P and T, and you need to find T_sat — so you enter the saturation table by pressure and read off the corresponding temperature. If you were given T and needed P_sat, you'd enter by temperature. They contain the same data, just organized differently. On the exam, whichever entry is more convenient — use that one. For this problem, since 400 psi is a round number, the pressure-based table is the natural choice.
🔄 Common Mistakes & Clarifications
OH 94
Q: What if the temperature was exactly equal to the saturation temperature — how would you determine if it's saturated liquid, saturated vapor, or a mixture?
Dan: If T equals T_sat at that pressure, all you know is that you're somewhere in the saturation dome — you could be saturated liquid, saturated vapor, or a two-phase mixture. You'd need additional information to pin down exactly where. Typically the problem would give you quality (x), or a specific volume, or enthalpy — something that lets you locate your position within the dome. With just P and T both on the saturation line, the state is indeterminate without that extra piece of information. That's a subtlety worth remembering for the exam.
OH 87
Q: I keep mixing up subcooled liquid and superheated vapor — is there a quick way to remember which is which?
Dan: Think of it this way: "superheated" means the vapor has extra heat beyond what's needed to boil — the temperature is above the boiling point. "Subcooled" (or compressed) liquid means the liquid is below the boiling point — it hasn't reached the temperature needed to start boiling. So if T > T_sat, you've gone past boiling → superheated vapor. If T < T_sat, you haven't reached boiling yet → subcooled liquid. The names literally describe the relationship to the saturation temperature.
