OH 35: HVAC: Thermo-18
Q: Why do we need to pull enthalpies and solve for COP in this problem instead of just using temperatures like in previous problems?
A: COP is fundamentally 'desired output over required input'—it's not a simple percentage-based efficiency. The distinction matters because you need to account for the actual energy transfers (enthalpies) in the system, not just temperature differences, to correctly calculate what you're actually getting out versus what you're putting in.
OH 44: HVAC: Thermo-18
Q: I'm off by one on H2 from the R123 chart (getting 118 instead of 117), which changes my calculated COP from 5.99 to 6.7 and makes me pick the wrong answer choice. What should I do if this happens on the actual exam?
A: Being off by one in the denominator (H2-H1, the compressor energy) can swing the COP by ~10% since the denominator is relatively small. Do your best to be precise using the line tool, understand this sensitivity exists so it doesn't rattle you, and recognize that on exam day the answer choices usually won't be this close—this exposure helps build your engineering judgment for unexpected situations.
OH 70: HVAC: Thermo Module #18
Q: Why does problem 18 add 20 degrees of superheat compared to problem 7, and why is finding H2 so critical that it changes the entire answer?
A: Both problems apply superheat to locate state 1 on the T-s diagram beyond the saturation curve—it's a subtle but non-negligible difference. The COP calculation is sensitive because it has a small denominator (H2 - H1), so small errors in H2 significantly impact the final answer, making precision essential.
