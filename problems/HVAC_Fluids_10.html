Problem Statement
Problem 10 — Minimum Supply Pressure for a Hotel Cold Water System

A hotel with 400 units requires a maximum cold water supply of 5 gpm per unit with a diversity factor of 60%. The supply line is 5,000 ft long and made of 8-inch standard weight steel with a roughness coefficient of C = 130. The minimum required pressure for all fixtures in the hotel is 80 psig. The start of the supply line is 100 ft above the delivery end at the hotel.

What is the minimum pressure required at the start of the line?
Answer Choices
A. 93 psig
B. 108 psig
C. 128 psig
D. 180 psig
✓ Answer: A — 93 psig

Apply the energy equation from start to delivery. The 100 ft elevation advantage reduces the required upstream pressure, while friction losses over 5,000 ft add to it. Net result: approximately 93 psig. See the Full Solution panel for the complete worked solution.

1 · Quick Overview

High-level steps to find the minimum required pressure at the start of the supply line:

  1. Calculate total design flow rate. Apply the diversity factor: 400 units × 5 gpm/unit × 0.60 = 1,200 gpm. This is the flow the pipe must carry at peak demand.
  2. Find friction head loss using Hazen-Williams. The C = 130 roughness coefficient is a direct signal to use the Hazen-Williams equation. Look up or calculate the friction loss per 100 ft for 1,200 gpm in 8-inch steel pipe, then scale to 5,000 ft.
  3. Calculate total friction pressure loss. Multiply the friction loss rate (ft/100 ft) by the pipe length (5,000 ft) and convert from feet of head to psi using the factor: 1 psi = 2.31 ft of water.
  4. Account for the elevation difference. The supply start is 100 ft above the delivery end. This elevation difference provides a pressure gain — fluid flowing downhill gains pressure. Convert 100 ft to psi: 100 ft ÷ 2.31 ft/psi ≈ 43.3 psi pressure gain.
  5. Apply the energy equation. P_start = P_end + friction losses − elevation gain. The required delivery pressure is 80 psig.
  6. Solve and select Answer A — 93 psig. P_start ≈ 80 + friction loss (psi) − 43.3 psi ≈ 93 psig.

💡 Key insight: The 100 ft elevation advantage significantly reduces the required upstream pressure. Without it, you would need to add 43.3 psi to the answer. Always sketch the system first to get the elevation sign right — being uphill helps you, being downhill hurts you.

2 · Full Solution

This is a pipe flow problem using the energy equation (Bernoulli with losses) applied between the start and end of a long supply line. The C = 130 roughness coefficient is given, which is the Hazen-Williams C value — a direct hint to use the Hazen-Williams friction loss equation.

Step 1 — Calculate Design Flow Rate

Apply the diversity factor to find the actual peak simultaneous demand:

Q = (Number of units) × (max flow per unit) × (diversity factor) Q = 400 units × 5 gpm/unit × 0.60 Q = 1,200 gpm

Step 2 — Hazen-Williams Friction Loss

The Hazen-Williams equation gives the friction head loss per unit length of pipe. The standard form for US customary units:

h_f / L = 10.44 × Q^1.852 / (C^1.852 × d^4.8655) Where: h_f = friction head loss (ft) L = pipe length (ft) Q = flow rate (gpm) C = Hazen-Williams roughness coefficient = 130 d = pipe inside diameter (inches) For 8-inch standard weight steel pipe: Inside diameter ≈ 7.981 inches (use 8 in for exam purposes) Plugging in Q = 1,200 gpm, C = 130, d = 8 in: Q^1.852 = 1200^1.852 ≈ 589,000 (approx) C^1.852 = 130^1.852 ≈ 9,430 (approx) d^4.8655 = 8^4.8655 ≈ 25,100 (approx) h_f / L = 10.44 × 589,000 / (9,430 × 25,100) h_f / L = 6,149,160 / 236,673,000 h_f / L ≈ 0.026 ft/ft → 2.6 ft per 100 ft of pipe

Step 3 — Total Friction Head Loss Over 5,000 ft

h_f = (h_f / L) × L h_f = 0.026 ft/ft × 5,000 ft h_f ≈ 130 ft of water head

Step 4 — Convert Friction Loss to PSI

Use the standard water conversion: 2.31 ft of water = 1 psi

ΔP_friction = h_f / 2.31 ΔP_friction = 130 ft / 2.31 ft/psi ΔP_friction ≈ 56.3 psi

Step 5 — Convert Elevation Difference to PSI

The supply start is 100 ft above the delivery end. Flowing downhill means the fluid gains pressure — this is a pressure gain, not a loss. The elevation head converts to:

ΔP_elevation = Δz / 2.31 ΔP_elevation = 100 ft / 2.31 ft/psi ΔP_elevation ≈ 43.3 psi (pressure GAIN — start is higher)

Step 6 — Apply the Energy Equation

The energy equation from start (1) to delivery end (2), in pressure units:

P₁ + ρgz₁ = P₂ + ρgz₂ + ΔP_friction Rearranging for P₁ (pressure required at the start): P₁ = P₂ + ΔP_friction − (elevation gain) P₁ = P₂ + ΔP_friction − ΔP_elevation P₁ = 80 psig + 56.3 psi − 43.3 psi P₁ = 80 + 13.0 P₁ ≈ 93 psig → Answer A

Result

The minimum pressure required at the start of the supply line is approximately 93 psig → Answer A.

Note: The exact Hazen-Williams calculation involves interpolation and rounding consistent with PE exam practice. Using steel pipe friction tables for 8-inch pipe at 1,200 gpm will give a consistent result within engineering tolerance. All three methods — Hazen-Williams, Darcy-Weisbach, and steel pipe friction tables — should agree within 5–15%.

Why the Other Choices Are Wrong

  • B (108 psig): This likely results from incorrectly ignoring the elevation advantage, or applying the elevation gain as a loss instead of a gain. Adding ~15 psi to the correct answer could come from a sign error on the elevation term.
  • C (128 psig): This suggests neglecting the elevation difference entirely and treating the problem as a horizontal pipe, adding friction loss directly to the required delivery pressure without subtracting the elevation gain.
  • D (180 psig): This is likely from using the undiversified flow rate (400 × 5 = 2,000 gpm) without applying the 60% diversity factor, significantly overstating friction losses. Always apply diversity when given.

💡 Exam Tip: When C is given in the problem statement, that is the Hazen-Williams C coefficient — use it. The "roughness coefficient" label and C notation are a dead giveaway. Note that steel pipe friction tables also use a roughness parameter but call it something different — they are not the same C. When in doubt, use the Hazen-Williams equation with the given C value.

3 · Video Synthesis
📹 Key Concepts — Dan's Solution Approach
  • Diversity factor dramatically reduces the design flow. Without diversity, you'd design for 400 × 5 = 2,000 gpm. The 60% diversity factor cuts this to 1,200 gpm — a 40% reduction. This matters enormously because friction loss scales with Q to the 1.85 power in Hazen-Williams. Diversity factors prevent massive oversizing of building water systems.
  • The C = 130 roughness coefficient is a direct hint to use Hazen-Williams. On the PE exam, if they hand you a C value, use the Hazen-Williams equation. That's the only common friction loss method that uses C. Without a given C value, default to the Darcy equation or steel pipe friction tables.
  • Elevation difference provides a pressure advantage when the supply is higher than the delivery point. Flowing downhill is thermodynamically favorable — gravity does work on the fluid, adding pressure. Here, 100 ft of elevation converts to about 43 psi of free pressure gain. Always sketch the system to get the sign right.
  • Always convert head (feet) to pressure (psi) using 2.31 ft/psi for water. This conversion factor comes from the specific weight of water (62.4 lb/ft³) and unit analysis. Memorize it: 1 psi = 2.31 ft of water, or equivalently 1 ft of water = 0.433 psi.
  • Watch units carefully — gpm, feet, and psi all need to be consistent. The Hazen-Williams equation produces head loss in feet. The energy equation is often written in psi. You need to convert at some point; do it explicitly and don't lose track of which quantities are in which units.
4 · Key Takeaways
⭐ Key Takeaways
  • When a roughness coefficient C is given in the problem, it's a signal to use the Hazen-Williams equation. This is the only standard friction loss method that uses C as an input. On the PE exam, this is a deliberate clue — don't ignore it.
  • Diversity factors are critical in building water supply sizing — they prevent oversizing. Real buildings never have all fixtures running simultaneously at full flow. Diversity factors account for this reality and reduce design flow to a realistic simultaneous demand.
  • Elevation differences can work for or against you; always sketch the system to get the sign right. If the supply is higher than the delivery, elevation helps you (pressure gain). If the delivery is higher, elevation hurts you (pressure loss). A quick sketch eliminates sign errors.
  • The steel pipe friction tables, Darcy equation, and Hazen-Williams should all give answers within engineering tolerance. If your results from different methods diverge by more than 15%, revisit your inputs. Small absolute discrepancies that look large as a percentage are often acceptable — you're doubling a small number, not making a fundamental error.
5 · Office Hours Q&A
Q: Why does pressure divided by gamma equal pressure? I keep getting stuck on the units in every Bernoulli problem. OH 55
A: In US customary units, specific weight (gamma) and density have the same magnitude but different units — density is lbm/ft³, specific weight is lbf/ft³. The g/gc cancellation makes them numerically equal, which is why you can treat them interchangeably in magnitude even though they're dimensionally different quantities. There are two forms of Bernoulli's equation: one with all pressure units (psi or lb/ft²), and one with all length units (feet of head). The head form — dividing everything by gamma — is used 80–90% of the time in HVAC and pipe flow problems. The key conversion to memorize: 2.31 feet of water = 1 PSI. Use this to convert between head and pressure at the end of problems. Once you're comfortable with this conversion, Bernoulli problems become much more manageable.
Q: Are we able to use the Hazen-Williams equation for this problem? I produced different results. OH 53
A: Yes, Hazen-Williams is valid for this problem — and in fact it's the preferred method here because a C value is given in the problem statement. PE exam questions tend to favor the Darcy equation or steel pipe friction tables in general, but the dead giveaway to use Hazen-Williams is when they hand you a C value. Without a C value, you can't make a reliable assumption. The Darcy equation, steel pipe friction tables, and Hazen-Williams should all align within 5–15% engineering tolerance for the same problem. One important note: the surface roughness factor C used in some steel pipe tables is NOT the same as the Hazen-Williams C coefficient — they unfortunately share the same variable letter. Don't confuse the two. The Hazen-Williams C is a flow coefficient (higher = smoother, more capacity), while the pipe table roughness factor is a different physical quantity.
Q: I calculated head loss from the Darcy equation to compare with the roughness coefficient method and got remarkably different results (more than double). Should they have been closer? Is the Darcy equation the assumed method unless explicitly given the roughness coefficient? OH 15
A: They should reconcile within engineering tolerance. The discrepancy seems large as a percentage but may be small in absolute terms — you're doubling a small number, and a small absolute difference can look huge as a percentage. Use the steel pipe friction tables if you have water flowing in steel pipe — it's faster and easier than either method. The Darcy equation is always valid as a fundamental approach and doesn't require a given C value. They should agree within reasonable engineering error, and you won't get a problem wrong on the exam by choosing either method — as long as you apply it correctly. If you're seeing a factor-of-two discrepancy, double-check your diameter (inside vs. outside), your units for Q, and your friction factor lookup from the Moody chart.
